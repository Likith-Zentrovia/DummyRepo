Description of the Disorders

Key characteristics of personality disorders (PDs) are maladaptive patterns of thoughts, feelings, perceptions, and/or behaviors that are broad, enduring, and dysfunctional. Personality patterns of PDs are wide-ranging and apply across nearly all contexts of a person’s life. These personality patterns have onsets that can be traced back to at least adolescence or early adulthood. Finally, PDs are characterized by dysfunctional personality patterns that are inflexible and extreme variants of personality characteristics found in the general population.

Estimates from community samples indicate that between 9 and 14% of adults in the USA would meet diagnostic criteria for at least one specific PD (Eaton & Greene, 2018). However, some have argued these estimates may be underestimated as individuals with PDs are not always aware of the impact their behavior has on other people and may be unable to identify the presence of their own maladaptive traits (Huprich et al., 2011). Prevalence rates of PDs are generally higher in high-income countries compared with low- and middle-income countries (Winsper et al., 2020). This may be attributable to variations in behavioral norms across countries (e.g., individualistic versus collectivist societies) or cultural differences that impact the validity of diagnostic traits and categories.

Categorical Model of Personality Disorder

In DSM-5-TR (American Psychiatric Association, 2013), there are two sections that discuss personality disorders; Section II contains the established, categorical model and Section III includes the Alternative Model of Personality Disorders (AMPD), a dimensional model. There are ten PDs defined in Section II of DSM-5-TR, which are broken up into three loosely organized clusters. Cluster A consists of the “odd or eccentric” disorders and includes paranoid, schizoid, and schizotypal PDs. Cluster B is characterized as the “dramatic, emotional, or erratic” disorders and includes antisocial, borderline, histrionic, and narcissistic PDs. Avoidant, dependent, and obsessive-compulsive PDs belong to Cluster C, which is categorized as anxious or fearful. Global rates of cluster A, B, and C personality disorders are 3.8%, 2.8%, and 5.0%, respectively (Winsper et al., 2020). Key features of each PD are described in Table 16.1. In addition to meeting the criteria for a specific PD, an individual must also meet all the general PD criteria, which differentiate PDs from other psychiatric conditions. PDs are notable in that they are defined by enduring patterns of behavior or inner experience that are inflexible and pervasive across situations. These patterns must affect two or more of the following: cognition, affectivity, interpersonal functioning, or impulse control. These patterns must also be stable and have an onset in early adulthood or adolescence. Finally, these patterns of behavior or inner experience must cause significant distress or significant functional impairment.

Categorical approaches to the classification of PDs have provided many benefits, including ease of communication and descriptions among clinicians and aiding the development of treatments for PDs. However, current categorical classification systems have serious limitations and threats to validity due to the high comorbidity between PDs and high heterogeneity within PDs. Individuals can be diagnosed with multiple PDs which provides evidence that PDs as defined in categorical models may be due to underlying maladaptive personality facets rather than distinct syndromes (Widiger et al., 2009). Multiple PD diagnoses are also very stigmatizing. Additionally, due to the way diagnostic criteria currently exist in DSM-5-TR Section II, two individuals can be diagnosed with the same PD and share few to no symptoms in common.

Dimensional Models of Personality Disorder

Alternative Model of Personality Disorder

The AMPD contained in Section III of DSM-5-TR is built on the premise that PDs are not categorical but rather dimensional constructs, as supported by decades of research. Dimensional views of PDs are connected both to scientific evidence and utility (Bender, 2013; Tyrer et al., 2015). The AMPD requires both impairments in personality functioning (Criterion A) and a manifestation of pathological personality traits (Criterion B) in order to diagnose personality disorder. Criterion A is operationalized by the Levels of Personality Functioning Scale (LPFS) which assesses four dimensions of impaired personality functioning to differentiate five levels of impairment, ranging from little or no impairment (0) to extreme impairment (4). The LPFS results in a single score that covers the four areas of core pathology in all PDs. Two dimensions are in the area of self-functioning (identity and self-direction) and two are in the area of interpersonal functioning (empathy and intimacy). To meet the requirements of AMPD Criterion A, a moderate or greater level of impairment (level of 2 or higher) in personality functioning is necessary to diagnose a PD. Personality functioning appears to be a better predictor for psychosocial functioning than DSM-IV PDs (Buer Christensen et al., 2020; Morey et al., 2013) and is a better predictor for dropout in psychotherapy for PDs than the DSM-IV categories (Busmann et al., 2019).

Criterion B requires the presence of pathological personality traits. These pathological personality traits are organized into five broad trait domains, which are further broken down into 25 trait facets. The five trait domains in the AMPD can be understood as maladaptive extremes of the five-factor model of personality (FFM), with each of the five AMPD domains having been shown to map onto the FFM domains (see Table 16.2). Of note, the relationship between psychoticism and openness is less clear compared to the other four facets, with inconsistent findings across several studies (Al-Dajani et al., 2016).

The maladaptive traits of the AMPD can be measured using the Personality Inventory for DSM-5-TR (PID-5) self-report or informant versions. One limitation of the AMPD is that it does not specify how to determine clinical significance of traits or facets. DSM-5-TR merely states “if a trait is elevated—that is, formal psychometric testing and/or interview data support the clinical judgment of elevation—then it is considered as contributing to meeting Criterion B” (American Psychiatric Association, 2013). It has been suggested that clinicians use established PID-5 norms to compute standard scores and define “high scores” with a conventional cut-off (e.g., 65 or 70 T) with either PID-5 self-report or informant forms.

While the AMPD uses a primarily dimensional approach, it allows for the classification of six specific PDs: antisocial, avoidant, borderline, narcissistic, obsessive-compulsive, and schizotypal. To give the specification of each personality disorder, a number of maladaptive traits specific to each disorder are required. The traits characterizing these six PDs in the AMPD are detailed in Table 16.1. Beyond the six specific PD classifications defined in the AMPD, the diagnosis of personality disorder-trait specified (PD-TS) may be given when a PD is considered present but the criteria for a specific disorder are not met. This category allows for diagnoses based on specific impairments in personality functioning and enables clinicians to clearly describe what maladaptive personality traits make up the individual’s PD, an aspirational improvement from previously given PD-Not Otherwise Specified diagnoses in the DSM categorical system.

International Classification of Diseases, 11th Edition

The International Classification of Diseases, 11th edition (ICD-11) has moved away from the categorical classification of PDs to a dimensional classification system somewhat similar to DSM-5-TR AMPD. Most notably, the ICD-11 merged all distinct PDs into one diagnostic category: personality disorder, with the ability to code for the severity of the PD. The ICD-11 system consists of two required steps to make a PD diagnosis and two additional steps to provide further description of the individual’s personality disturbance. The first step consists of assessment whether the individual’s difficulties meet the general requirements for a PD diagnosis. Similar to criterion A of the AMPD, disturbances in both self and interpersonal functioning must be both present and enduring (defined as lasting a minimum of two years) across a range of personal and social situations and not limited to single contexts. ICD-11 defines self-dysfunction as persistent difficulties in maintaining a stable sense of identity, a pervasive sense of impoverished or highly over-valued self-worth, inaccuracies in self-perception, or challenges in self-direction and decision-making. Interpersonal dysfunction is defined as persistent difficulties in making and sustaining close relationships or in the ability to understand other people’s perspectives. Features of both self- and interpersonal dysfunction manifest in maladaptive patterns of cognition, emotional experience and expression, and behavior. Additionally, the individual’s developmental context must be considered (e.g., a certain degree of unstable identity is normative among adolescents).

The second necessary step needed to make a PD diagnosis in ICD-11 is an assessment of the severity of the difficulties experienced. Areas examined are the degree and pervasiveness of disturbance in the person’s relationships and their sense of self, the intensity and breadth of the emotional, cognitive, and behavioral manifestations of the person’s disturbance, the extent to which these patterns and problems cause distress or psychosocial impairment, and the level of risk of harm to self and others. These difficulties are then holistically rated as mild, moderate, or severe difficulties.

The third step, which is not required to make a PD diagnosis in ICD-11, involves further assessment of the person’s personality trait domains to more comprehensively describe an individual’s personality disturbance. ICD-11 describes five trait domain specifiers that are continuous with normal personality characteristics, consistent with the FFM: negative affectivity, detachment, dissociality, disinhibition, and anankastia. Negative affectivity, detachment, and disinhibition are largely the same as described in the AMPD, whereas dissociality aligns with disinhibition as described in the AMPD. People high in this trait may demonstrate a sense of entitlement, expecting others to admire them and wish to be at the center of other people’s attention, dramatically expressing their dissatisfaction if others do not respond in the way they desire. Dissociality may lead to a disregard of the importance of others and the person may have a relentless focus on their own needs, desires, and comfort. Anankastia is described as a very clear and detailed personal sense of perfection and imperfection that extends beyond the typical standards of an individual’s community. Individuals high in this trait may believe strongly that everyone should follow all rules exactly and meet all obligations.

A final optional step allows clinicians to apply a borderline pattern specifier, if appropriate. The diagnostic features are essentially the same as the DSM-5-TR BPD symptoms, including the nine categorical symptoms of borderline PD as well as (1) a view of the self as inadequate, bad, guilty, disgusting, and contemptible; (2) an experience of the self as profoundly different and isolated from other people; a painful sense of alienation and pervasive loneliness; and (3) proneness to rejection hypersensitivity; problems in establishing and maintaining consistent and appropriate levels of trust in interpersonal relationships; frequent misinterpretation of social signals. Unlike the trait specifiers, the borderline pattern specifier requires at least five of the nine core features of borderline PD from DSM-5-TR. This specifier was added to ICD-11 as concern was expressed by the clinical and academic community about continued access to well-established evidence-based treatments.

A final distinctive feature of the ICD-11 is the special case of diagnosis of a PD in adolescence. In line with an overall shift to a lifespan approach in ICD-11, clinicians can make a diagnosis of a PD, as appropriate, a change from ICD-10 which stated that it is “unlikely that the diagnosis of personality disorder will be appropriate before the age of 16 or 17 years.” This allows the opportunity for early intervention for adolescents whose behaviors may meet the requirements for a diagnosis and yet because of their young age, these behaviors may be less engrained and more open to change. However, the diagnosis of a PD in adolescence still merits caution as other diagnostic descriptions or developments and environmental contexts may better account for exhibited behaviors.

Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology

A more recent approach to classification of psychopathology, including PDs, is the Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiTOP) framework. HiTOP conceptualizes psychopathology in terms of dimensions rather than categories to avoid problems of arbitrary boundaries between disorder and normality as well as difficulties with unclear boundaries between disorders, frequent disorder co-occurrence, heterogeneity within disorders, and diagnostic instability (Kotov et al., 2017). HiTOP makes use of multilevel classification and therefore avoids subthreshold and “not otherwise specified” diagnoses as everyone can be characterized on a set of dimensions, even individuals with low levels of pathology. The HiTOP model starts with a general factor of psychopathology (p-factor) at the superspectra level and then breaks psychopathology down into six spectra (somatoform, internalizing, thought disorder, disinhibited externalizing, antagonistic externalizing, and detachment). These spectra (with the exception of somatoform, a currently provisional aspect of the framework) have demonstrated alignment with dimensions of general personality (i.e., FFM) and personality pathology trait models (i.e., AMPD or ICD-11; Widiger et al., 2019). The HiTOP spectra are further specified into subfactors (currently including sexual problems, eating pathology, fear, distress, mania, substance abuse, and antisocial behavior). Below subfactors are dimensional syndromes (e.g., DSM diagnoses) with homogeneous symptom components and maladaptive traits below (e.g., anxiousness, emotional lability, hostility, identity problems, etc.). This is, in fact, a strength of the HiTOP model, as the dimensional and hierarchical model reduces diagnostic heterogeneity by regrouping related symptoms together and assigning unrelated symptoms to different components. PDs exist across the HiTOP model rather than in one cluster. For example, paranoid and schizoid PDs as defined by DSM-5-TR fall under the Thought Disorders and Detachment spectra, while borderline PD falls under Antagonistic Externalizing and Internalizing Distress subfactors. This hierarchical classification reduces extensive overlap between diagnoses and problematic within-diagnosis heterogeneity that pervades the DSM diagnostic system.

Procedures for Gathering Information

Many factors are important to consider during the information-gathering phase of making a diagnosis of personality disorder. First and foremost, building rapport is essential. Rapport may be easily developed in the first session with some clients while others may need many sessions before rapport can be established. This is especially true for individuals with PDs. Individuals with PDs, depending on their diagnosis, may be particularly hesitant (avoidant PD) or suspicious (paranoid or schizotypal PDs) of new relationships or may be particularly vulnerable to feeling misunderstood or not heard (borderline PD). Rapport also can be easily damaged by not attending to client concerns. Focusing too closely on a strict protocol of questions in a structured interview without care for building a relationship with the client may damage rapport if the client believes that the clinician’s agenda is more important than their feelings and experience. Empathetic and validating responses to the client’s feelings, experiences, and behaviors are essential to maintain necessary rapport. Rapport also may be damaged if the pacing of questions in a protocol does not match the client’s comfort level. Therefore, clinicians must allow sufficient time for trust between clinician and client to develop. Additionally, the level of rapport is ever-changing, and therefore rapport must be assessed consistently, across and within sessions. When a rupture in rapport occurs, this should be addressed as a top priority.

When assessing PDs, it is essential to take a full history covering the individual’s whole life paying particular attention to developmental history and early adversity and trauma as these are key etiologic factors for PDs. As noted above, PDs are broad, enduring, and dysfunctional; therefore, it is important to look for patterns that date back to late adolescence or early adulthood (if not earlier as in the case of antisocial PD). Additionally, in order to make a PD diagnosis it is essential to ensure that the maladaptive cognitions, feelings, and behaviors are consistent across contexts and not limited to certain situations or environments.

There are multiple methods of assessing PDs including clinical interviews (unstructured or semi-structured), self-reports, and informant reports. Ultimately, each method has strengths and weaknesses and the best diagnostic assessment will make use of multiple methods. Self-report measures offer many benefits and can be complimentary when used as a way of first identifying which PDs should be emphasized during the interview and which disorders could be ignored with minimal risk (Widiger & Samuel, 2005). This way of narrowing down what PDs should be the focus of the interview can help with time efficiency as well as with maintaining rapport as clients may become frustrated if asked many questions in a semi-structured interview that are not relevant to them. An additional benefit is that self-report inventories can alert the clinician to PDs that might have otherwise been missed as a result of false expectations, bias, or assumptions (e.g., failing to notice histrionic PD traits in male patients). Self-reports pose a risk of possible false-negatives due to denial or inadequate insight on the part of the client. However, many self-report inventories include validity scales that can signal the presence of response biases and distortions (i.e., malingering or inaccurate reporting of symptoms) that might invalidate the diagnostic assessment. Although brief screening questionnaires can be used to determine what PDs to focus on when conducting a more in-depth interview, these do not provide a comprehensive assessment and typically lack validity scales.

Clinical interviews allow clinicians to ensure diagnostic criteria are fully met. For example, a clinician in an interview can ensure a client who endorses on a self-report “many mood shifts per day” truly meets the BPD criterion for unstable affect due to emotional hypersensitivity rather than merely experiencing normative affective experiences throughout the day. Relying on unstructured interviews can cause clinicians to fail to consider all of the necessary criteria necessary to make a diagnosis or lead to clinicians falling prey to confirmation bias. For these reasons, semi-structured interviews are the preferred method of assessment. Semi-structured interviews ensure and provide documentation that each PD diagnostic criterion has been systematically, objectively, and comprehensively assessed. This also aids in the likelihood that assessments will be consistent across interviewers.

Informant-report inventories also provide useful information in the assessment of PDs given individuals with PDs may lack insight into their own dysfunctional personality patterns. Agreement between self-descriptions and peer-descriptions of personality traits has generally been good to excellent when sampling within nonclinical populations (McCrae et al., 1998), although agreement between self-descriptions and informant descriptions of PDs within clinical samples has at times been criticized due to substantial variability across studies in agreement between self- and informant-reports (Miller & Lynam, 2015). When selecting informants with the client to complete informant-reports, it is necessary to consider the relation of the informant to the client, how long they have known the client, and in what contexts. As PDs are pervasive, consistent across contexts, and begin in young adulthood typically, it is important the informant has known the individual at the appropriate age (i.e., not exclusively in childhood) in multiple contexts, if possible.

DSM-Section II Personality Disorders

As noted above, categorical personality disorders are commonly assessed via clinical interviews and self-reports. The Structured Clinical Interview for DSM-5 Personality Disorders (SCID-5-PD) is a widely used clinical interview, directly assessing each DSM-IV and DSM-5 Section II Personality Disorder symptoms. The SCID-5-PD consists of two components: a self-report and a clinical interview. First, clients endorse items that directly represent DSM Criteria A Personality Disorders. Subsequently, the clinician conducts a clinical semi-structured interview based on the client’s symptom endorsements to determine if the diagnostic criteria have been met. The SCID-IV-PD and SCID-5-PD serve as effective instruments for evaluating symptoms of personality disorders and can provide supporting evidence for a personality disorder diagnosis.

Other clinical interviews such as the International Personality Disorder Examination (IPDE; Loranger, 1999) focus on six broad functioning domains relevant to personality disorder assessment: work (i.e., employment functioning), self (i.e., stability of self-image), interpersonal relationships (i.e., interpersonal functioning), affect (i.e., emotional or affective experience), reality testing (i.e., personal beliefs), and impulse control (i.e., impulsivity). The IPDE questionnaire and interview reflect collaborative efforts between the World Health Organization ICD-10 and the National Institute of Health with the American Psychiatric Association’s DSM-IV by corresponding with both nosologies. The Structured Interview of Personality Organization—Revised (STIPO-R; Clarkin et al., 2016) is a brief psychodynamic measure that dimensionally assesses personality functioning or personality organizations along five domains. The five domains describe identity (i.e., the experiences of a consistent self-image), object relations (i.e., interpersonal relationships), defenses (i.e., defensiveness strategies), aggression (i.e., aggression directed at the self or others), and moral values (i.e., capacity for guilt and practicing norms in interpersonal relationships). The domains are rated along two broad personality organizational dimensions: Normal, Neurotic (levels 1 or 2), and borderline (low, middle, or high). Finally, the Personality Disorder Inventory for DSM-IV (PDI-IV; Widiger & Samuel, 2005) assesses the ten DSM-IV personality disorders. The PDI-IV is further organized along several thematic content areas, such as attitudes toward self, attitudes toward others, comfort with others, friendships, and relationships, conflict, work and leisure functioning, consideration of social norms, mood, and appearance and perception.

Some clinical interview instruments target specific categorical personality disorders such as the Revised Diagnostic Interview for Borderlines (DIB-R; Zanarini et al., 1989). The DIB-R is a semi-structured clinical interview specifically designed to assess borderline personality disorder across four areas: affect, cognition, interpersonal relationships, and impulse action patterns (Zanarini et al., 1989). While not primarily used for a clinical diagnosis, the DIB-R is useful for evaluating specific features of borderline personality disorder and examining their longitudinal stability over time (Tragesser et al., 2010).

There are a variety of self-report measures of personality disorder. For example, the Personality Diagnostic Questionnaire 4+ (PDQ-4+; Hyler, 1994) is a commonly used screening instrument to directly identify the presence or absence of the ten DSM personality disorders. The Personality Assessment Inventory (PAI; Morey, 2004) includes the scales borderline and antisocial features but does not provide comprehensive coverage of personality pathology. Other personality disorder self-report measures assess DSM PD criteria with adaptive and maladaptive personality traits along with assessments of impairment; however, these are separate from the AMPD. For example, the Schedule for Nonadaptive and Adaptive Personality (SNAP; Clark, 1993) is a self and informant report of adaptive personality traits or characteristics related to personality pathology, which includes 15 primary traits across three higher-ordered domains: Negative Temperament, Positive Temperament, and Disinhibition. Trait profiles have been shown to capture DSM-5-TR personality disorders including avoidant, borderline, obsessive-compulsive, and schizotypal (Wilt et al., 2010). Next, the OMNI Personality Inventory (OMNI; Loranger, 2001) is a self-report assessment of adaptive and maladaptive personality traits but subscales can be organized to identify the 10 DSM-IV personality disorders and five personality disorder scales (e.g., agreeableness, conscientiousness, extraversion, narcissism, neuroticism, openness, and sensation seeking). The abbreviated version, the OMNI Personality Disorder Inventory (OMNI-IV; Loranger, 2001) only assesses for DSM-IV personality disorders. The Millon Clinical Multiaxial Inventory (MCMI-IV) is a self-report assessment that evaluates adaptive and maladaptive personality features and psychopathology. It operates under the theoretical framework that personality disorders are evolutionarily based adaptations or maladaptations (Millon et al., 2015). The MCMI-IV includes 15 personality-related subscales to measure clinical personality disorder features and ten psychopathology subscales across three general patterns: typical functioning, abnormal traits, and disordered.

Finally, the Multi-source Assessment of Personality Pathology (MAPP; Oltmanns & Turkheimer, 2006) formerly known as the Peer Nomination Inventory is a self and informant report measure of DSM-IV personality features and personality traits. Informant reports are a key component of this assessment as individuals with a personality disorder may experience impairment in describing themselves or may avoid endorsing behaviors or personality traits that are less socially desirable (Oltmanns & Turkheimer, 2006).

DSM-Section III Alternative Model of Personality Disorders

The Alternative Model of Personality Disorders (AMPD) includes instruments to evaluate maladaptive personality traits (Criteria B) and personality functioning (Criteria A). For example, the SCID-5-AMPD (Bender et al., 2018; Skodol et al., 2018) is a clinical interview designed to evaluate Criteria A and B of the AMPD, along with the six personality disorders retained from DSM-5 Section II Personality Disorders (i.e., antisocial, borderline, narcissistic, schizotypal, avoidant, and obsessive-compulsive PDs). The SCID-5-AMPD can be useful in assessing maladaptive personality traits and severity with a dimensional lens that bypasses the pitfalls of the categorical model.

The AMPD also can be assessed through self-report measures. For example, the Levels of Personality Functioning Scale (LPFS; Morey et al., 2013) measures Criteria A of the AMPD which is the degree of impairment in interpersonal (intimacy and empathy) and intrapersonal (identity, self-direction) domains. Additionally, the Personality Inventory for DSM-5 (PID-5; Krueger et al., 2012) is a widely used self-report of the AMPD maladaptive personality trait domains (i.e., negative affectivity, detachment, psychoticism, antagonism, and disinhibition), closely related to the Five Factor Model broad domains. Both self-report instruments of the AMPD can be used concurrently to screen for PD pathology.

Five-Factor Model of Personality Disorders

The Five-Factor Model (FFM) is a widely accepted framework for understanding general personality traits, defining five broad domains and 30 facets along a bipolar dimension. Researchers advocating for a dimensional structure of personality disorders recognize that extreme representations of adaptive personality traits, either high or low, may be understood as maladaptive personality trait variants (Widiger et al., 2012). Studies have demonstrated that the FFM can capture symptoms outlined in both the DSM-5 and DSM-IV Personality Disorders. This recognition led to the development of the Five-Factor Model of Personality Disorders (FFMPD), primarily assessed via self-report measures. FFMPD scales aim to assess DSM-5 Section II Personality disorders through the lens of the Five-Factor Model. Several FFMPD scales have been developed for personality disorders (Bagby & Widiger, 2018) with the exception of paranoid personality disorder. The FFMPD scales can be useful in clinical contexts for assessing maladaptive personality traits relevant to each of the DSM-5-TR Section II Personality Disorders.

Comprehensive Assessment of Traits Relevant to Personality Disorder Model

The Computerized Adaptive Test of Personality Disorder (CAT-PD; Simms et al., 2011), also known as the Comprehensive Assessment of Traits Relevant to Personality Disorders Model (Ringwald et al., 2023), represents an early initiative toward adopting a dimensional model of personality disorders. The CAT-PD instrument aims to comprehensively assess personality traits relevant to personality pathology from a large pool of test items. The CAT-PD model conceptualizes five personality domains: Negative Emotionality, Positive Emotionality, Antagonism, Disconstraint, and Oddity. The presentation of items is dependent upon the client’s responses to items that measure traits relevant to personality pathology. Efficiency stands out as the primary strength of CAT-PD. Additionally, the CAT-PD offers a static test form, serving as a standard self-report measure.

Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology

Relevant to personality pathology, HiTOP conceptualizes Spectra (e.g., Internalizing, Thought Disorder, Disinhibited Externalizing, Antagonistic Externalizing, Detachment, and Somatoform) related to maladaptive personality trait variants, and psychopathologies included in the DSM-5-TR. The HiTOP Digital Assessment and Tracker (HiTOP-DAT) is a freely available battery of self-report instruments that assess HiTOP-consistent constructs (https://​www.​hitop-system.​org/​clinical-tools), which can be used freely, and includes a manual and online training materials. This collection of instruments can be useful in identifying maladaptive personality traits as they relate to personality disorders and other psychiatric disorders, through a dimensional lens. The HiTOP-DAT was developed to be an interim step in HiTOP assessment to fill the gap until HiTOP-specific measures could be developed. A preliminary HiTOP-Self Report is now available for research use (https://​www.​3plab.​org/​hitop).

International Classification of Diseases-11 Personality Disorders

The International Classification of Diseases-11 (ICD-11; World Health Organization, 2019) Personality Disorder model proposes a dimensional model, departing from the previous ICD categorical approach. Comparatively, this is one step ahead of the DSM model, since the AMPD was placed in Section III Emerging Measures and Models. Nevertheless, the AMPD and ICD-11 personality disorders are closely aligned, sharing several components such as a focus on general dysfunction and maladaptive personality traits. Unlike the DSM-AMPD, no structured clinical interviews assess the ICD-11 PD (Pan & Wang, 2024) but several self-report forms have been developed.

The ICD-11 PD model measures general severity across self and interpersonal functioning domains, measured by the Standardized Assessment of Severity of Personality Disorder (SASPD; Olajide et al., 2018). Additionally, the Personality Inventory for ICD-11 (PiCD; Oltmanns & Widiger, 2018) measures maladaptive personality traits across five domains—negative affectivity, detachment, dissocial, disinhibition, and anankastic. Similar to the AMPD, there are several self-report measures to assess maladaptive personality traits and functioning, including the ICD-11 Personality Disorder Severity Scale (PDS-ICD-11; Bach & Mulder, 2022), a brief measure of personality disorder severity, or the Personality Inventory for DSM-5 Brief Form Plus (PID5BF+; Bach & Mulder, 2022), which assesses maladaptive personality traits from both the AMPD and ICD-11 models.

Case Illustration

Jim is a 34-year-old mixed-race bisexual male who was referred to therapy by his employer due to interpersonal conflicts and difficulties managing his emotions, including outbursts during team meetings and with customers. He reports feeling misunderstood and unappreciated by his colleagues, which leads to strained relationships and decreased productivity. Upon initial evaluation, Jim exhibits a number of pathological personality symptoms. Broadly, he shows impairment in cognition (his perception of other people), affect (intensity and lability), interpersonal functioning, and impulse control. These concerns cause him distress as well as social and occupational impairment. Through a semi-structured interview, Jim endorsed four symptoms of borderline personality disorder and two symptoms each of paranoid, narcissistic, avoidant, and dependent personality disorders (see Table 16.4). These additional characteristics provide a more comprehensive understanding of Jim’s personality pathology, highlighting the complex interplay of maladaptive traits. Jim’s presentation aligns well with the AMPD with impaired self and interpersonal dysfunction, pervasive and intense negative emotions (including anger, anxiety, and shame); difficulties with trust, rejection, and abandonment; impulsivity and lack of self-control; and hostile attitudes toward others, leading to conflicts and strained relationships.

Impact of Sex/Gender, Culture, Race, Age, and Other Aspects of Diversity

This section emphasizes the importance of incorporating identity as a contextual factor when considering personality disorder diagnoses. Specifically, this section will present evidence on the impact of several identities, including age, culture, race and ethnicity, sex, gender and gender expression, and sexual orientation on the manifestation of personality disorder symptoms or maladaptive traits and functioning. This section concludes by highlighting the importance of awareness of bias that can result in wrongly pathologizing identities.

Age

Age is a contextual factor impacting PD diagnosis due to the heterogeneous presentation of symptoms in younger and older populations as a result of generational differences, cognitive changes, and physical changes. DSM states that the age of onset for PDs is during adolescence or early adulthood (American Psychiatric Association, 2013). Antisocial personality disorder (ASPD) is the only PD that overtly requires the occurrence of symptoms in adolescence. This diagnostic criterion may make it more difficult to diagnose the disorder in older adults due to cognitive decline that may prevent some older individuals from providing a detailed account of behavioral patterns in their adolescence or young adulthood that will meet ASPD diagnostic criteria (Holzer & Vaughn, 2017) and may contribute to the difference in diagnostic prevalence in younger and older populations. Individuals with ASPD may engage in more risky behaviors, leading to incarceration or death, contributing to further differences in diagnostic prevalence throughout the lifespan (Holzer & Vaughn, 2017). Finally, existing DSM diagnostic criteria for ASPD include engaging in behaviors that require substantial energy including physically aggressive behaviors toward others and criminal behaviors. When conducting diagnostic assessments for ASPD, it is important to consider the heterogeneous presentation of aggressive behavior that does not only include physical assaults or criminal behaviors that warrant arrests because physical strength and engagement in criminal behaviors have been shown to decrease with age (Holzer & Vaughn, 2017). Other expressions of aggression can include non-violent behaviors such as verbal aggression, purposefully damaging others’ personal properties, intentionally spreading rumors about others, or harassing them indirectly through other means.

More recently, some researchers have questioned whether PDs can emerge in older adults. Because the age of onset for DSM-5-TR personality disorders is during adolescence/early adulthood, the onset of personality disorders in older adults is not well-researched. Stone et al. (2023) argue traumatic experiences are strongly associated with personality dysfunction or psychological diagnosis in older adults. In a study that examined the late onset of personality disorders in individuals older than 55, the researchers found the occurrence of a personal illness and illness or death of a relative predicted the onset of personality disorders (Dupree et al., 2023). Dupree et al. (2023) attributed the occurrence of late onset of personality disorders to a change in the individuals’ roles in life from being “healthy” to “unhealthy” or independent to dependent. This could be an important consideration when conducting diagnostic interviewing of personality disorders in older populations. More generally, due to the unique context of later life, researchers have proposed “geriatric variants” for all of the DSM-based PDs (Segal et al., 2006), which could be used to help clinicians and researchers identify (and study) unique presentations of PDs in later life.

Culture

When making a clinical diagnosis of a PD, it is important to consider cultural differences and how these might impact the presentation of symptoms. The consideration of cultural practices is important in working with individuals from diverse backgrounds because the clinician must consider whether symptom expressions are typical of cultural expressions or a deviance from it. Cultural context can provide a more comprehensive understanding of an individual’s symptoms especially as it relates to PD diagnosis because it concerns how a person’s culture is impacting how they think, express how they feel, and react to certain events. Additionally, one’s cultural expression of feelings and emotions is especially important because some translated versions of Western instruments, especially diagnostic interviews, may not convey the same meaning. For example, some have argued that diagnostic criteria, such as fear of abandonment, are not appropriate in collectivist cultures. Some cultural expressions that may be considered pathological may be common practice in one’s culture, and this could lead to misdiagnosis. For example, in some Indigenous communities, an appropriate expression of grief includes hearing the voices of dead ancestors (Balaratnasingam & Janca, 2017).

In a study that examined the heterogeneous cultural representations of self-harming behaviors and the psychological stressors of borderline personality disorder (BPD), individuals from Eastern cultures (e.g., East Asia, East India, Morocco, Hong Kong, India, and Japan) were more likely to engage in self-harming methods such as self-poisoning and overdose (Munson et al., 2022). This may be due to discouragement of externally expressing one’s emotions in Eastern nations. Additionally, these methods may be used because poisons and chemicals are more commonly accessible in the regions where the studies were conducted. In Western nations (e.g., Canada, Croatia, Italy, Norway, Sweden, the United States, and Western Australia), it was found that skin mutilation was more common. This may be because individuals from Western nations are more emotionally expressive (Munson et al., 2022). It is not possible or expected of any clinician to be knowledgeable of all people’s cultural backgrounds. However, one’s culture should be considered in diagnostic interviewing of personality disorders to gather more information about the context in which the symptoms may occur. If the clinician is not well-educated on the individual’s cultural background, it is encouraged that the clinician does the necessary research into the individual’s cultural practices and expressions. It is also important for clinicians to not assume that a patient fully identifies within a specific cultural background, thus clinicians should consider assessing how each patient identifies with a cultural background. Finally, one’s culture may be informative of the differential stressors such as political and social climates that may impact the onset of PDs (Munson et al., 2022).

Race and Ethnicity

When working with individuals from diverse, underrepresented, and epistemically excluded populations, clinicians should be aware of personal biases. These biases can impact the type of diagnosis a patient may receive. When conceptualizing a diagnostic assessment, clinicians should be aware of the possible biases that may contribute to their decision of a diagnosis. For example, racial stereotypes associated with certain groups should be of note to prevent bias in diagnostic interviewing and conceptualization. Previous research suggested PDs were more frequently diagnosed in racial and ethnic minoritized groups compared to their White counterparts (e.g., Grant et al., 2008; Huang et al., 2006; Tomko et al., 2014).

However, more recent research has found that some racial and ethnic groups may have lower prevalence rates of personality disorders than White populations. In a meta-analysis that examined how race and ethnicity impacted the prevalence of PDs, individuals who identified as Black had a lower rate of diagnosis of a personality disorder than individuals who identified as White (McGilloway et al., 2010). When comparing rates of PD diagnoses of individuals who identified as Asian and White, there were no statistically significant differences. Although there were also no statistically significant differences in individuals who identified as Hispanic and White in their PD diagnoses, Hispanic populations were found to be more likely to be diagnosed with a PD. The authors concluded that while the significantly lower diagnoses of PDs in Black populations could be a true lower prevalence rate, this also could be due to potential neglect of assessing PD diagnoses in ethnic groups (McGilloway et al., 2010). In another more recent study that examined PDs across racial groups from hospital admissions data across 6 years, they found that Black and other minority ethnic groups had lower prevalence rates of PDs than the White British population (Hossain et al., 2018). Whether racially and ethnically minority groups are over or underrepresented in PD diagnosis, it is important to be aware of potential diagnostic biases against racial and ethnic groups. It is important for the assessing clinician to be aware of their own biases and beliefs about stereotypes they may have toward certain groups.

Sex and Gender Identity and Gender Expression

Another important identity aspect to consider in diagnostic interviews of PDs is sex and gender differences. An individual’s gender identity may impact the diagnosis they receive due to potential biases that some clinicians have toward gender identities. Alternatively, a person’s sex and gender identity can impact how symptoms present due to the association of certain behaviors with different gender identities. For example, a cisgender woman with borderline personality disorder (BPD) may display more overt mood fluctuation, fear of abandonment, and interpersonal difficulties due to societal associations and expectations of femininity while a cisgender man with BPD may feel pressured to suppress their emotional oscillations and express it through overtly aggressive behaviors or risk-taking behaviors to align more with societal expectations of masculinity. Research shows differential behavioral correlates in men and women diagnosed with BPD (Sher et al., 2019) where men with BPD scored higher on aggression and impulsivity measures than women. Similarly, the manifestation of symptoms of antisocial personality disorder (ASPD) differs by the binary expression of gender (Sher et al., 2015). In individuals diagnosed with ASPD, women were found to have committed less antisocial acts than men. Additionally, boys later diagnosed with ASPD may have an earlier onset of conduct disorder and are more likely to engage in aggressive acts than adolescent girls. Dimensional models of PD may decrease gender biases. For example, when a case vignette was described in terms of Five-Factor Model components, there were no sex differences in the ratings as compared to the DSM ratings (Samuel & Widiger, 2009).

Sexual Orientation

Another factor in diagnostic interviewing of PDs that can be impacted by bias is sexual orientation. In a study that examined bias in the diagnosis of BPD in sexual minority individuals, compared with their heterosexual counterparts, sexual minority individuals were more likely to receive a diagnosis of BPD (Rodriguez-Seijas et al., 2021). The authors noted that individuals in this sample who were diagnosed with BPD also had other comorbid psychopathologies and maladaptive personalities. However, even after controlling for these clinical correlates, the diagnostic disparity between sexual minority individuals and heterosexual individuals in BPD still persisted, providing evidence for diagnostic criteria bias against sexual minority individuals. Rodriguez-Seijas et al. (2021) also investigated diagnostic disparity in diagnostic instruments in this group. Results were stratified based on sexual orientation subgroups including heterosexual, lesbian/gay, and bisexual individuals. The disparity in diagnostic methods among sexual orientation subgroups was greatest among bisexual individuals. Rodriguez-Seijas et al. (2021) suggested future research focus on investigating potential reasons that may have led to this disparity including diagnostic instrument differences or clinician’s unfamiliarity with how personality pathology symptoms manifest in non-heterosexual populations.

Information Critical to Making a Diagnosis

When conducting an assessment for personality disorder, whether using a categorical or dimensional model, certain key information must be clarified prior to reaching a diagnostic decision. This section highlights the importance of assessing each presenting symptom in addition to gathering important contextual information during assessments in attempts to comprehensively understand a patient and their concerns. First, as mentioned in previous sections, personality assessments can include multiple sources of information. This is a key component of a psychological evaluation. For example, evaluating clinicians should gather as much information as possible about a patient’s presenting concern from clinical interviews, self-reports, informant reports, objective testing, and behavioral observations. Although including all may not be possible, clinicians should aim to make diagnostic decisions informed by multiple sources and instruments.

When assessing PDs or any psychopathology there are several key factors clinicians must assess. First, clinicians should assess the onset, course, severity, and duration of each symptom. Next, assessing functioning or impairment is particularly important when formulating a psychological assessment. In addition to functioning, culturally relevant factors or personal identity are also key in clinical decision-making. This is because a comparative reference group may not accurately represent minoritized identities or cultures, potentially over-pathologizing or ignoring the impact of institutionalized or systemic harms and how these impact a client’s functioning. Thus, the heterogeneous presentation of any psychopathology should be carefully considered. Furthermore, family medical and psychiatric history is also important to know as personality disorders exhibit heritability ranges between 35% and 60% (Reichborn-Kjennerud, 2010). The developmental history of the client is important in informing the onset of the symptoms of a PD, such as traumatic or adverse events (Swales, 2022). Finally, psychosocial factors may help identify events that led to the onset of symptoms.

In addition to assessing symptoms, functioning, and culturally relevant behaviors, psychosocial factors such as physical health should be assessed. For example, a client’s physical health can be especially critical to making a diagnosis. The client’s physical health such as medical conditions and medication use can help rule out or explain some symptoms or provide information regarding comorbid conditions to consider. Additionally, the client’s lifestyle factors such as substance use can also be informative to rule out factors that are impacting the client’s symptoms.

Finally, consulting with the existing diagnostic criteria of PDs is key when assessing for a personality disorder. If using categorical diagnostic criteria, it is important to consider the heterogeneous presentation of symptoms and the ways that these symptoms can be expressed. The context in which the symptoms are present is also helpful to know when making a diagnosis because it can help establish a current pattern of when, where, and how the symptoms occur. Current contexts may include major life events, life stressors, recent life changes, or work or school environment. If using dimensional models for diagnosis, these contexts are also important to consider, but the severity of symptom impact may also be especially considered here. In order to apply diagnostic criteria appropriately, knowing the different presentation of the diagnostic criteria is helpful in forming a diagnosis.

Dos and Don’ts

PDs remain one of the most highly stigmatized types of psychiatric disorders, including among mental healthcare providers (Sheehan et al., 2016). Many providers are reluctant to make a diagnosis of a PD, so we include a table of Dos and Don’ts for providing these diagnoses (see Table 16.5).

Dos and Don’ts

Dos

Don’ts

Obtain a thorough symptom picture and assessment of psychological, interpersonal, and vocational functioning in order to make a differential diagnosis, especially in patients who present with substance abuse, affective disorder, anxiety, and/or interpersonal or work-related problems

Fail to consider how symptoms may impact style of reporting; individuals with PDs may have a grandiose self-image (i.e., narcissistic PD), an overly dramatic manner of speaking (i.e., histrionic PD), or a wary suspiciousness which may not be readily disclosed (i.e., paranoid PD) or the individual may lack insight/forthrightness

Summary

Personality disorders impact the way an individual thinks, feels, and behaves and are currently classified into ten distinct categories. However, the field largely is moving toward a dimensional classification of PDs, through the use of the AMPD, ICD-11, and/or HiTOP. Personality pathology can be assessed using a number of semi-structured interviews or self-report questionnaires. The shift toward dimensional models of personality pathology is accompanied by the ongoing development of assessment tools aimed at capturing dimensional personality. Moving forward, the focus for dimensional models may be on demonstrating the clinical utility of these instruments. When making a personality disorder diagnosis it is critical to gather from multiple sources of information, assess the onset, course, duration, and severity of each symptom, as well as the context in which the symptoms present, the cultural context in which the symptoms occur, and the client’s personal and family medical history. The impact of identity such as sexual orientation, sex and gender expression, race and ethnicity, cultural background, and age also should be carefully considered.
