Description of the Disorders

The group of Somatic Symptom and Related Disorders (SSRD) is a heterogeneous group of different mental disorders. This diagnostic group has undergone significant modifications over the past decade. The Somatoform Disorders in the fourth edition of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-IV) (American Psychiatric Association, 2000) were replaced by the renamed and reconceptualized group of SSRD in the fifth revision of the DSM (DSM-5) (American Psychiatric Association, 2013). Somatic Symptom Disorder (SSD) replaced the previous diagnostic entities of Somatization Disorder, Undifferentiated Somatoform Disorders, and Pain Disorder in the DSM-IV. Functional Neurological Symptom Disorder (FNSD) represents the equivalent to the former Conversion Disorder, as does Illness Anxiety Disorder (IAD) to the former Hypochondriasis. Psychological Factors Affecting Other Medical Conditions (PFAOMC) and Factitious Disorder (FD) were not included in the former group of Somatoform Disorders in the DSM-IV but in other chapters. By introducing the SSRD, the requirement of distinguishing between medically unexplained and explained physical symptoms was abolished and the focus was shifted to symptom-related psychological distress. In this sense, the DSM-5 makes an impactful step in counteracting body-mind dualism by including psychological features as classification criteria and conceptualizing somatic symptoms in terms of a biopsychosocial model. In the following, the features of each SSRD are described in detail. Note these disorders were not revised in the text revision of the DSM-5, the DSM-5-TR (American Psychiatric Association, 2022).

Somatic Symptom Disorder (SSD)

SSD is characterized by the occurrence of one or more somatic symptom(s) that are distressing and result in significant disruption of daily life. The somatic symptom has to be accompanied by at least one of the following psychological features: disproportionate and persistent thoughts about the seriousness of one’s symptom, persistently high level of anxiety about health or symptoms, or excessive time and energy devoted to these symptoms or health concerns. Symptoms have to be present for more than 6 months. To specify symptom persistence, dominance of pain symptoms, and level of symptom severity, the DSM-5-TR provides different specifiers. SSD covers a heterogeneous group of individuals suffering from

  • Persistent distressing somatic symptoms of a monosymptomatic or polysymptomatic nature that are either not better explained or not sufficiently better explained by another medical condition.

  • Persistent somatic symptoms that are caused by a known biomedical condition (e.g., fear of recurrence in successfully treated individuals with cancer who have side effects from the medical treatment).

  • Persistent pain that is more or less medically explained.

  • Illness anxieties that occur in addition to persistent distressing somatic symptoms.

Individuals with common functional somatic syndromes (FSS), defined as “well-recognized clusters of bodily symptoms that are common in medical practice and cause considerable disability” (Henningsen et al., 2018; p. 12), often present with psychological features that meet the criteria of an SSD. The three most common and best investigated FSS are Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) (Lacy et al., 2016), Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS) or Systemic Exertion Intolerance Disease (SEID) (Committee on the Diagnostic Criteria for Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome, 2015), and Fibromyalgia (FMS) (World Health Organization, 2018).

Illness Anxiety Disorder (IAD)

The central feature of IAD is intense fear about the possibility of having or developing a serious illness. The DSM-5-TR criteria of IAD (American Psychiatric Association, 2022) emphasize the absence of somatic symptoms. The focus is on worrying and typical illness behaviors (e.g., frequent visits to physicians to seek reassurance about a feared illness). Individuals who predominantly experience somatic symptoms and who present with anxieties about their symptoms and behaviors that aim at symptom relief are classified with SSD. A substantial subgroup of individuals with IAD avoids physicians due to intense anxiety about receiving a confirmation of a diagnosis they fear (Scarella et al., 2019).

Functional Neurological Symptom Disorder (FNSD)

Individuals diagnosed with FNSD show symptoms of altered voluntary motor or sensory function (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). In contrast to the SSD diagnosis, it is required that a clinical examination provides evidence that the symptom cannot be explained by a recognized neurological or medical condition. Symptoms are diverse (see Fig. 12.1; Espay et al., 2018). Terms such as “conversion disorder” or “psychogenic” that have been used as an equivalent to FNSD are not well accepted anymore. They refer to underlying mechanisms, that is, unconscious, psychodynamic processes in patients, which are not necessarily present. In contrast, “functional symptoms” refer to an abnormal functioning of the central nervous system and are rather of descriptive nature. To make the diagnosis of an FNSD, it is essential that the diagnostic process does not depend on excluding other disorders but on the examination of so-called positive signs. For example, a positive sign to diagnose functional leg weakness would be the Hoover sign. The Hoover sign is present if a weak hip extension is corrected when the patient flexes the contralateral hip against resistance (Espay et al., 2018).

Psychological Factors Affecting Other Medical Conditions (PFAOMC)

Diagnosing a patient with PFAOMC should be considered when individuals present with an underlying pathology of a medical condition in addition to diverse psychological factors that add an additional health risk and/or adversely affect the course, treatment progress, and outcome (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). The effect of psychological factor(s) on the medical condition has to be evident, although it is possible that the direct causal effect underlying the relationship cannot be fully demonstrated. Medical conditions for which PFAOMC can be diagnosed are various and can be identified in different medical specialties (see an overview in Fig. 12.2) (Porcelli & Sonino, 2007).

Factitious Disorder (FD)

A diagnosis of FD is made when individuals intentionally feign or produce either somatic or psychological symptoms, although there is no obvious external incentive (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). The term FD is historically related to the “Munchausen’s Syndrome” which is a more severe form of chronic fabrication of symptoms (American Psychiatric Association, 2022; Hausteiner-Wiehle & Hungerer, 2020). The DSM-5-TR differentiates between FD Imposed on Self and FD Imposed on Another (or “by Proxy”). FD Imposed on Another is an ethically and legally complex disorder, in which the perpetrator’s behavior might represent criminal behavior. A more modern form of FD is indicated as “by internet” or “by Google,” when stories of illness are disseminated on the internet. Methods that individuals who are diagnosed with FD apply to feign or produce symptoms vary. Examples are false or exaggerated reports of symptoms, exacerbating existing disease (e.g., interfering with wound healing), psychiatric symptoms through use of psychoactive substances, manipulating laboratory test, or induced bleeding.

Procedures for Gathering Information

Six Cornerstones of an Evidence-Based Assessment of SSRD

Clinical Interview

Several of the existing validated structured/semi-structured interviews include sections to assess SSD and IAD. Symptoms of FNSD and PFAOMC have to be explored through unstructured clinical interviews. There are no validated structured/semi-structured clinical interviews available to assess SSRD in children and adolescents.

Gaining Information from Medical Files and Other Healthcare Providers

SSRD constitute a group of diagnostic entities in the interface between psychology, medicine, and other disciplines. Therefore, it is crucially important that diagnostic information, if accessible, is gained from medical files and other healthcare providers of a patient in addition to a clinical interview and other procedures of gaining diagnostic information. This information impacts different stages of the diagnostic and treatment process. For example, it makes a difference to plan a psychological treatment for individuals whose symptoms are explained by an identifiable medical diagnosis compared to individuals who present with symptoms that currently cannot be medically explained (e.g., supporting the individual in accepting to have a diagnosed progressive chronic illness).

Self-Report and Clinician-Rated Scales to Assess Symptoms of SSRD and Associated Features

In addition to clinical interviews, using self-report or clinician-rated scales to assess the symptoms and associated cognitive, emotional, and behavioral features of SSRD is recommended. Moreover, validated scales exist to gain the following additional information that can support the diagnostic process: (a) impact of somatic symptoms on individuals’ physical, social and emotional functioning in their daily life, (b) ways of coping with somatic symptoms, (c) perceived self-efficacy and locus of control, and (d) symptom perceptions (e.g., individuals’ expectations about course and timeline of symptoms).

Prospective Assessment Tools

Memory bias in the assessment of somatic symptoms is common. Prospective measures, such as symptom diaries, can help individuals to provide a more accurate symptom report. They support the diagnostic process, help with planning the treatment for an individual, and can be used to monitor changes in symptoms during the course of the treatment as well. Some patients might be worried that their symptoms could deteriorate through paying more attention to them and are hesitant to complete a diary. These worries should be validated and the benefits of using a diary emphasized. Individuals should be invited to complete a diary only for a limited period of time (e.g., a week during the diagnostic phase and another week at the end of the treatment).

Informants Other Than Healthcare Providers

Involving individuals from patients’ environment to gain diagnostic information is important for several different reasons. First, as mentioned earlier, the retrospective memory of somatic symptoms and their course can be inaccurate or biased. Therefore, family members and friends of the patients and their observations of the patient’s symptoms in daily life can add important information to the diagnostic process. Second, living with persistent somatic symptoms has an impact on an individual’s social life. It is not uncommon that patients with SSRD feel disrespected and misunderstood by their social system. Associated with problematic and less supportive social interactions, these patients report decreased self-confidence, avoidant coping behaviors, increased stress, and problematic symptom appraisals. Learning about problems in social interaction both from individuals with SSRD themselves as well as from their family members and friends offers important information for the diagnostic process.

Behavioral Observation

Observational methods are a central part of diagnosing certain SSRD. For example, in individuals with FNSD with motor symptoms, the clinician will have to observe the intensity and quality of the symptoms. Illness behaviors, which are central features of several of the SSRD, can be very subtle, highly automated, and difficult for patients to describe (e.g., body scanning, avoidance behavior). Behavioral observation is needed to identify these behaviors (Oliveira et al., 2018; Vlaeyen et al., 1990).

Additional Interview Considerations

Building a Relationship with the Patient’s Healthcare Providers

As mentioned earlier, gaining information from other healthcare providers plays a central role in the diagnostic process of SSRD. Assuming the patient provides consent, healthcare providers should be contacted, updated about the diagnostic outcome, and a collaborative relationship should be established. If physicians who are involved in the patient’s treatment made a medical diagnosis, it is recommended to gain information about this condition. Being informed when starting the diagnostic process helps patients to feel understood and taken seriously and helps the interviewer to add relevant questions.

Patients with a Biomedical Symptom Model

Many patients with SSRD struggle with understanding why they are seen by healthcare providers other than physicians. Patients often experience their symptom(s) of primarily somatic but not psychological nature. Being sent to a psychologist or psychiatrist, or statements such as “your symptoms are caused in your brain” can be extremely confusing and can cause patients to feel dismissed or stigmatized as somebody who makes up symptoms. Part of the diagnostic process is therefore to explore how much the patient has already adopted a biopsychosocial than solely biomedical understanding of their symptoms.

Assessing Somatic Symptoms in Persons with Intellectual or Other Disabilities or in Nonverbal Individuals

Cognitive impairment has been demonstrated to be associated with an inability to rate symptom intensity with numerical rating scales. Patients who are unable to complete traditional self-report scales may be able to complete verbal pain rating scales that use simple phrases to describe different intensities of a symptom. When communication is impossible or the self-report is discordant, clinicians can resort to observational/behavioral symptom scales. Especially for pain symptoms, several scales have been developed and validated, e.g., the Chronic Pain Scale for Nonverbal Adults with Intellectual Disability (CPS-NAID; Burkitt et al., 2009).

Tools to Improve Rapport

Sometimes patients with SSRD can experience difficulties with putting their symptom perceptions into words. Creative methods, such as the use of pain cards (Semino et al., 2017) or photovoice (Teti et al., 2019), can be helpful to facilitate the patient’s description of their problem. These methods can help patients to disclose sensitive and emotional aspects of the impact of the symptoms on their life.

Procedures for Gathering Information Specific to Factitious Disorder

Factitious Disorder constitutes a challenging diagnostic entity of the SSRD. It cannot be identified through common interview techniques and assessment tools. Red flags and symptoms have to be observed and consistently documented through a treatment team (Hausteiner-Wiehle & Hungerer, 2020; Yates & Feldman, 2016). Often patients open up in particular to non-medical staff. In the following, some typical red flags are listed (Hausteiner-Wiehle & Hungerer, 2020):

  • Visible clinical signs, e.g., changed skin color, wound edges, blisters, strangulation marks.

  • Contradictory results of medical/psychological examination.

  • Uncommon course of healing (e.g., patient’s symptoms deteriorate prior to discharge).

  • Clinician’s presence associated with symptom changes.

  • Witnessed observation of patient manipulating tests, inducing symptoms, and other illness behaviors.

  • Indicators of (suspected) manipulations of patient’s medical history.

Assessing the risk of self-harming and suicidal behavior (Posner et al., 2011) is one of the most central steps of the diagnostic interviewing of individuals with FD. It is important to avoid developing prejudice towards patients to be saboteurs. Supportive confrontation and providing the patient with a safe space to disclose are the basic communication tools. Balancing between patient’s autonomy, open communication, the preservation of the patient’s health, and the clinician’s integrity is the basis for successful confrontation.

Recommendations for Formal Assessment

Tools for the Formal Assessment of SSRD in Adults

Clinical Interviews

The Diagnostic Interview for Anxiety, Mood, and OCD and Related Neuropsychiatric Disorders (DIAMOND) is one of the best validated structured interviews (Tolin et al., 2018) to assess current (not lifespan) SSD and IAD. In addition to determining whether an individual meets the diagnostic criteria, clinicians have the opportunity to rate the severity of the disorder on a scale from 1 (normal) to 7 (extreme) based on their clinical judgment of the patient’s current level of distress. Interrater and test-retest reliability across all diagnoses were rated very good to excellent, and evidence for convergent validity was established for almost all diagnoses (Tolin et al., 2018). The DIAMOND is accessible for free.

The Structured Clinical Interview for DSM-5 Disorders (SCID-5) is a semi-structured interview and exists in different versions. The clinical version (SCID-5-CV) only screens for SSD and IAD (First et al., 2016). The most comprehensive version of the SCID-5 is the research version, SCID-5-RV (First et al., 2015), which includes a section on current SSD and IAD. Similar to the DIAMOND, the questions of the SCID-5 operationalize the diagnostic criteria of the disorder in DSM-5. The clinician has to choose between whether a symptom is absent/subthreshold or the threshold is met. So far, psychometric data are provided for the clinical version of the SCID-5 regarding validity, sensitivity and specificity, interrater reliability, as well as test-retest reliability (Osorio et al., 2019).

Another semi-structured interview that was specifically designed to evaluate emotional and related disorders and claims to be more circumscribed compared to the SCID and DIAMOND is the Anxiety and Related Disorders Interview Schedule for DSM-5 (ADIS-5) by Brown and Barlow (2014). The ADIS-5 allows differential diagnoses between all anxiety and related disorders and includes a section on current as well as lifetime SSD and IAD. The clinician provides a clinical severity rating on a 9-point severity scale to distinguish between clinical and subclinical diagnoses. The lifetime version of the ADIS-5 (ADIS-5L) is designed to capture past (lifetime) diagnoses. So far, psychometric data has not been available for the ADIS-5, but just for its predecessor, the ADIS-IV.

Given that there are no validated (semi-)structured interviews available to make a PFAOMC and FNSD diagnosis, both disorders have to be explored by conducting unstructured clinical interviews. Prospective Assessment Tools/Symptom Diaries

Prospective self-monitoring tools and diaries exist for various somatic symptoms. Figure 12.3 provides examples of diary questions. Richardson et al. (2023) applied a diary to assess FNSD (non-epileptic seizures) and documented the time and date of the attack, symptoms before, during, and after the attack, patients’ ratings of their daily stress level on a scale between 0 and 10, activities and events during the day, and emotions that the patients felt during the day. Symptom diaries also exist for more specific functional somatic syndromes, such as Irritable Bowel Syndrome, e.g., the GI Symptom Diary by Blanchard (2001).

Tools to Document Informants’ Observations

There are not many tools available that help with documenting the informants’ observations. Vlaeyen et al. (1990) developed the Checklist for Interpersonal Pain Behavior (CHIP) specifically for patients with pain symptoms, which covers six domains of problematic symptom-related behaviors: distorted mobility, verbal complaints, nonverbal complaints, nervousness, depression, and day sleeping. Scales like the CHIP can be easily adjusted to other somatic symptoms.

Behavioral Observation Tools

Avoidance behavior (e.g., avoiding certain movements because of the fear of inducing pain) is a very common illness behavior across different types of SSRD that needs special attention in the diagnostic process because it can turn into a subtle yet highly automated behavior. Observational methods can help clinicians to identify and characterize avoidance behavior. The Photograph Series of Daily Activities (PHODA) is a popular tool to observe pain-related avoidance behaviors (Oliveira et al., 2018).

Tools for the Formal Assessment of SSRD in Children and Adolescents

Formal assessment tools to support the diagnosis of an SSRD in children and adolescents are very limited and were mostly developed to assess pain. According to the PedIMMPACT (McGrath et al., 2008) recommendations, traditional self-report tools to rate pain intensity can be used for children who are 8 years of age or older. Unidimensional measures such as visual analogue scales, numerical rating scales, or verbal rating scales (Lalloo & Stinson, 2014) are available. For children who are in pre-school age (5–7 years), graphic rating scales, e.g., Pieces of Hurt Tool (Hester, 1979), should be applied. Observational/behavioral scales are suggested for children who are 5 years of age or younger. There are several scales validated for children who are aged between 0 and 5 years, e.g., COMFORTneo (van Dijk et al., 2009). Besides pain intensity, children and their caregivers’ perspectives on the pain-related disability (e.g., Bath Adolescent Pain Questionnaire for adolescents aged between 13 and 18 years; Eccleston et al., 2005) and health-related quality of life (e.g., Paediatric Quality of Life; Varni et al., 1999) should be considered.

Case Illustrations

The recommended diagnostic procedure for the following two cases of SSRD requires three sessions to assess the patient. If this number of sessions is not available to clinicians who would like to follow our case presentations, we recommend conducting a clinical interview, providing self-report questionnaires to the patient prior to the first session, and gaining patients’ consent to contact their primary care provider (PCP).

Case 1: Somatic Symptom Disorder

Sabrina is 45-year-old woman who worked as a receptionist in a large company. She quit her job a couple of years ago and has lived from disability benefits since then. According to Sabrina, the pain spreading across her body has existed for more than 7 years and has become unbearable. She feels very frustrated after her enormous but unsuccessful efforts to gain control over her pain. Sabrina gave consent to contact her PCP as well as her pain physician. Both doctors confirm a diagnosis of fibromyalgia, a series of different diagnostic and treatment procedures Sabrina has undergone and that she has changed her healthcare providers several times.

Sabrina was asked to complete the DIAMOND Self Report Screener before her first session with us and affirmed the screening questions on a depressive episode, substance use disorders, and SSD. During the first two sessions, we conducted the corresponding sections of the DIAMOND with Sabrina. The interview confirmed that she meets all criteria of SSD. Sabrina reported various especially future-related worries. Sabrina reported emotions of anxiety, deep sadness, helplessness, and desperation, but also anger about her pain. Sabrina experiences the severe impact of the pain on numerous domains of her life. The DIAMOND confirmed that Sabrina meets the criteria of an episode of severe depression lasting for about 12 months and associated with occasional suicidal ideation. The suicidal risk assessment was conducted by applying the Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale. Conducting the substance use disorder section of the DIAMOND reveals Sabrina’s abuse of opioids. By asking Sabrina additional questions (see Table 12.1), we learned about her biomedical model of her persistent symptoms and low expectations regarding psychological interventions.

We asked Sabrina to complete four brief self-report scales, which were evaluated together with her in the second session. The Somatic Symptom Disorder-B Criteria Scale confirmed the severity of psychological symptoms of the SSD. The Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia revealed her avoidance behaviors in various domains of her everyday life. The Pain Catastrophizing Scale provided more insight into her disturbing thoughts associated with her symptoms. The Patients’ Endorsement of the Biopsychosocial Model of Chronic Pain Scale confirmed the clinician’s assumption that psychoeducation and cognitive reappraisal of the patient’s pain perceptions would be an important component of the treatment plan. After the second session, Sabrina was invited to complete a symptom diary (e.g., Fig. 12.3) over the upcoming 7 days. Through the diary, she observed that distressing events (e.g., emotionally distressing call with her healthcare insurance) can increase the severity of her pain symptoms substantially.

Case 2: Illness Anxiety Disorder

Jeff is a 35-year-old male engineer working for an international company. He often travels abroad for his work. After the last travel to an African country 1 year ago, Jeff returned with a bacterial infection of his bowel and suffered from gastrointestinal symptoms over a week. His PCP treated him successfully with antibiotics. Jeff felt very distressed by this infection and about future international travel. In the meantime, he developed persistent anxieties that he, for sure, suffers from a very rare form of bowel infection that is currently asymptomatic but could break out again in the future and that cannot be treated with common antibiotics. Every little change of his stool and gastrointestinal habits confuses Jeff and causes him to urgently and persistently seek advice from his PCP. He has changed his PCP several times over the past year because he cannot trust that the doctor has checked his condition sufficiently. He is worried about his future in the company he is working for, given that he has refused any further job-related travel since last year. Jeff started to restrict his diet and to be very selective about the places he eats food from. This also implies that he withdrew from several of his social contacts.

Conducting the Structured Clinical Interview for DSM-5 Disorders in the first and second session showed that Jeff meets the criteria of an IAD but no other mental health disorder. With his consent, we contacted his current PCP and gained access to Jeff’s medical file, which confirmed 35 consultations with 10 different PCPs and gastroenterologists over the past year. After the first session, we asked Jeff to complete the Short Health Anxiety Inventory as well as the Depression Anxiety and Stress Scales-21. Jeff showed increased scores on the health anxiety scale of the SHAI as well as increased beliefs of negative consequences of becoming ill, which is important information for later treatment planning, especially cognitive interventions. Although Jeff does not meet criteria of depression, his score on the depression scale of the DASS-21 was in the moderate range.

Jeff lives together with his partner John. We invited John to the third session with Jeff’s consent. John appeared to be very worried about Jeff’s anxieties and how they restrict his life. John also mentioned that communication with Jeff has become challenging. Jeff tries to seek reassurance from his partner. If John declines to provide this reassurance, Jeff often reacts with distance and expresses that he does not feel taken seriously. We asked Jeff to complete a diary on his health anxieties over the 7 days between the second and third session. We created this diary on our own and asked about the triggers of the health anxiety, anxiety intensity (scale 0–100%), unhelpful thoughts, and how much the patient believes in them (scale 0–100%) and focuses on his body (0–100%).

Impact of Sex/Gender, Culture, Race, Age, and Other Aspects of Diversity

Given that SSRD had not been introduced before 2013 with the publication of the DSM-5 (American Psychiatric Association, 2013), high-quality epidemiological studies based on the DSM-5 (and DSM-5-TR) criteria are still scarce and the following summary will refer to older studies based on criteria of the predecessor of SSRD, the so-called Somatoform Disorders.

Sex/Gender

Women tend to report somatic symptoms more commonly than men, an outcome that has been demonstrated across numerous studies of samples with less severe symptoms (Hiller et al., 2006) as well as clinically relevant syndromes of SSD (Jacobi et al., 2004). It seems however that gender-related distribution of symptom report is also influenced by cultural factors. A study by Piccinelli and Simon (1997) showed that the higher prevalence in women is especially common in Western societies. Women also present more frequently with FNSD compared to men (60–75%) (Espay et al., 2018). Regarding FD, the prevalence is as twice as high for women compared to men, and regarding FD by Proxy, most of the abusers are female and the victim’s mother (Yates & Bass, 2017). IAD on the contrary seems to be relatively evenly distributed between men and women (Scarella et al., 2019).

Age

Individuals of higher age seem to report persistent somatic symptoms more frequently (Hiller et al., 2006); however, it is unclear how much these symptoms are explained by nonspecific age-related degeneration. Interestingly, syndromes of multiple persistent somatic symptoms seem to be more common in younger age groups, between 18 and 34 years (Leiknes et al., 2007). There have not been differences between IAD patients and the general population in terms of age (Scarella et al., 2019). FNSD can occur across the lifespan (Feinstein, 2018). The onset of nonepileptic seizures peaks in the third decade, and functional motor symptoms have their peak onset in the fourth decade (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). Across different medical specialties and presentations, individuals who are affected by FD are usually younger, peak prevalence in the middle of their 30s (Hausteiner-Wiehle & Hungerer, 2020; Yates & Feldman, 2016). Regarding FD by Proxy, the vast majority of victims are younger than 5 years of age (Galvin et al., 2005).

Race and Ethnicity

In an epidemiological study conducted by the World Health Organization (Üstun & Sartorius, 1995), approximately 5500 patients in primary care practices across 15 different study centers were examined in southern and western European countries, Africa, Asia, the USA, and South America. Syndromes of SSD were increased in South American centers. The Epidemiological Catchment Area Study (Robins & Regier, 1991) in the USA revealed increased prevalence rates of syndromes of multiple persistent somatic symptoms in Black women and men. In a study in British primary care practices, individuals with a European background showed significant lower prevalence rates of somatic symptom report compared to individuals with an Asian background (Farooq et al., 1995). However, these studies are dated from the 1990s and their results are outdated, thus they should be interpreted carefully. Studies demonstrated no differences between patients with IAD and the general population in terms of race or ethnicity (Scarella et al., 2019).

Cultural Factors

Studies on transcultural differences show that reporting persistent somatic symptoms and associated distress is a common phenomenon worldwide (e.g., Üstun & Sartorius, 1995). One huge problem is that studies examining transcultural differences in SSRD and somatic symptom report use scales that have mainly been developed in Western countries and are not sensitive toward culturally specific symptoms. Somatic symptoms are, however, prominent in so-called “cultural concepts of distress,” defined as “ways that cultural groups experience, understand, and communicate suffering, behavioral problems, or troubling thoughts and emotions” (American Psychiatric Association, 2022, p. 871). An example is Ataque de nervios, a syndrome in individuals of Latin descent, which includes intense emotional upset and various somatic symptoms such as trembling, heat in the chest, or seizure-like episodes. The DSM-5-TR provides a glossary of nine cultural concepts of distress. We strongly recommend to additionally apply the Cultural Formulation Interview (American Psychiatric Association, 2022) in a diagnostic process to assess SSDR in individuals with a cultural background different from the interviewers’ cultural background.

Socioeconomic Status and Level of Education

The results on relationships between somatoform disorders and socioeconomic status and level of education are mixed. A high-quality epidemiological study conducted in Germany demonstrated that the highest prevalence rates of somatoform disorders occurred in individuals of the lowest socioeconomic level and those who are unemployed, divorced, widowed, or live in separation (Jacobi et al., 2004). There are no differences between patients with IAD and the general population in terms of socioeconomic status and level of education (Scarella et al., 2019).

Information Critical to Making a Diagnosis

Differential Diagnosis: Other Medical Conditions

The importance of a differential diagnosis regarding another medical condition varies between the different types of SSRD. The FNSD is the diagnostic entity that requires a thorough medical assessment and ruling out any neurological disease that might better explain the symptoms. PFAOMC requires a medical symptom or condition to be present. FD does not exclude the presence of a medical condition and often occurs along with FD as a comorbid condition (e.g., diabetes and manipulating blood sugar test results). IAD also does not exclude the presence of a medical condition. If a medical condition is present, the health anxiety has to be clearly disproportionate to its seriousness. Regarding SSD, it is important to emphasize that just the presence of somatic symptoms either of clear or unclear etiology (e.g., irritable bowel syndrome) is not sufficient to make the diagnosis. There must be psychological symptoms (cognitions, emotions, behaviors) that go along with the somatic symptoms. Importantly, note that the presence of an established medical diagnosis does not exclude a diagnosis of an SSD.

Differential Diagnosis: Other Mental Disorder

Somatic symptoms occur commonly in mental disorders other than SSRD, such as Anxiety Disorders (e.g., Panic Disorder, Generalized Anxiety Disorder) or Depressive Disorders. Somatic symptoms in the context of Panic Disorder appear in acute episodes, whereas in SSD and FNSD, they are more persistent. Although patients with Panic Disorder have health anxieties, anxieties as core characteristics of IAD are more persistent. Generalized Anxiety Disorder is associated with worries about multiple aspects, and health is only one of them. Depressive Disorders are commonly accompanied by somatic symptoms; however, a Depressive Disorder is distinguished from SSRD through the cardinal symptoms of depression. Individuals with depression can excessively ruminate about health and illness. A separate diagnosis of IAD could be justified as long as these anxieties persist after remission of an episode of depression.

In individuals diagnosed with a serious illness, health-related anxieties can be a normal response. If the health anxiety is severe enough but only transient, an Adjustment Disorder can be diagnosed. An IAD would be only diagnosed if symptoms are severe enough, are distressing and disabling, and last for at least 6 months. Regarding the differentiation between Adjustment Disorder and PFAOMC, it is a question of the direction of the relationship between psychological factor and illness. For example, if an individual where cardiovascular symptoms of an angina always follow episodes of intensive negative emotions, PFAOMC would be the adequate diagnosis. If a patient with a cardiovascular disease develops maladaptive anticipatory anxiety, they would be diagnosed with an Adjustment Disorder. Usually, the psychological factors and somatic illness interact in a reciprocal manner, in which case the distinction is arbitrary.

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder and IAD both are characterized by intrusive thoughts about having a disease and compulsive behaviors (e.g., IAD: reassurance seeking). The central differentiation is, however, that intrusive thoughts in patients with OCD focus on getting a disease in the future whereas in IAD they focus on having a disease. Moreover, people with OCD often have a variety of intrusive thoughts, not all focused on health.

Differential Diagnosis: Some Specific Considerations About Factitious Disorder Versus Malingering

Malingering has to be differentiated from FD, mainly through the existence of obvious external rewards and more conscious motivation, although the latter can be difficult to assess. Moreover, in individuals who malinger, compared to FD (Hausteiner-Wiehle & Hungerer, 2020), self-harm is usually nonexisting or only mild, symptoms are feigned (versus rather self-induced in FD), willingness to change is nonexistent or little, any comorbid disorders are rare, and results of medical or psychological examinations are usually normal.

Dos and Don’ts

As mentioned earlier, many patients with SSRD have undergone a long history of partly disappointing interactions with the healthcare system. Clinicians conducting interviews with individuals with SSRD should be aware that the diagnostic sessions do not just have the purpose to make a diagnosis but to build a therapeutic relationship and to facilitate patients’ readiness to adopt an interdisciplinary treatment approach including psychological interventions. The following Dos and Don’ts shall help guide clinicians through the diagnostic process.

Dos

Reflect on your own emotions toward patients with persistent somatic symptoms. If you observe negative emotions and attitudes, first think about how you can handle them. Demonstrate genuine interest in your way of asking questions and understanding the patient. Validate the reality of the symptom severity and interference. Although the diagnostic sessions focus on making a diagnosis, it is helpful for patients to be aware of what psychology can offer and what it cannot. Be transparent about patients’ proactive role in the therapeutic process. Use the opportunity that patients have often not been taken seriously and that you now provide time to understand them. Patients also have a strong need to find explanations for their symptoms. For patients, finding causes usually is the first step to finding a successful treatment. Be validating toward patients’ needs, but at the same time do not get involved in unfruitful, frustrating discussions of symptom attributions and potential causes. Introduce psychotherapy as a “second treatment path,” which opens new perspectives and approaches in addition to the approaches the patient has already applied. Apply techniques from the Motivational Interviewing approach (e.g., ask open questions, active listening, validation, summarizing) (Miller & Rollnick, 2023). In case the patient has a known medical condition, inform yourself about it. Gain the patient’s consent to contact other healthcare providers involved in the patient’s treatment and access to the patient’s medical file. Emphasize the patient’s autonomy in decision-making and if they want to move on with a psychological treatment. Keep an open mind regarding etiology: maybe the symptom cannot be completely explained under the current circumstances. This does not mean that there will be no explanations in the future. Most important is that the patient currently does not only focus on the intensity of the symptom but also on how distressing the symptom is and the negative impact it has.

Don’ts

Don’t tell patients that they “will have to live with it” or that “symptoms are produced in their mind or brain.” Don’t use terms that imply a biopsychosocial nature of the symptom too early in the interaction with the patient, such as “psychosomatic,” “psychogenic,” “somatoform,” or “psychological/mental.” Don’t make decisions for the patient or fall into body-mind dichotomous thinking (e.g., “We cannot find a medical cause, for this reason your symptom is psychosomatic”). Avoid providing reassurance that your patient does not have an organic disease.

Summary

Somatic Symptoms and Related Disorders are a very heterogeneous group of mental disorders. They include disorders characterized by persistent somatic symptoms with associated distress and disability (SSD, FNSD), by a cognitive preoccupation of having or gaining a somatic condition (IAD), by psychological factors that impact the onset and perpetuation of somatic symptoms (PFAOMC), or by induced somatic or mental symptoms (FD). Compared to other mental disorders, it is important to consider that SSRD are phenomena in the intersection between medicine, psychology, and other healthcare disciplines. That makes the diagnostic process unique compared to other disorders, and in addition to the application of common diagnostic tools in mental health (e.g., structured interviews, self-report, and observational tools), it is important to establish a collaborative relationship with your patient’s other healthcare providers. If possible and if time is given, the diagnostician should involve also informants from the patient’s environment.
