Interviewing Individuals at Risk for Suicide

Description of the Problem

Suicide is a public health concern in the United States and abroad. The National Center for Health Statistics in the United States estimates that 49,449 individuals died by suicide in the United States in 2022, a three percent rise from the prior year (Curtin & Garnett, 2023). The twenty-first century has seen a rise in suicide rates in the United States. In the year 2000, population-level suicide rates were estimated to be 10.4 deaths for every 100,000 individuals; this rate has risen to 14.3 deaths for every 100,000 in 2022 (National Institute of Mental Health, 2024). While there were brief declines in the suicide rate between 2019 and 2020, the overall rates have rebounded and are now on the rise again, highlighting the clear need for evidence-based assessments and interventions.

Even greater than suicide deaths are the number of individuals who report experiencing serious thoughts of suicide. Upwards of 13 million adults in the United States report having had serious thoughts of suicide in the last year, and 1.6 million adults report attempting suicide in the last year (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration, 2023). The rise in the incidence of suicidal thoughts and behaviors has been linked to numerous social and economic risk factors such as a lack of access to mental health resources and financial strain (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS), 2024). In more recent years, research has demonstrated that suicidality disproportionately affects marginalized groups. For example, research has demonstrated that non-Hispanic American Indian and Alaskan Native persons are disproportionately represented in suicide statistics, with an estimated rate of 22.1 deaths for every 100,000 individuals (Stone et al., 2023). Suicide rates have also significantly increased in Black youth, rising by 19.2% from 2018 to 2021 (Stone et al., 2023). Additionally, LGBTQ+ youth are at four times greater risk of attempting suicide than their same-aged peers (The Trevor Project, 2023). When working with individuals at risk for suicide, clinicians must be sensitive to the diverse risk factors that may influence an individual’s suicidal thoughts and behavior, a point that will be further discussed in this chapter.

While we cannot predict with any certainty whether an individual will die by suicide, an adept clinician can evaluate various risk factors and warning signs for suicidal risk (Tucker et al., 2015) along with protective factors during the interview process that can lead to an overall clinical formulation of relative risk. Moreover, the formulation of risk can be further supplemented by considerations of relative stability that can directly inform considerations of “clear and imminent” danger that potentially triggers more restrictive care (Jobes, 2023). The interview for suicidal risk can take many different forms depending upon the needs of the clinician and patient. There have been several attempts at routinized assessment over the years (e.g. Berman & Silverman, 2014; Chu et al., 2015; Shea, 2009; Silverman & Berman, 2014), each taking a systematic approach that includes assessment of one’s history of suicidal behavior, plans or intended method of suicide, access to lethal means, one’s experience of suicidal desire and intent, and other clinical risk factors (e.g., hopelessness, stressful life events). Bottom line, the ultimate goal of the assessment is to create a suicide risk formulation. Clearly, people who are suicidal are not identical; the formulation of relative risk is thus not an exercise in prediction as much as it reflects a level of clinical concern that can inform intervention (Berman & Silverman, 2014).

To create the basis for sound clinical judgment related to suicide risk, clinicians must be aware of the best practices for assessing suicide, and receive adequate training. Moreover, it is important that clinicians develop “usual and customary practices” in advance for working with individuals at risk (Jobes, 2020). Working with patients who are suicidal may be anxiety-provoking for clinicians without such training and expertise. In fact, such clinical practice may actually be unethical and could further put the patient at risk (Jobes & Barnett, 2024; Sommers-Flanagan & Sommers-Flanagan, 1995). The current chapter is thus a guide, offering concise and specific recommendations for interviewing individuals at risk for suicide, emphasizing the integrative use of evidence-based tools and semi-structured assessment approaches.

Procedures for Gathering Information

The process of gathering information from individuals at risk for suicide will vary greatly depending on the setting, amount of time allotted, and the clinician’s skill and preferences. In general, it is preferred that a combination of structured assessment tools and semi-structured or unstructured approaches be used when conducting an interview. The clinical interview should answer basic questions related to the patient’s history and current experiences with suicidal thoughts, feelings, and behaviors. The following section highlights important domains for assessment. In addition to unstructured or semi-structured interviewing, clinicians should consider incorporating actuarial assessments (both self-report and clinician-administered) to aid in collecting further information. These instruments can meaningfully supplement the clinician’s interview and also add valuable documentation that reduces exposure to malpractice liability if there is an adverse outcome (Jobes & Barnett, 2024).

Clinical Interview

The clinical interview can be unstructured, semi-structured, or structured, depending on the needs of the clinician, patient, and resource or time limitations. Unstructured or semi-structured approaches allow for a greater degree of flexibility. Structured approaches may have the advantage of providing specific questions and prompts for the clinician to follow and may have greater reliability and validity. However, structured interviews have less flexibility, which could mean that important aspects of suicide risk remain unexplored. Regardless of the approach, it is important to ensure that a number of evidence-based risk and protective factors are assessed during the interview. These domains have been previously discussed by scholars, and are further adapted and outlined in detail below (e.g., Berman & Silverman, 2014; Bryan & Rudd, 2006; Chu et al., 2015; Fowler, 2012; Silverman & Berman, 2014).

Domains of Assessment
Within the clinical interview, there are key domains of assessment that should be emphasized, leading to an overall clinical formulation of risk (see Table 21.1 for sample questions to guide the assessment of these core areas).
  1. 1.

    Suicidal Thinking: The patient’s current experience of suicidal ideation and thoughts, including frequency, intensity, and duration.

     
  2. 2.

    Suicide Planning: The patient’s stated plans for suicide. This may include time, place, method, and access to means. Increased specificity of plans may indicate increased risk.

     
  3. 3.

    Suicide Preparation: Any steps the patient has already taken to prepare for suicide; This may include actions such as giving away belongings or accessing lethal means.

     
  4. 4.

    History of Behavior: The patient’s history of suicidal behaviors including time since occurrence, the frequency and context around previous behavior, and the lethality and method of any previous attempts or aborted attempts. Significantly greater risk is associated with anyone who has two or more suicide attempts.

     
  5. 5.

    Precipitating Stressors: Any recent precipitating factors such as significant losses (e.g., loss of a loved one, financial loss, loss of a relationship, or loss of identity), health problems, or relationship problems.

     
  6. 6.

    Other Symptoms: Any symptoms related to psychiatric diagnoses (depression, bipolar disorder, anxiety, schizophrenia, substance use disorder, etc.) and symptoms such as sadness, anhedonia, anxiety, fatigue, hopelessness, impulsivity, or a lack of self-control.

     
  7. 7.

    Protective Factors: Factors that may be protective against suicidal thoughts and behavior, such as the presence of social support, coping skills, reasons for living, religion or a sense of meaning in life, and active participation in treatment.

While actuarial assessments can be helpful for gathering large amounts of information quickly, they can be subject to several limitations such as high false positives and a narrow range of questions (Bryan & Rudd, 2006). Advantages and Disadvantages of Assessment Measures

Of the instruments listed in Table 21.2, the ASQ and C-SSRS are noteworthy as they are both non-proprietary (readily available online) and are relatively easy to use (Jobes, 2023). The ASQ is a brief screening tool that can provide clinicians with a rapid identification of a patient’s possible risk of suicidal ideation and behaviors. The ASQ was originally developed for use with pediatric patients but has since been normed with adults and has been translated into many languages (Horowitz et al., 2020). While its brevity is an advantage, it does not delve deeply into more questions about suicide planning or preparation. The National Institute of Mental Health offers a “toolkit” for using the ASQ at the following link: https://​www.​nimh.​nih.​gov/​research/​research-conducted-at-nimh/​asq-toolkit-materials.

Andreotti et al. (2020) have noted that the C-SSRS and BSSI are often mentioned in the clinical research literature and both have sound psychometric support for their use. As probably the most widely used tool, the C-SSRS has multiple versions, including one designed for tracking between visits, one assessing lifetime suicidal thoughts and behavior in addition to recent symptoms, and a brief screener (Posner et al., 2011). The C-SSRS can either be a clinician- or self-administered measure. The clinician-administered version has the advantage of allowing for qualitative responses. The BSSI has the advantage of being quicker and easier to administer given that it relies on patient self-report and more objective, numerical responses. However, as noted, the C-SSRS is free and accessible (https://​cssrs.​columbia.​edu/​) whereas the BSSI is a proprietary measure that must be purchased, an option that may not be accessible to all clinicians or institutions. The BSSI is often used in clinical trials of patients who are suicidal as the “gold standard” tool for suicidal ideation.

The remaining assessment instruments that we are highlighting—the PHQ-9 and SITBI—are somewhat less cited within the literature but still have the ability to provide useful information to supplement clinical interview assessment data. The PHQ-9 (a depression screening measure) is widely used in the field in part because it is non-proprietary and there are data for its value to identify potential suicidal risk (Simon et al., 2016). However, the PHQ-9 has the disadvantage of only including one conflated question (i.e., “Thoughts that you would be better off dead, or of hurting yourself”) related to suicide. Importantly, other research has shown that the PHQ-9 can miss patients who are suicidal, fundamentally undermining its value as a suicide assessment (Mournet et al., 2021).

The SITBI is the longest of the highlighted instruments, assessing a wide array of risk variables and characteristics related to self-injurious thoughts and behavior. While the SITBI has the advantage of providing a wealth of information related to suicidal thoughts and behaviors, it leaves less room for flexibility in assessment and may have more utility within clinical research.

Recommendations for Formal Assessment

It is well understood that suicide risk assessments are anxiety-inducing for clinicians (Roush et al., 2018; Wingate et al., 2004). Therefore, during these encounters, it is important for clinicians to remain calm and adopt a balanced approach—avoiding over- or under-estimating a patient’s suicidality based on a single consideration. By its nature, a case formulation approach considers and integrates the full range of assessment data to conceptualize a patient’s relative risk and stability. Research has established that a clinician’s confidence in conducting suicide risk assessments is enhanced through access to trainings, even those that are brief (Wakai et al., 2020). Given that a clinician’s comfort level is correlated to their consistent use of evidence-based practices (Roush et al., 2018), training resources can offer vital guidance to performing effective assessments that can meaningfully enhance suicide prevention practices.

Many professional organizations offer their guidelines and general recommendations for accurately assessing suicidal risk in clinical populations, such as the American Psychological Association (APA; Knapp, 2020) and the American Psychiatric Association (ApA; Jacobs et al., 2003). As these resources highlight, there is no single instrument that is superior in screening for suicidal risk (Saab et al., 2022), both in terms of sensitivity (i.e., reliably detecting truly at-risk individuals) and specificity (i.e., consistently identifying no-risk individuals). Instead, as Bernert and colleagues suggest, procedures must be standardized to ensure that the core competencies of suicide risk screening are being met across settings and professionals (Bernert et al., 2014).

Such competencies include discussions of an individual’s suicidality experience (e.g., establishing their presenting problem, risk and protective factors, degree of intent, history of behavior, and any preparation), symptom management (e.g., safety planning, lethal means restriction, treatments, and strategies), and other important considerations (e.g., ethical and legal, such as confidentiality and proper documentation). These competencies can be ensured by using the aforementioned assessment tools along with self-report structured and semi-structured interviewing which can also be meaningfully supplemented by collateral information (i.e., with the patient’s permission, obtaining information from family and close friends).

First and foremost, formal assessments for suicidality must rely on validated procedures in the given setting and population clinicians work within. A suicide risk assessment must be adapted for its immediate context. The assessment process is going to differ between virtual and in-person settings, emergency departments and outpatient practices, and research and community settings. Regardless of the environment, formal assessments must be consistent in their acceptability, feasibility, and adherence to best practices.

Until recently, standard practice has been in-person assessments. However, since the COVID-19 pandemic, telemedicine has skyrocketed and revolutionized the mental health field. While in-person assessments may be more suitable for patients with comorbid and complex conditions (e.g., substance use, violence; Shore & Lu, 2015), virtual platforms and telehealth-care provide undeniable conveniences such as timely assessment and safety planning, cost-effectiveness, increased continuity of care, and may be more considerate of patient preferences. It is recommended that while conducting virtual suicide risk assessments, clinicians should establish (1) the current location of the patient, (2) a plan for reconnecting if technology fails, (3) emergency contact information, (4) an assessment of their immediate lethal means access, and (5) a discussion of privacy requirements and confidentiality limits (for more information refer to: https://​theactionallianc​e.​org/​resource/​covid-guidance-screening-suicide-risk-during-telehealth-visits).

The procedures outlined previously can be used to help identify individuals who are open to directly expressing their suicidality risk. The reality is that not all individuals who experience suicidality directly communicate or endorse suicidal intent, with some even actively concealing their experiences (Podlogar & Joiner, 2020). In such cases, there can be indirect communications and behaviors that may suggest potential suicidal risk. Such communications can be conscious or may be unconscious but nevertheless provide indications that may reflect intention and increased risk therein (e.g., preparatory behaviors, help-rejecting, and hopelessness). This underscores the importance of listening to what the patient overtly says but what also may be inferred through certain behaviors or vague comments. For example, a provider should pay close attention and follow-up when a patient off-handedly says “what’s the point?” or “things just never work out for me” or “I just need the pain to end!”

The importance of an empathic approach to assessing suicidal risk cannot be over-stated. Indeed, the “Aeschi Approach” in the 2000s was a movement that focused on the merits of an empathic and non-judgmental approach to forming alliances with patients who were suicidal (Michel & Jobes, 2011). The Aeschi movement was decidedly not a “medical model” approach to suicide, emphasizing a patient-centered and narrative approach that was featured in ten international conferences and an edited book. By adopting an empathetic approach, clinicians can thus create an optimal and validating relationship that is free from judgment and stigma.

Despite professional guidance, increased scrutiny, and updated guidelines (such as the APA’s lengthy 2020 guide), recent studies underscore the issue of patient nondisclosure or concealment within formal assessment practices. For example, Hallford et al.’ (2023) meta-analysis showed that in comparison to family and friends, participants were much less inclined to disclose their suicidal risk to healthcare professionals. A recent qualitative study recruiting individuals who had previously attempted suicide highlighted various reasons behind suicidality concealment such as fear of stigma, involuntary treatment, and invasion of privacy (Sheehan et al., 2019). To combat nondisclosure, Podlogar and Joiner (2020) recommend that clinicians prioritize a patient-centered approach, which should immediately foster hope and provide validation. Patients should be encouraged to share their experiences in their own words and be active collaborators in their treatment planning and intervention outcomes. A supportive clinician-patient alliance is key to accurate suicide screening and treatment (Knapp, 2020).

An important goal of suicide risk assessments is to help better inform and guide clinical case management and treatment planning such that suicidal suffering can be decreased and patients become more stable and able to manage any suicidal thoughts or feelings (Jobes, 2023). Clinicians should be mindful that the assessment of suicidal risk provides valuable information for effective treatment planning that better informs safety planning and stabilization as well as setting the stage for effective suicide-focused treatment (Jobes & Barnett, 2024). Formal assessments should not be a one-time occurrence, but rather a continued and evolving assessment process in which individuals are provided an opportunity to fine-tune their responses concurrent to their self-exploration within the course of clinical treatment.

Case Illustrations

“Janice”

As a 20-year-old gay Latina college student, Janice screened positive for suicidal thoughts on an ASQ that was administered by an emergency department (ED) nurse. She was brought to the ED by her roommate who witnessed Janice impulsively overdose on her SSRI antidepressant and medications after a fierce fight and break up with her girlfriend of 3 years. Janice remained extremely anxious, dysregulated, and inconsolable in the ED and was subsequently admitted to an inpatient psychiatric unit. The day after her admission, Janice met with a psychology intern on the unit who administered a C-SSRS, a Beck Depression Inventory, and a Beck Anxiety Inventory. In addition, the intern also conducted a 45-minute assessment interview.

The intern had been well trained in suicide assessment and was both empathic and validating. In the course of their interview, Janice acknowledged that she did not really want to die, stating: “I just didn’t know what else to do—I can’t imagine living without her.” Janice occasionally experienced passing suicidal thoughts in high school after a classmate took his life, but this overdose was her first suicide attempt. Janice tearfully disclosed a fairly extensive history of self-harm cutting behavior that was always triggered by acutely anxious states mostly related to family of origin conflicts. Her family relationships were complicated by years of sexual abuse by her maternal grandfather and her sexual orientation which was unacceptable to her devotedly Catholic family. Janice noted that all her relationships tended to be volatile and intense.

After several days on the inpatient unit, Janet was referred to a community treatment center and put on a 3-month waiting list for Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT). In the meantime, she would receive supportive counseling and family therapy was strongly recommended.

Comment on the Case of Janice

As a 20-something, marginalized, gay woman, over-dosing on medication following a relationship conflict, Janice represents a modal suicide attempter within the United States. Her history of sexual trauma, anxiety, depression, and self-harm cutting behavior along with volatile relationships all contribute to the complexity of her case. Given this history and set of symptoms, Janice would be an excellent candidate for intensive DBT, but waitlists for receiving this excellent evidence-based treatment are not atypical.

Of note, the distinction between her self-harm behaviors and suicide is important to make (and patients need psycho-education on this distinction). Janice did not aspire to terminate her biological existence; she just did not otherwise know how to cope with her intense dysregulation in the wake of her breakup. Her overdose on SSRIs is not objectively lethal but is nevertheless concerning. The absence of any other previous attempts is important. If Janice is able to receive DBT to learn emotion regulation skills and engage in family therapy (and perhaps some trauma-informed therapy), her prognosis could be promising but such care can be elusive, time-consuming, and expensive.

“Frank”

As a divorced, alcoholic, middle-aged (53), white male, electrical engineer, Frank has struggled with a history of depression and several failed attempts to become sober. Estranged from his three sons and at risk of losing his job at a power company, Frank recently bought a handgun and had written several suicide notes to his ex-wife and sons to say goodbye. At work, and to his surprise, Frank broke down in tears and shared with a work colleague that he was depressed and “ready to give up.” The astute colleague walked Frank down to the personnel office and an evaluation with an Employee Assistance Program (EAP) psychologist was immediately made.

The savvy EAP clinician administered a C-SSRS and noted Frank’s serious suicidal risk. In a calm, even manner, he talked to Frank about possible treatment options that would be covered under the company’s health plan. Frank admitted to the EAP clinician that he was reluctant to reveal his suicidal thinking because he feared being hospitalized against his will. While the clinician acknowledged that hospitalization is sometimes necessary, there are excellent suicide-focused treatments that can be conducted on an outpatient basis. Toward the end of their 2-h interview, the EAP clinician referred Frank to an in-network clinical social worker who could provide an intervention called the “Collaborative Assessment and Management of Suicidality” (CAMS) and an appointment with her was made for later in the week. While skeptical, Frank nevertheless felt guardedly encouraged and was grateful for the compassionate EAP response.

Comment on the Case of Frank

In contrast to Janice, Frank is a prime example of a person who dies by suicide in the United States, wherein men—particularly white males—dominate suicide deaths. Frank’s history of depression, alcohol abuse, isolation, and job troubles are all worrisome. Immediately concerning is his purchase of a handgun (the number one method for suicide in the United States); his preparatory behavior of writing suicide notes is also troubling. However, Frank’s lack of any previous suicide attempts is encouraging and the relative “newness” of his suicidal thinking make him an especially good candidate for CAMS which can be safely rendered on an outpatient basis (Jobes, 2023).

Within the first session of CAMS Frank received an in-depth assessment of his suicide risk using a tool called the “Suicide Status Form” that was completed collaboratively by Frank and his clinician (sitting side-by-side). The SSF in-depth assessment led to the development of the CAMS Stabilization Plan and a suicide-focused treatment plan that centered on identifying Franks “drivers” of suicide. In Frank’s case, a key initial intervention involved removing access to the new handgun (which was taken by his co-worker for safe keeping). Frank’s CAMS treatment intensively focused on alleviating his suicidal drivers (i.e., his two problem drivers focused on re-engaging with his sons and getting sober). While Frank is a worrisome clinical presentation, he did not need to be hospitalized because lethal means were quickly secured, a solid Stabilization Plan was established, and he received an evidence-based suicide-focused treatment.

The handling of Frank’s case is an excellent example of superb clinical risk management response that is ethical and optimal as least-restrictive, evidence-based, and suicide-focused care was provided, which was in Frank’s best interest and was simply the right thing to do (Jobes & Barnett, 2024). Two years after six sessions of CAMS, Frank was sober and spending time with his sons after engaging in successful family therapy. With his suicide clinically averted, and a solid program of sobriety, Frank was dating a woman in recovery and he reported feeling like he had been “reborn” into a life worth living.

Impact of Sex/Gender, Culture, Race, Age, and Other Aspects of Diversity

During formal assessments, it is important to remain cognizant and actively consider an individual’s biological and sociocultural experiences as they may impact their suicide risk. While certain populations have historically posed the greatest risk of suicide deaths (e.g., white males), other subpopulations have been increasing at alarming rates (e.g., Hispanics; Khubchandani & Price, 2022). Overall, a little less than 50% of all people who suffer from suicidal thoughts and behaviors actually disclose their experiences making identification and treatment difficult (Hallford et al., 2023). The following section reviews the intersectionality of suicidality with various demographic and sociocultural identities, including the consideration of differing tendencies related to disclosures of suicide risk.

Sex and Gender Differences

The gender paradox has been well studied in the suicidology field (Canetto & Sakinofsky, 1998; Schrijvers et al., 2012). When looking through a biological lens, suicidal ideation and non-lethal behavior rates tend to be higher for females whereas males have higher rates of suicide mortality (Beautrais, 2002; Bommersbach et al., 2022). Males tend to choose more lethal methods, but a range of other methodological and sociocultural factors can also explain their increased risk. As Cibis et al. (2012) suggest, males’ level of suicidal intent, help-seeking behaviors, substance use, aggression, and impulsivity all play a role in increasing their risk. Clinicians should be aware when formally assessing individuals that both biological sex and gender identity alter one’s suicide risk. In a meta-analysis conducted by Surace et al. (2021) of ten studies from mostly Western cultures, gender non-conforming youth had higher prevalence rates of suicidality than their cisgender peers. The authors noted how experiences with stigmatization, minority stress, peer victimization, social isolation, and comorbidities especially affect individuals with gender dysphoria. Suicide risk assessments should therefore include opportunities for individuals to share such stressors.

Another study looked at sex differences in disclosure of suicide thoughts and behaviors (Balt et al., 2021). Researchers interviewed family and friends of youth decedents to gauge any differences in frequency, primacy, mode (e.g., verbal, nonverbal, and written), and content of suicide-related communications. They also investigated who individuals preferred as a confidant for disclosing their suicidality. Males tended to disclose their suicidality experiences later, in closer proximity to their deaths compared to females. Males also indirectly communicated their suicidality (e.g., hints, jokes, and broad questions about death) while females were more explicit. Females frequently self-harmed and wrote about their suicidality privately (i.e., journals, stories, drawings). Females more frequently sought help and disclosed their suicidal ideation. Respondents reported that males seemed more verbally aggressive and communicated their frustration, whereas females spoke of their guilt and burdensomeness. If males did disclose their suicidality, it was only to family, partners, and friends. Females boasted a larger range of confidants, from family, close friends, peers (online and from school), and healthcare professionals. The results of this study can help to inform formal suicidality assessments, particularly when gathering information from multiple informants (collateral information). But further research is needed to replicate and expand these findings beyond a single sample.

Cultural and Racial Differences

A brief review of literature and national data reveals the disproportionate rate at which racial and ethnic minorities are dying by suicide. At the forefront are American Indian and Alaskan Natives, followed by non-Hispanic Whites (CDC, 2023; Curtin, 2019). Alarmingly, African American—especially black youth—suicide prevalence rates are growing the fastest with recent estimates around 11.8% (Bridge et al., 2018; Ivey-Stephenson et al., 2020). Similarly, the US Hispanic population has experienced increasing suicide rates over the years especially pronounced in youth populations (Meza & Bath, 2021). These increasing rates are outpacing the field’s attempts to develop culturally responsive suicide assessment tools (Molock et al., 2023).

Cultural and racial identities impact an individual’s perception and therefore experience of suicidality. For example, Hispanic individual’s beliefs (i.e., machismo and marianismo), nativity, acculturation, and values (familismo and religion) have all been tied to their experiences of suicidality (Barranco, 2016; Bersani & Morabito, 2020; Hovey, 2000; Langhinrichsen-Rohling et al., 2009; Silva et al., 2023). In lieu of well-validated tools designed for varied cultural contexts, clinicians are encouraged to take an open approach with their selected measures. The bare minimum is for all suicide assessments to provide individuals with the opportunity to reflect how their cultural and racial identities influence their suicidality.

One’s cultural experiences can impact their ability and willingness for suicidality disclosure. Help-seeking behaviors are already low in individuals who suffer from suicidality (Mann et al., 2005). Ethnic and racial minorities must also overcome what Yu et al. (2022) describe as the “double stigma,” or the compounding experience of discrimination based on both racial identity and mental health diagnostic status. These researchers did observe that racial stigma increased Black adults’ perceived barriers to seeking mental healthcare. It is also important to note that medical mistrust, especially among the African American community given their historical mistreatment, impacts their willingness to seek mental healthcare to this day (Colvin et al., 2024). Therefore, culturally competent clinicians must consistently recognize the systematic and individual barriers ethnically and racially diverse individuals must overcome to participate in formal assessments.

Unsurprisingly, these trends and barriers are consistently noted within young adult population research. In a sample of college students, ethnic minority clients took longer to disclose suicidal ideation, and most only reported their suicidality when prompted by the clinician (Morrison & Downey, 2000). In a follow-up study, Morrison and Downey recorded that African American students tended to list more reasons for living than their White peers. Moreover, the survey revealed that the African American students have stronger moral objections to suicide. In sum, one’s cultural and racial backgrounds undoubtedly affect how one thinks about and communicates their suicidality. Clinicians conducting suicide risk assessments must foster their own cultural competency knowledge to best serve their all populations—particularly those from marginalized communities.

Age Differences

Trends in suicide risk across age groups continue to evolve. Since 2009, suicide rates for individuals aged 10–24 have been on the rise (Curtin & Garnett, 2023). Based on the latest data for 2022, suicide has been a top 10 leading cause of death from ages 10 years old to 54 years old (CDC, 2023). Furthermore, the data has shown that suicide is the second leading cause of death for individuals aged 10–34 years old with rates declining into later adulthood. Scholars have been dedicated to understanding the development and sustainment of suicidality across age groups. This research is helpful for clinicians when formulating case conceptualizations from suicide risk assessments.

A large-scale study was recently conducted with the United States’ National Violent Death Reporting System (NVDRS) suicide deaths data from 2017 to 2019 (Choi et al., 2023). The data set included reports from death certificates, to medical examiners, law enforcement officers, and even family and friends close to the decedents (N = 94,454, ages 18–105). They observed that suicidal ideation disclosure was highest in those aged 18–44 rather than the latter age groups spanning 45+. Much like previous research has established, disclosure of suicidality across age groups was most often to partners, family, friends, and colleagues. Despite being the lowest disclosure group, 65+ individuals disclosed their suicidality to healthcare professionals the most out of any comparison age group. Moreover, they found an interaction between grief and disclosure. Those within the 65+ age group that were grieving a family member or friend were more likely to disclose their suicidal ideation. The researchers also studied the included age groups varied propensity toward certain lethal methods. Those that went on to die by suicide either by firearms or poisoning were more likely to disclose their suicidality at some point prior to their death. In contrast, those who died by hanging and suffocation had lower rates of suicidal ideation disclosure. This is concerning considering hanging and suffocation were found to be highest within those aged 45 or younger—the age group at highest risk of dying by suicide.

As this research supports, suicide assessments should always include a discussion of plans and preparatory behaviors as certain age and method pairings are of particular concern. Building on Choi and colleagues’ findings, a study utilizing Dutch National Survey data from 2016 also found alarming rates of non-disclosure of suicidality in adults (Mérelle et al., 2018). These rates were heavily related to adult’s self-reported levels of social loneliness. This research reminds us of the many precipitating factors that interact to effect suicide risk across the lifespan and necessitates a multi-faceted suicide assessment and treatment process. We need assessments that will help clinicians to identify and target often overlooked groups of people—those who tend to not disclose.

A high level of clinical thought and care is needed when assessing children and adolescents. An immediate youth suicide risk assessment concern is the concept of conditional confidentiality. Sometimes working in the best interest of youth patients (i.e., minors) requires clinicians to break confidentiality, or notify parents of thoughts and behaviors youth have disclosed in private. Within the context of suicidality, adolescents tend to filter or withhold from reporting their experiences in fear of confidentiality breaches and the consequences (e.g., judgment and reactions) thereafter (Fox et al., 2022). This fear is exacerbated for vulnerable populations, such as those from racial and sexual minorities. One study found that adolescents from marginalized racial and gender-minoritized communities often chose to not disclose their suicide-related experiences out of fear of judgment (Shin et al., 2023). Therefore, clinicians assessing for suicide risk should remain updated on their state-level age of medical consent and ensure the universal understanding of conditional confidentiality with all key stakeholders.

It takes a tremendous amount of courage for youths to be open and honest about their suicidality—particularly because of the inherent confidentiality breach risk. Especially with younger children, clinicians may think to rectify youth’s unreliable reports by heavily weighting parent reports. However, parents similarly may be inaccurate reporters of their children’s suicide risk which can lead to an increase of “false positives.” Children may have a natural curiosity about death which may be overemphasized by parents. Parent reports are undoubtedly helpful in suicide risk assessments and can be used to validate child self-reports. Regardless, a clinician’s priority should be to conduct a developmentally appropriate and flexible assessment that validates the youth’s suicidality experiences.

Information Critical to Making a Diagnosis

It is imperative to underscore that suicide risk assessments are simply formulations of relative risk at one given point. Clinicians cannot predict future behavior, given behaviors’—especially suicidal thoughts and behaviors—instability over time (Kleiman et al., 2017). Chu et al. (2015) warn against rigid classifications (e.g., labeling individuals low, moderate, and severe risk). Rather, they recommend taking a flexible approach and staying open to the dynamic nature of suicidality.

Firstly, clinicians should aim to delineate where their patient’s suicide risk falls on the suicidality ideation to action spectrum. Suicidal ideation is often defined as thoughts of ending one’s life without the presence of any intention to act on said thoughts. Instead, suicidal intent reflects that one is in a suicidal crisis state or at a heightened risk of fatally injuring themselves. This is differentiated from lethality, or one’s tendency to select more fatal methods (e.g., firearms), since method planning could be less about one’s combined desire and intention to die and instead be greatly influenced by accessibility. Access to lethal means and planning both have a role to play in demonstrating one’s suicide risk—making them important variables to prioritize discussing within risk assessments. Usually, when patients report making plans and preparatory behaviors, they can be deemed higher on the suicide risk spectrum. Furthermore, it is vital to establish patient’s lifetime history of suicidal thoughts, behaviors (including non-suicidal self-injury), and attempts. The frequency and duration of lifetime suicidality is a predictor of future suicide attempt risk (Horwitz et al., 2015).

During formal assessments, clinicians should be mindful of and actively consider suicidality precipitants and risk factors given their potential influence over future suicidal thoughts and behavior occurrences. Common precipitants and stressors include but are not limited to one’s level of hopelessness, psychological pain, stress, agitation, irritability, impulsivity, affectivity, rumination, exposure to trauma, and sleep difficulties. Higher suicide risk can result from sociocultural intersectionality, particularly those identifying as gender diverse, racial minorities, and individuals with disabilities (Cramer et al., 2022). The Choi et al.’ (2023) study discussed above also provides a thorough overview of differential risk factors throughout the lifespan. Regardless of age, about half of the decedents within NVDRS data had mental health disorders. Depression was the most prevalent disorder followed by alcohol and substance use. The youngest age group (18–24-year-olds) of decedents had more reports of interpersonal problems.

The mid age group (25–44) had the highest reports of financial, employment, housing, and legal precipitating factors. For elderly populations, almost 50% of those who died by suicide had reports of physical health problems. The second leading stressor common to the 65+ age group was the death of a family member. Eades et al. (2019) also explored risk factors of suicidal ideation in those 60+ age group, specifically investigating personality’s influence on individual’s reported suicidality and common risk factors. Overall, two personality models—the Five Factor Model and the DSM’s Personality Disorder traits—significantly explained participants’ varied levels of suicidal ideation, thwarted belongingness, and perceived burdensomeness. Given this significant finding, the authors suggest automatically assessing for suicide risk in adults with specific personality traits. Regardless, we argue that given the high risk associated with suicidality across diagnoses, all individuals regardless of referral concern should be automatically assessed for suicide risk in mental healthcare settings. Structured and semi-structured assessments that allow eclectic but thorough discussions of current and past experiences of suicidality as well as potential risk factors and stressors are imperative for effective suicide risk formulations.

Dos and Don’ts

As clinical suicidologists, we believe suicidal suffering can be effectively decreased and suicide deaths can be prevented. By definition, lifesaving clinical suicide prevention always starts with effective and ongoing suicide risk assessment. To ensure the development of an accurate formulation of suicidal risk and a personalized treatment plan congruent with evidence-based practices, we recommend adhering to the following guidelines.

Dos

  1. 1.

    Do use validated measures.

     
  2. 2.

    Do use flexible interviews.

     
  3. 3.

    Do consider relevant diversity factors.

     
  4. 4.

    Do use explicit procedures for ensuring safety and confidentiality if possible.

     
  5. 5.

    Do ensure a safe environment: nonjudgmental, empathetic, collaborative, and validating.

     
  6. 6.

    Do conduct ongoing assessments/check-ins if possible.

     
  7. 7.

    Do use documentation: it is crucial for good clinical practice and to decrease liability.

     

Don’ts

  1. 1.

    Do not panic or prematurely assume hospitalization is always needed.

     
  2. 2.

    Do not assume there is a “one-size-fits-all” assessment: take note of each patient and pay special attention to aspects of their identity.

     
  3. 3.

    Do not assume disclosure of suicidal risk; patients may not be forthcoming and may provide subtle/indirect cues.

     
  4. 4.

    Do not treat all contexts the same: in-person clinic ≠ virtual ≠ emergency department.

     
  5. 5.

    Do not neglect your continued training and dedication to staying informed.

     

Summary

Given the significant rise in suicide rates in recent decades, evidence-based, suicide-focused assessments and interventions are crucial for reducing suffering and mitigating suicidal behavior. The process of gathering information during assessment can follow structured or semi-structured approaches—though, we advocate for an integrated approach utilizing actuarial assessments and open-ended questions examining evidence-based suicide risk factor domains. Numerous actuarial assessment measures have been developed, but those demonstrating the most robust evidence include the ASQ, C-SSRS, BSSI, PHQ-9, and SITBI, all of which are mentioned and discussed earlier in this chapter. Regardless of validated tools, suicide risk assessments are anxiety-inducing. Clinicians across all practice settings should prioritize leading with a balanced, empathetic, and patient-centered approach to ensure an accurate formulation—thereafter management—of their patients’ suicide risk. Likewise, special attention is needed when caring for vulnerable populations, such as youths and those from culturally and racially diverse backgrounds, given their well-documented rising suicide rates. Some key elements in assessing for suicide risk are to gather where an individual falls on the ideation-to-action pathway, taking into consideration lifetime history of SITBs, stressors specific to their age, and the influence of sociocultural factors. Most suicide risk assessments are a single snapshot of an individual’s suicide risk at a given time. Rather than prioritizing rigid classifications and measurement tools, clinicians are recommended to take a flexible yet consistent approach to accurately capture an individual’s suicidality and its dynamic nature.
