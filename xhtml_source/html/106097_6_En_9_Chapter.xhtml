Description of the Disorders

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a psychiatric condition that involves obsessions (i.e., recurring, unwanted thoughts, images, or impulses that lead to anxiety/distress) and compulsions (i.e., repetitive behaviors or mental acts performed to reduce anxiety/distress related to obsessions; American Psychiatric Association [APA], 2022). Obsessive-compulsive symptoms are often time-consuming and associated with significant distress and functional impairment in familial, social, educational, and occupational domains. The lifetime prevalence of OCD is approximately 1–3%, with most individuals also diagnosed with comorbid disorders (Ruscio et al., 2010). The presentation of OCD symptoms can vary widely among individuals, but there are four common symptom dimensions including contamination, responsibility for harm, unacceptable/taboo thoughts, and symmetry or “just right-ness” (Abramowitz et al., 2010; Table 9.1). Although symptom dimensions are not specified in an OCD diagnosis, understanding these OCD themes can help providers diagnose and treat people with the condition. Contamination

Contamination OCD fears involve intrusive thoughts about being contaminated due to touching, or being close to, certain objects, people, or situations, such as doorknobs, individuals with varied illnesses, and bathrooms. Approximately, 46% of patients with OCD have contamination fears that are often associated with becoming sick or spreading germs to others (Markarian et al., 2010). Contamination fears can also lead to feelings of disgust and beliefs that they may never feel clean. Another common concern is cross-contamination, a phenomenon in which contaminants spread or transfer from the initial contaminated object (e.g., spoon) to additional objects (e.g., bowl, plate). Individuals with contamination OCD engage in excessive cleaning/washing rituals, including washing hands, showering, and sanitizing, which can lead to skin irritation or bleeding. Additionally, avoidance of contaminated triggers is common, as is the use of barriers (e.g., paper towels) when handling contaminated objects. Patients may engage in ritualized washing habits, in which they clean their body or objects for a certain amount of time or number of sets until they feel the contaminated area is clean. Reassurance-seeking (e.g., asking if an area is clean) and accommodation (e.g., mother helping child with OCD clean a contaminated area) are also common. Finally, these symptoms can lead to economic burden, as patients may buy excessive amounts of cleaning material or discard benign objects that are perceived as contaminated.

Responsibility for Harm

Also referred to as “harm OCD” or “responsibility OCD,” this domain of OCD is characterized by an inflated sense of personal responsibility for harm to oneself or others. Specific harm/responsibility obsessions include fears of harming oneself or others due to carelessness (e.g., hitting a pedestrian due to being inattentive, providing incorrect information to someone that leads to negative outcomes), fears of harming oneself or others by acting on an unwanted impulse (e.g., pushing a family member in front of a car, stabbing a pet), or fears of being responsible for an untoward event (e.g., fire, burglary, house flood, divorce, death).

Common compulsions associated with harm/responsibility OCD include checking and reassurance-seeking. These compulsions are typically related to the content of the obsessions. For instance, a patient who fears they have run someone over with their car may return to a particular intersection repeatedly to check that no one there is injured or dead. A patient who fears being responsible for a house fire or house flood may repeatedly check that their stoves are turned off, appliances unplugged, and faucets tightly shut. Reassurance-seeking compulsions include repeatedly calling or texting a significant other to confirm their safety, or asking a family member for reassurance that the patient has not harmed someone. Compulsions do not necessarily have to be directly or logically related to the content of the obsessions, however. For example, a patient may develop a ritual (e.g., mentally reciting a phrase, tapping in a specific manner) that decreases their perceived likelihood of a bad event happening. Also common are avoidance behaviors, such as avoiding handling potentially dangerous objects (e.g., sharp objects, vehicles, machinery), avoiding contact with people or living things that could be harmed, or avoiding talking to or writing to others out of fears of communicating incorrect information.

Symptoms of harm/responsibility OCD can intersect with those of other OCD presentations. For example, a patient who also has contamination OCD may fear that they will harm others by spreading germs or contaminants; a patient with unacceptable thoughts/taboo OCD may fear that they will act on an aggressive impulse to harm a loved one; or a patient with scrupulosity OCD may feel the need to confess a perceived misdeed in painstaking detail to prevent their family members from going to Hell.

Unacceptable/Taboo Thoughts

Within the unacceptable/taboo subtype, individuals often report distress due to intrusive thoughts or images related to sex/sexuality, scrupulosity (i.e., immoral thoughts), or sexual violence. These thoughts are ego-dystonic (i.e., are inconsistent with the individual’s perception of their personality or values), which contribute to feelings of significant distress, stigma, or shame. This differs from ego-syntonic thoughts that feel consistent with the individual’s personality, values, feelings, and self-view. For instance, individuals with obsessive-compulsive personality disorder or eating disorders (i.e., anorexia nervosa) are more likely to experience ego-syntonic thoughts. Awareness of this discrepancy between ego-syntonic and ego-dystonic thoughts is critical when working with individuals with unacceptable/taboo OCD thoughts, who may carry stigma or shame due to having received negative feedback from peers or clinicians who mistook their intrusive thoughts for true intention or desire to act on those thoughts. The sections below describe the most common categories of unacceptable/taboo thoughts.

Sexual thoughts

Individuals who experience sexually themed obsessions report fears of having a desire to engage in sex or intimate actions with children, family members, or strangers in the community. These intrusive thoughts or images lead to compulsions, including excessive reassurance-seeking or confessing, engaging in self-reassurance/mental rituals (i.e., justifying, reviewing situations that occurred), neutralizing (i.e., canceling out thoughts), and checking for arousal within their body. One of the most common compulsions for this subtype is avoidance. For instance, an individual may avoid certain locations where children are frequently present (i.e., parks, swimming pools, schools) or physical touch with family members or friends (i.e., hugging, handshakes). An individual may also avoid having sexual contact with their partner or hugging a family member, which may result in feelings of confusion or tension in interpersonal relationships.

Another aspect of this subtype is the fear of being sexually attracted to someone that is not in line with the person’s beliefs about their sexuality. For example, a male who identifies as heterosexual may experience intrusive sexual thoughts or images about other men, resulting in fears of being gay. In addition, a female who identifies as bisexual may experience intrusive thoughts that they are “lying” to themselves and others, leading to uncertainty about whether they are interested in both women and men, despite acknowledging that they are attracted to both genders.

Scrupulosity

This obsessive-compulsive dimension consists of fears about committing sacrilege/blasphemy, fears about not going to heaven or an afterlife, fears about not truly being a “believer” or having faith, or fears about sinning because of having sexually intrusive thoughts. These concerns are highly distressing and surpass what is typical for their peers within the same religious denomination. As a result of these intrusive thoughts, compulsions are typically centered around excessive involvement in—or avoidance of—behaviors associated with their religion. Common compulsions include excessively praying, frequently attending church, excessively reading scriptures or spreading the gospel to others, seeking reassurance (e.g., consulting with church leaders about salvation or whether they are moral or a “good person,” asking family members excessive questions, engaging in repeated baptisms), and avoiding satanic content (e.g., sections of the bible mentioning Satan, the numbers “666,” movies/media showcasing the devil). Avoiding religious practices as a result of intrusive thoughts can lead to strong feelings of sadness or loss of connection within one’s desired community.

Violent Thoughts

Violent intrusive thoughts or obsessions are often related to an unwanted fear of harming another person, such as sexually assaulting a child or adult, experiencing unwanted thoughts of dead corpses, thoughts related to stabbing or shooting someone, or intrusive images/thoughts of wanting to hurt themselves (i.e., gun, knifes, rope, shower curtain). As a result of these intrusive thoughts or images, individuals will often engage in avoidance so that the feared outcome does not occur. For example, they may avoid kitchens due to knives, avoid the home of a friend who has guns, or avoid areas at stores that have household appliances. Furthermore, these obsessions can lead to compulsions of confessing, reassurance-seeking, and mental rituals (e.g., mentally reviewing situations or interactions to ensure they did no harm).

Symmetry or “Just Right-ness”

Another feature of OCD is concerns related to symmetry and wanting things to feel “just right.” Although individuals with this type of OCD may endorse magical thinking (e.g., “my dad will die if I do not make my bed perfectly”), which lead to feelings of anxiety, it is more common for them to experience feelings of incompleteness or discomfort that are alleviated by symmetry, ordering, repeating, or counting rituals (Vellozo et al., 2021). Examples of these rituals include adjusting clothes hung in the closet to be perfectly spaced apart, touching a desk repeatedly until feelings of distress are reduced, and repeating a phrase or rewriting a sentence until it feels “just right.” Patients with these symptoms may present with attenuated insight, which may explain why they are less likely to seek treatment than individuals with other dimensions of OCD (Elvish et al., 2010). They are also more likely to have obsessive-compulsive and related (OC-related) conditions, such as tic disorders and skin picking (Mayerovitch et al., 2003).

OC-Related Disorders

In the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition (DSM-5-TR), OCD is no longer classified as an anxiety disorder as it was in the DSM-IV. It is instead now included in a new diagnostic category titled obsessive-compulsive and related disorders, which consists of OCD and conditions that are distinct from, but similar to, OCD. In this section, we provide an overview of OC-related disorders, including body dysmorphic disorder (BDD), hoarding disorder, trichotillomania (TTM), and excoriation disorder, with an emphasis on how the conditions can be differentiated from OCD. Since there are many similarities in the diagnostic procedures among OCD and these related conditions, the other sections of this chapter will primarily focus on OCD. There are a number of resources for evidence-based information on diagnosing these conditions (Phillips & Stein, 2015). See Table 9.2 for common assessment tools for OC-related disorders.

Body Dysmorphic Disorder

Individuals with BDD have excessive and persistent concerns related to perceived flaws in physical appearance that appear minimal or non-existent to others (Jassi & Krebs, 2023). Facial features (e.g., nose, eyes, hair) are the most common areas of preoccupation, but worries can center on physical features of any body part. Similar to OCD, those with BDD engage in repetitive behaviors, such as checking, grooming, and camouflaging, to attempt to correct or conceal the perceived flaw. Avoidance of social situations and mental rituals (e.g., comparing their physical features to others) is also common. BDD is distinct from OCD because rituals are narrowly focused on attempts to correct perceived problems in appearance and not on other obsessions/compulsions related to common OCD content themes (e.g., contamination, responsibility for harm). Patients with BDD typically have poor insight and are convinced that there is an actual flaw in their physical appearance. The DSM-5-TR includes insight as a specifier for BDD, and studies demonstrate that insight can improve with treatment (Harrison et al., 2016). Additionally, BDD is underdiagnosed because individuals with the condition may not disclose concerns related to appearance due to embarrassment, individuals tend to seek help from dermatologists and cosmetic surgeons before seeking mental health treatment, and individuals are misdiagnosed with other psychiatric conditions (e.g., social anxiety disorder, OCD). Finally, BDD is associated with higher rates of suicide attempts, as individuals with BDD are 2.6 times more likely to engage in suicide attempts compared to those without BDD (Angelakis et al., 2016).

Hoarding Disorder

Hoarding disorder is characterized by persistent difficulty with discarding or parting with possessions regardless of their actual value. This difficulty is due to a perceived need to hold on to these items and distress related to discarding them. This causes the individual to accumulate possessions to the point that the resulting clutter renders living spaces no longer usable for their intended purpose. To meet DSM-5-TR criteria for hoarding disorder, this accumulation of possessions must cause clinically significant distress or functional impairment, not be attributable to another medical condition, and not be better explained by another mental disorder. The DSM-5-TR notes that an individual with hoarding disorder may have uncluttered living spaces only with intervention from third parties (e.g., family members, cleaners, authorities). The patient’s level of insight is indicated with one of the following specifiers: with good or fair insight, poor insight, or absent or delusional insight.

If a clinician notes the presence of hoarding behaviors, a thorough assessment of symptoms is necessary to differentiate between hoarding disorder, normative collecting, and potential differential diagnoses. In contrast to hoarding, collecting is a widespread, normative behavior characterized by focused, organized, and pleasurable acquisition of a specific category of items, and is rarely associated with social or occupational impairment. Hoarding behaviors can be the consequence of other psychological or medical conditions, in which case hoarding disorder should not be diagnosed. For example, if a patient hoards items due to fears that something bad will happen if they discard them (e.g., throwing away a gift will place bad luck on the gift giver; throwing away an old memento will cause the patient to lose a part of their identity), but does not hoard items for other reasons, then a diagnosis of OCD is more appropriate. Clinicians are reminded, however, that a patient can concurrently have both hoarding disorder and a comorbid diagnosis independent of one another.

The most comprehensive assessment for hoarding disorder is the Structured Interview for Hoarding Disorder (Nordsletten et al., 2013)—a semi-structured, clinician-administered interview that evaluates all six DSM-5-TR criteria for hoarding disorder. This assessment also includes risk assessment associated with hoarding behaviors (e.g., fire hazards, unhygienic conditions) and questions to facilitate differential diagnoses (e.g., autism spectrum disorder, OCD). Ideally, assessment should include direct observation of the individual’s living spaces and information gathered from collateral sources (e.g., family members), as patients with hoarding disorder often have low insight into their condition. If it is not possible to directly observe the individual’s living spaces, a standardized tool such as the Clutter Image Rating (Frost et al., 2008) can be used. It is also important that assessment includes a thorough evaluation of medical history and psychopathology to rule out other potential causes.

Trichotillomania

Trichotillomania (TTM; also referred to as hair-pulling disorder) involves recurrent pulling of one’s hair that leads to noticeable hair loss, accompanied by repeated, unsuccessful attempts to stop pulling. Common pulling sites include the scalp, eyebrows, and eyelashes, and most individuals report pulling from more than one area. Many individuals with TTM experience a building sense of tension near the hair-pulling site prior to pulling, followed by a decrease in tension after the pulling episode, which becomes reinforcing. TTM is also associated with significant impairment in social, academic, and occupational domains (Woods et al., 2006). Additionally, environmental variables, such as sensory stimulation (e.g., touching hair), emotional states (e.g., anxiety, boredom), and thoughts (e.g., “I can’t control my hair pulling”), are linked to pulling episodes. Studies have identified “focused” and “automatic” pulling as the two subtypes of hair pulling (Duke et al., 2009). Focused pulling includes purposeful hair pulling that is triggered by an intense urge, thought, or emotion, while automatic pulling involves a lack of awareness of the pulling episode, typically during sedentary activities, such as watching television or driving. Although hair pulling can occur in other disorders, such as OCD and BDD, clinicians can differentiate the diagnoses by examining the reason for pulling. For instance, individuals with TTM pull hair in response to an ego-syntonic urge or desire to pull, while a hair-pulling ritual in those with OCD may occur in response to distressing ego-dystonic thoughts. Further, pulling hair with intent to fix a perceived flaw in appearance that is not noticeable to others is more closely aligned with a diagnosis of BDD than TTM.

Excoriation Disorder

Excoriation disorder, otherwise known as skin picking disorder or dermatillomania, is the act of repetitive picking at healthy areas of skin that causes injury (i.e., bleeding, scarring, scabbing). To receive a diagnosis of excoriation disorder, an individual must cause injury or skin lesions to otherwise heathy skin by the act of picking. An individual must also experience feelings of distress or impairment in their daily functioning. Skin picking often begins during childhood and early adolescence and continues into adulthood. Common skin-picking areas include the face, arms, and hands. Typically, individuals pick with their fingernails; however, tweezers, pins, or other tools can also be used to remove skin. Skin picking is often provoked by emotional, situational, perceptual, tactile, and environmental triggers. For example, an individual may experience an urge to pick due to a situational trigger, such as driving or watching television. Additionally, individuals may experience an urge to pick due to a tactile trigger, such as sensing imperfections on the skin. Emotional triggers, such as feelings of anxiety, sadness, or anger, can also increase the likelihood of skin picking. Individuals will often describe feelings of adrenaline, decreased pain, and being in a “trancelike state” during a picking episode. However, following the skin-picking episode, they often report feelings of guilt, shame, embarrassment, and loss of control. This may result in urges to pick again, thus creating a cycle of skin picking.

Within excoriation disorder, the two frequent skin-picking subtypes that have emerged include emotional/reward pickers and functional pickers. Emotional/reward skin pickers are defined as individuals who endorse strong and frequent urges to pick, experience negative emotions before picking, engage in automatic picking, and report little control over picking behaviors. Functional pickers demonstrate milder symptoms of skin-picking behaviors, have less intense urges to pick, and report less distress/impairment related to skin picking.

Procedures for Gathering Information

Structured, evidence-based interviews and assessment tools are the gold standard for gathering information on symptom presentation to diagnose OCD; however, diagnostic interviewing procedures will vary depending on the purpose of the diagnosis, clinical setting, session time restrictions, and clinician preferences. For research studies, accurate participant diagnoses are extremely important; therefore, structured assessment tools should be used. In clinical settings, there may be barriers (e.g., time restrictions) that do not allow for a structured assessment, but unstructured interviews can be completed based on these tools. Recommendations for Formal Assessment

Clinician-Administered Interviews

Structured diagnostic interviews—such as the Anxiety Disorders Interview Schedule-5 (ADIS-5; Brown & Barlow, 2014), the Structured Clinical Interview for DSM-5 (First et al., 2015), Structured Diagnostic Interview for DSM-5 Anxiety, Mood, and Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Neuropsychiatric Disorders (DIAMOND; Tolin et al., 2018), and the Mini International Neuropsychiatric Interview (MINI; Sheehan et al., 1998)—contain OCD modules that allow clinicians to obtain an accurate diagnosis of OCD according to DSM-5 criteria. For children and adolescents, the ADIS-5-Child/Parent (Silverman & Albano, 2024), Schedule for Affective Disorders and Schizophrenia for School-Age Children-Present and Lifetime Version (K-SADS-PL; Kaufman et al., 1997), Structured Diagnostic Interview for DSM-5 Anxiety, Mood, and Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Neuropsychiatric Disorders in Children and Adolescents (DIAMOND-KID; Tolin et al., 2023), and MINI-Kid (Sheehan, 2016) are available.

The Yale-Brown Obsessive-Compulsive Scale (Y-BOCS; Goodman et al., 1989) is the gold standard instrument for assessing the presence and severity of OCD symptoms in adults. The Y-BOCS is a semi-structured, clinician-administered interview that contains two parts. The Symptom Checklist assesses the presence of a wide range of obsessions and compulsions, while the Severity Scale consists of 10 questions assessing the severity of obsessions and compulsions, each of which the clinician rates on a scale of 1–4 depending on the patient’s responses. If a patient is suspected to be under- or overreporting symptoms, the clinician uses clinical judgment to rate the item. The Children’s Y-BOCS (CY-BOCS; Scahill et al., 1997) is the analogous measure for children ages 6–17 years. It is recommended that both the child and their caregiver(s) are interviewed together, as this allows clinicians to obtain the most accurate clinical picture, although clinician judgment should dictate this. Second editions of the Y-BOCS (Y-BOCS-II; Storch et al., 2010) and CY-BOCS (CY-BOCS-II; Storch et al., 2019) have also been created to modify the severity scale item content and scoring, add avoidance into the severity scale item scoring, and expand the symptom checklist.

Clinician-Rated Measures

The Dimensional Y-BOCS (Rosario-Campos et al., 2006) combines a patient self-report questionnaire with clinician ratings of symptom severity. Based on the patient’s questionnaire responses and the clinician’s observations while reviewing these with the patient, the clinician rates dimensional symptom severity, global symptom severity, and global impairment. The National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH) Global Obsessive Compulsive Scale (GOCS) is a single-item, clinician-rated measure of OCD symptom severity.

Self-Reported Measures for Adults

Self-reported measures of OCD for adults have been used as screening tools, as well as tools to gather additional information that may not be available via clinician-rated measures. These include the Y-BOCS-Self-Report (Storch et al., 2017), the Dimensional Obsessive-Compulsive Scale (DOCS; Abramowitz et al., 2010), the Obsessive-Compulsive Inventory-Revised (OCI-R; Foa et al., 2002), and the Florida Obsessive-Compulsive Inventory (FOCI; Storch et al., 2007). In recent years, shorter versions of the OCI-R have been developed and tested with good psychometric findings (Abramovitch et al., 2021a, 2021b), providing promise as brief screening tools.

Child- and Parent-Reported Measures

For children and adolescents under the age of 18 years, the following child-report and parent-report measures are appropriate for assessing OCD symptom severity. The CY-BOCS-Child-Report and -Parent-Report (Storch et al., 2006) are each 10-item questionnaires derived directly from the CY-BOCS. The OCI-Child Version (OCI-CV; Foa et al., 2010) is a child-reported version of the OCI, which assesses symptom severity across six domains including hoarding. In light of literature suggesting that hoarding is separate from OCD, researchers developed the OCI-CV-Revised (Abramovitch et al., 2022a), which removed the three hoarding items from the OCI-CV. The Children’s FOCI (Storch et al., 2009) is the child-report version of the FOCI, which, like the FOCI, boasts a brief administration time of 5–10 min. The Child Obsessive-Compulsive Impact Scale (Piacentini et al., 2007) assesses parent- and child-perceived functional impairment caused by OCD across developmentally relevant domains of life.

Measures of OCD-Related Factors

Additional factors related to OCD, such as level of patient insight and family accommodation, have implications for treatment response. The Brown Assessment of Beliefs Scale (BABS; Eisen et al., 1998) is a semi-structured clinician-administered interview that assesses a patient’s level of insight into delusional beliefs. Although it was developed for a range of disorders including psychosis, it is widely used to assess insight in patients with OCD. Family accommodation, which refers to family behaviors that are intended to reduce patient’s OCD-related distress, but fuel OCD symptoms in the long term (e.g., taking on patients’ responsibilities so they can avoid OCD triggers), is also highly relevant to the assessment and treatment of OCD. Data can be gathered via clinician-administered interviews (Calvocoressi et al., 1999), parent-report on parental accommodation of child OCD (Flessner et al., 2011), family member self-report on their accommodation of a patient’s OCD (Pinto et al., 2013), and patient self-report on perceived family accommodation (Wu et al., 2016).

Case Illustration

The following case illustration shows an example of a hypothetical unstructured clinical interview of a clinician meeting with a patient for the first time. The patient is a woman presenting with harm-related OCD. The interview highlights how to assess the presence and severity of OCD, help a patient feel comfortable disclosing behaviors/thoughts that they perceive as odd or embarrassing, and gather information to distinguish between different diagnoses.

Clinician: What brings you in today?

Susan: I have been really keyed up lately, especially at night before I go to bed. I have so many thoughts and I can’t stop them or get them out of my head.

Clinician: That sounds challenging. Can you tell me a bit more about these thoughts?

Susan: I just worry a lot about my family. These worries run through my mind throughout the day, but they get much worse before I go to bed, and it’s getting in the way of my sleep.

Clinician: Concern for family members can be extremely upsetting. What do you worry about related to your family?

Susan: The worries don’t really make sense. I’m actually a little hesitant to tell you about them because I don’t want you to think I’m crazy.

Clinician: I understand how hard it can be to talk about these distressing thoughts, especially with someone you just met. I want to assure you that I have worked with many patients with anxiety, and there are no thoughts that can surprise or shock me. Everyone has odd thoughts from time to time, and that doesn’t mean you’re crazy. Learning more about your specific thoughts will help us develop the most effective treatment plan for you.

Susan: Thanks for letting me know that, but it’s not just the thoughts that I’m anxious to tell you about. There are things I do throughout the day because of these thoughts that are not normal, and I don’t know anyone else who does them. Sometimes my family gets annoyed at me for it, too.

Clinician: Do you do these things throughout the day to help get rid of your anxiety related to the thought?

Susan: Yes, but it only helps for a little bit and then the thought comes up again.

Clinician: What’s an example of one of these behaviors?

Susan: Before I go to bed, I check to make sure the stove is shut off and all the doors in the house are locked, but I don’t just do it once. I’ll check each stove and lock at least four to five times, sometimes even more. Sometimes I spend almost a half hour repeating these checks, and on most nights, I get out of bed multiple times to check again.

Clinician: What are you worried will happen if you don’t do these checks?

Susan: I fear that someone will come into my house and take my children. I check to make sure the back door next to my children’s bedrooms is locked the most. I also worry that it will be my fault that there is a house fire.

Clinician: You mentioned these thoughts come up all the time. Do you always check the locks and the stove when these thoughts come up?

Susan: Most of the time! But when I can’t do the check, I will have my husband check for me. Like when I was in the clinic waiting room just earlier, I called my husband and asked him to check to make sure I shut the stove off before I left this morning. He gets mad at me because I ask him to do this almost every day.

Clinician: I see… that sounds very time-consuming and stressful to feel the need to do these checks all the time. When you have unwanted and upsetting thoughts about bad things happening to your family, you engage in checking behaviors, which give you temporary relief, only for those thoughts and urges to come right back again. These symptoms you’re describing are very similar to experiences of people with obsessive-compulsive disorder. Have you heard of obsessive-compulsive disorder or OCD?

Susan: Yes, I’ve heard of OCD, but I thought people with OCD usually wash their hands a lot and are scared of germs. I’m not like that at all.

Clinician: You’re right–people with OCD can have fears related to germs, but the disorder is actually much more than that. People with OCD can present with many different thoughts and behaviors. Would it be okay if I told you a bit more about OCD and some other common symptoms that are related to the disorder?

Susan: Sure.

Clinician: Great, I have a list of common thoughts that sometimes come up for people with OCD. Let me know if you ever have these thoughts.

Next, the clinician briefly provides psychoeducation on obsessions and compulsions and could administer the Y-BOCS symptom checklist to identify any other OCD symptoms that Susan experiences. At the beginning of an unstructured clinical interview, the patient is typically asked a broad question about their presenting concern. To determine if a patient has OCD, a clinician should focus on asking questions that can help clarify whether the content of the thoughts and purpose of the behaviors are characteristics of obsessions/compulsions or related to other similar diagnoses, such as generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), negative thinking common in depressive disorders, or behaviors associated with other OC-related disorders like trichotillomania and skin picking.

Susan initially reported that she worries a lot about her family during the day, which could be a symptom of several different disorders. The clinician probed about the thought content, but Susan was hesitant to tell them about her thoughts because she feared the clinician would think she is “crazy.” It is common for individuals with OCD to worry that others will think they’re “crazy” or “weird” because of the content of their intrusive thoughts. The clinician normalized these concerns for the patient, which also led her to disclose that she is embarrassed by the behaviors she engages in to reduce her fears related to the thoughts. This was the first indication that these thoughts and behaviors may be OCD symptoms.

It is also helpful to point out to patients that all people have intrusive thoughts from time to time to again help normalize these unwanted, distressing thoughts. Additionally, using the Y-BOCS symptom checklist can give the clinician a clearer diagnostic picture of symptoms that the patient may not realize are related to OCD. Using this assessment can also show the patient that she is not the only person with these thoughts/behaviors. Further, Susan told the clinician that she frequently seeks reassurance from her husband about the stove. There are probably many other ways Susan seeks reassurance from her husband and other people, and the clinician should specifically ask questions about this during the clinical interview.

Impact of Cultural Variables

Gender

The prevalence of OCD is similar for both females and males; however, there are some gender differences in the onset of symptoms, comorbid diagnoses, and subtype or presentation of OCD symptoms. For instance, males have a greater likelihood of symptom onset in childhood perhaps linked to co-occurrence of Tourette or autism, while OCD is more common for females in adolescence and adulthood (Mathes et al., 2019). Additionally, males are more likely to present with comorbid tic disorders and females are more likely to present with comorbid excoriation disorder or eating disorders (Mathis et al., 2011). Furthermore, females are more likely to present with contamination subtype, whereas males are more likely to present with sexual and symmetry-related obsessions. There are inconclusive findings for gender differences with harm-related obsessions, as many studies report mixed results. There are no reported gender differences in treatment outcomes (for reviews, see Mathis et al., 2011; Mathes et al., 2019). Considering these findings, it is important that clinicians make note of common presenting subtypes for each gender when assessing and evaluating individuals for OCD.

Age

Despite OCD onset being most common in adolescence (mean age of onset = 16.9 years; Brakoulias et al., 2017), intrusive thoughts and compulsions can appear during early childhood between ages 5 and 7 (Garcia et al., 2009). Although older adults may present with OCD symptoms, the onset of their symptoms is likely to have started in childhood/young adulthood and progressed as they aged (Grenier et al., 2009).

Race and Ethnicity

Ethnically/racially minoritized groups are often unrepresented and understudied in OCD research, which increases the risk of misdiagnosis and cultural insensitivity in clinical practice. According to Williams et al. (2020), clinicians who are unfamiliar with different cultural backgrounds may inadvertently convey through their words or actions disrespect, insensitivity, or discrimination. For instance, clinicians may hold stereotypes about how people of color cope and what types of presenting concerns they report, resulting in misdiagnosis or avoidance of discussion of OCD symptoms when clinically indicated (Williams et al., 2020). Furthermore, African Americans with OCD are more likely to be misdiagnosed with other disorders, such as psychosis, as a result of culturally specific explanations for their symptomology. Additionally, Latinos continue to be underrepresented in clinical trials for OCD, which may be due to a reluctance to participate in psychological treatment and/or research. Latinos may be hesitant to seek help for their OCD symptoms due to cultural stigma, attribution of symptoms to somatic reasons, socioeconomic barriers, or fears that clinicians may misunderstand their concerns due to limited education on cultural norms/differences (Wetterneck et al., 2012).

Given that minoritized populations are less likely to seek treatment than their White counterparts, it is important that mental health providers disseminate psychoeducation in these communities to decrease stigma and provide an accurate description of what symptoms are consistent with an OCD diagnosis. Mental health providers can adapt their recruitment efforts to ensure minoritized communities are able to receive accurate diagnostic assessment and treatment, such as having fliers for psychological services in various languages, providing affordable assessment and treatment options (i.e., financial compensation with clinical trials, sliding scale option), and having flexible appointment slots that accommodate families’ work schedules (Wetterneck et al., 2012). Multiple marginalized identities (i.e., race, gender, sexuality) can also lead to greater OCD symptom severity, as individuals may experience greater symptoms of depression and lower quality of life during treatment and after discharge compared to their more privileged counterparts (Wadsworth et al., 2020). Throughout the diagnostic process, it is important for clinicians to be mindful of patients’ ethnic/racial and intersecting identities.

Religion and Spirituality

Religious and spiritual backgrounds can shape the presentation of certain domains of OCD symptoms. For individuals who experience obsessions or compulsions related to their religious or spiritual beliefs, intrusive thoughts can be associated with specific customs of their religion, such as scripture or beliefs regarding the afterlife. For example, an individual who identifies as Muslim and is from an Islam-based faith may engage in contamination-related compulsions in addition to washing rituals during prayer that other Muslims follow. When assessing children experiencing OCD related to religious thoughts, it can be especially helpful to gather information from their parents to distinguish normative practices within their faith community from obsessions and compulsions. For example, a child from a Christian faith may have the urge to compulsively pray outside of what is typical for the family or what is suggested at church.

As an individual’s religion or faith can be an influential part of their identity, they may wish to enlist support from religious leaders or healers within their community. There can be a risk for mental health providers to attempt to control or suppress an individual’s religious or spiritual beliefs if they perceive that the religion is at the root of the individual’s intrusive thoughts or is exacerbating OCD symptoms (Williams et al., 2020). It is important that clinicians assess and treat OCD while being mindful and respectful of the confines of the individual’s religious beliefs/traditions. When necessary, it can be helpful for clinicians to defer to the client’s religious leaders or healers to identify normative practices for the religion. Although it is not required for clinicians to be experts in specific religions for successful OCD treatment, increasing knowledge on basic customs and traditions within the religion can increase rapport and assist with differentiating normative cultural/religious practices from compulsions (Williams et al., 2020).

Information Critical to Making a Diagnosis

A diagnosis of OCD requires an individual to meet all four DSM-5-TR criteria (Criteria A through D). The DSM-5-TR also includes specifiers for OCD that indicate the patient’s level of insight and whether the patient’s OCD is tic-related.

Criterion A

To meet Criterion A, the individual must demonstrate the presence of obsessions, compulsions, or both. Obsessions are thoughts, images, or impulses that are (a) repeated and persistent (i.e., difficult to get rid of); (b) experienced as unwanted or intrusive (i.e., ego dystonic); and (c) cause anxiety or distress. Compulsions are repetitive behaviors (whether observable behaviors or mental acts) that the patient feels compelled to perform in response to an obsession, or in accordance with rigid rules. Compulsions are intended to neutralize the obsession, decrease anxiety caused by the obsession, or prevent a feared outcome from occurring. Compulsions are either not realistically related to the outcome or are clearly excessive or out of proportion to the situation.

Criterion B

To meet Criterion B, the individual’s obsessions or compulsions must be time-consuming, cause clinically significant distress, or cause clinically significant functional impairment. The DSM-5-TR defines “time-consuming” as taking more than 1 h a day. It is important that clinicians carefully assess how much total time is spent on obsessions or compulsions per day by asking about both duration and frequency. Some obsessions and compulsions occur briefly (i.e., a few seconds) but frequently throughout the day (i.e., every few minutes), such that all the time may add up to more than the patient initially realizes. Functional impairment is commonly observed in social and occupational settings. In the case of children and adolescents, functional impairment is also seen in school settings. In addition, clinicians should assess for functional impairment in activities of daily living (e.g., hygiene) and impairments to home life (e.g., family conflict).

Criterion C

Criterion C requires that the individual’s obsessive-compulsive symptoms are not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance (e.g., drug, medication), substance withdrawal, or another medical condition. For example, acute and severe onset of OCD in children has been observed following streptococcal infection. As another example, OCD symptoms may be induced by stimulant medication. If organic causes are suspected, a medical evaluation is recommended.

Criterion D

Criterion D requires that the individual’s disturbance is not better explained by the symptoms of another mental disorder. Differential diagnosis is an important part of the diagnostic process. Clinicians should be familiar with how other mental disorders—especially those characterized by avoidant or compulsive behaviors—present similarly to OCD, and how OCD differs from these disorders. For example, a patient with social anxiety disorder may avoid speaking to others out of fear of judgment or check individual’s responses to them excessively, whereas a patient with OCD may avoid speaking to others out of fear of communicating incorrect information that causes harm. A patient with an eating disorder may eat in a ritualistic manner to avoid gaining weight, whereas a patient with OCD may eat in a ritualistic manner to prevent their family members becoming ill. A patient with autism may repeat a motor movement to self-stimulate, whereas a patient with OCD may repeat a movement until it feels “just right.” When assessing potential differential diagnoses, clinicians should pay particular attention to the emotional impact of the obsession (e.g., is it distressing or enjoyable? Is it congruent or incongruent with their beliefs?) and the function of the compulsive behavior (e.g., to provide relief from anxiety, or to achieve another purpose? Is it linked to an obsession?).

Insight Specifier

The DSM-IV used to have only one specifier, “with poor insight.” The DSM-5 added new insight specifiers to reflect the range of insight possible in OCD. An individual with good or fair insight recognizes that their OCD beliefs may not be true. An individual with poor insight thinks their OCD beliefs are probably true. An individual with absent insight/delusional beliefs is fully convinced that their OCD beliefs are true. When assessing for insight, clinicians may ask questions such as, “What do you think would happen if you did not perform the compulsion?”, “How likely is it that the feared outcome would occur?”, “What do other people (in your faith community, in your family, in your friend group) do in this situation, and if it differs from you, how come?”. Aforementioned assessments, such as the BABS, can also be a helpful tool to assess insight.

Tic-Related Specifier

The tic-related specifier is given if the individual has a current or past history of a tic disorder. The DSM-5 added the tic-related specifier in response to a growing literature showing the link between OCD and tic disorder. Indeed, when defining tic status broadly (i.e., presence of motor and/or vocal tics), over 50% of youth met the criteria for the tic-related specifier. However, youth with tic-related OCD did not differ from youth with non-tic-related OCD in terms of OCD severity, related impairment, comorbidity, or treatment response (Conelea et al., 2014). Therefore, clinicians should be mindful to not make assumptions about a patient on the basis of the tic-related specifier.

Dos and Don’ts

See Table 9.4 for a suggested list of dos and don’ts when interviewing and assessing for OCD and related disorders. In general, it is crucial that clinicians create an emotionally safe environment to ensure they are gathering accurate information about the patient’s OCD symptoms. Depending on a patient’s OCD subtype and severity of symptoms, perceived stigma and feelings of shame can prohibit them from fully disclosing their symptoms. Normalizing the patient’s experiences in a non-judgmental manner, as demonstrated in the case illustration, will not only facilitate disclosure but also increase patient’s self-acceptance and promote therapeutic rapport. Furthermore, to gather accurate information when interviewing and assessing for OCD, there are four fundamental elements to keep in mind: knowing what to ask (i.e., using standardized/evidenced-based measures and clinical experience), who to ask (i.e., gathering collateral information, when necessary, from others in the individual’s life), how to ensure accurate information is being communicated by the individual (i.e., provide OCD psychoeducation defining obsessions and compulsions), and how to ensure assessment is occurring from a lens of cultural sensitivity (i.e., ensuring one is not misconstruing cultural practices or beliefs as OCD symptomology). Above all else, showing empathy and compassion for patients is the most essential element when interviewing those with OCD and related disorders.

Summary

OCD and OC-related disorders are psychiatric conditions that lead to significant distress and functional impairment in multiple domains. In clinical practice and healthcare settings, it is important that providers accurately diagnose these conditions to ensure that patients receive adequate care. There are several challenges associated with diagnosing these conditions, including diverse presentation of symptoms, symptom overlap, comorbid conditions, and low patient insight. It is critical that providers use evidence-based assessment tools when conducting diagnostic interviews with these patients. This chapter provides information on common clinician-administered interviews, as well as other assessment measures that can assist clinicians with gathering clinical information. Further, a case illustration demonstrates how an unstructured clinical interview can be guided by structured assessment tools to help diagnose OCD. We also highlight important diversity factors to consider, along with dos and don’ts when interviewing and assessing for OCD and related disorders. Overall, this chapter can be used as a resource for providers across different settings to help them obtain an accurate and informed diagnosis of OCD and related disorders.
