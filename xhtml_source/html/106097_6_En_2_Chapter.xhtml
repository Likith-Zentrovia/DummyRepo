Introduction

The clinical interview is a foundational practice of psychology. It forms the basis for assessment, diagnosis, and treatment planning. The clinical interview can vary somewhat depending on the environment in which it is applied and the services it is supporting. Irrespective of its form, the clinical interview is critical to delivering effective evaluation, intervention, and referral services, and it may be the first or only interaction a provider has with a client.

The clinical interview is more than an opportunity to gather information. Some argue that, at its core, the two most important components of a clinical interview are collecting data and building rapport (Pashak & Heron, 2022). Interviewers must obtain information related to the client’s primary issues, including biological, psychological, and sociocultural factors that may influence their presentation, while leveraging an empathetic, compassionate stance to cultivate trust, openness, and honesty. Interweaving these clinical interview objectives is a nuanced, complex, and important skill set. Clinical interviewing is a multi-purpose and useful tool for clinicians. In this chapter, we present several concepts and strategies that support a productive clinical interview. We outline the anatomy of a clinical interview and highlight its possible applications.

First, we discuss factors that contribute to the process of effective clinical interviewing. We present empathy and rapport as core interactive components of a clinical interview that have the potential to grow and change throughout the interview and therapeutic process. We review practical and relevant approaches to develop empathy and rapport, highlighting therapeutic assessment as an important concept in clinical interviewing. We also identify vital considerations for building trust and rapport in telehealth clinical interviews, a modality which became ubiquitous during the COVID-19 pandemic and continues to be frequently used.

Second, clinical interviews can be practically implemented for assessment and diagnosis. We review how data collected during clinical interviews can inform case formulation and conceptualization, which are critical components of treatment planning. We also discuss two legacy diagnostic taxonomies, the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders and the International Classification of Diseases, and we highlight emerging diagnostic frameworks. Diagnostic frameworks can inform how an interviewer may choose to conduct a clinical interview. Additionally, we discuss three broad categories of clinical interviews: unstructured, semi-structured, and structured interviews. We present advantages and disadvantages of the different types of interviews and provide guidance about how to choose among these options. Lastly, we provide a summary of several semi-structured and structured clinical interviews that are commonly implemented in clinical settings. We aim for this chapter to be useful to rising and trained clinicians alike by addressing one of the foremost skills in the practice of psychology: the clinical interview.

Interpersonal Process Skills

Effective interviewing depends on establishing a trusting, open, and collaborative working relationship with a client, and this requires a clinician to convey empathy and establish positive rapport with the client. Empathy and rapport are not only preconditions of effective assessment data gathering but they also actually function as common therapeutic factors, along with positive expectancy, goals, and other therapeutic outcomes from a well-conducted interview and assessment process (Wampold, 2015). Empathy and rapport interact to support one another, but, as will become apparent later in this section, empathy may be more foundational to rapport than vice versa. Here, we define the concept of empathy, illustrate its application and importance in interviewing, and describe how it can be facilitated. We then do the same for the concept of rapport. For both empathy and rapport, we integrate conceptual considerations related to cultural competence and telepsychology that have developed in recent years. Although current evidence indicates that assessments provided via synchronous teleconferencing are acceptable to clients and provide valid data (Bistre et al., 2022; Brown et al., 2021), there are specific considerations for developing empathy and rapport in telepsychology settings (Baier & Danzo, 2021).

Empathy

Although there is no universally accepted single definition, empathy is generally defined as the accurate understanding and acknowledgement of another person’s mental experience, from their frame of reference (Sinclair et al., 2017). The ability to achieve empathy with a range of people varies among the general population (Banissy et al., 2012), but a natural affinity for empathy is often a factor in why people pursue training and careers that involve clinical interviewing. Empathic ability can be further developed through environmental interactions, practice, and training (Abramson et al., 2020), but ultimately in clinical encounters, empathy is an interpersonal process that must be deployed with responsive timing and nuance. This may require a clinician to activate similar mental representations as their client, to exert cognitive control to shift attention away from one’s own perspective, and to activate perspective-taking abilities (Abramson et al., 2020).

Evidence indicates that empathy consists of two components: cognitive empathy and affective empathy (Banissy et al., 2012). Cognitive empathy involves using cognitive processes to understand and predict another person’s mental state, including how it would feel to be in their particular situation (Cuff et al., 2016). By contrast, affective empathy involves activating an emotional response due to and resonant with another person’s state (Halpern, 2003). Within a clinical interview, this cognitive-emotional understanding and resonance is an ever-unfolding “dynamic process that involves cognitive and emotional discoveries about others’ experiences” (Main et al., 2017, p. 358). Although clinicians may generally be predisposed to empathize with clients, client actions can, at times, elicit frustration, disgust, and judgment from a clinician, which could impede the clinician’s ability to provide the client empathy (Ahn et al., 2021). But why does empathy matter?

Empathy serves several important functions. First, providing empathy establishes that the client is worthy of respect, understanding, and compassion (Barnett & Mann, 2013). This anchors the interview in familiar affiliative human interaction patterns so that the client is more apt to lower defensiveness and increase trust, ultimately fostering clinical rapport. This is important for all clients, and it may be particularly important for clients who have experienced trauma, as part of a trauma-informed approach that explicitly values and supports collaboration and a client’s autonomy, experience, and need to feel safe. Accurate empathy reduces the possibility of an interview being re-traumatizing, as the clinician will cognitively and emotionally perceive and respect a client’s limits. Aside from preventing harm, greater empathy can promote better outcomes from assessment or subsequent treatment (Riess, 2017), potentially by reducing clinician biases that would impede good outcomes (Garb, 2021), as the clinician more accurately understands the client from a lived experience perspective.

Relatedly, when a client receives accurate empathy in a clinical interview, it may itself have therapeutic effects (Finn et al., 2012). For example, when a client shares their experiences and feels understood by a clinician, it can reduce feelings of isolation and stigmatization while increasing hope and self-understanding (Wampold, 2015). Empathy may also increase client adherence to clinical recommendations (Riess, 2017). If they feel understood, accepted, and validated, they may be more open to behavioral change (Rogers, 1995; Shapiro et al., 2018). Further, greater empathy is also associated with reduced medical errors and malpractice claims (Riess, 2017) as well as greater professional quality of life for clinicians (Laverdière et al., 2019). Thus, empathy provides clear benefits for clients and clinicians, if clinicians can develop and activate it.

Empathy can be developed and activated through intentional approaches, practices, and attention to facilitators and barriers. One well-articulated model called therapeutic assessment (TA; Finn, 2020; Finn et al., 2012) seeks to use interviewing and testing as means of gaining empathic understanding of the client’s challenges, dilemmas, problems, and hopes. In eliciting client information and experiences and reflecting them back (e.g., restatements, paraphrases, summaries, reflections of emotion and meaning; Ivey et al., 2023), the clinician helps the client develop accurate and self-compassionate narratives that include new shared discoveries and understandings. Another model, dialectical behavior therapy, has articulated the concept of phenomenological empathy with strived-for benchmarks of empathic validation (Carson-Wong & Rizvi, 2016). At a basic level, empathic validation requires being fully present, while the client communicates thoughts, feelings, and needs, and accurately reflecting back what the client has said. Going a level deeper, empathy involves inferring unspoken thoughts or emotions, recognizing how the person’s thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are completely understandable based on their background and present circumstances. At the deepest level, empathy involves relating to the client genuinely at a person-to-person level.

Empathic understanding can also be facilitated by addressing potential barriers. One potential barrier can be disconnected or disjointed communication. In an in-person interviewing format, this could result from an inability to reach shared understanding of content between a client and interviewer due to sensory-perceptual impairment or a failure to understand the other’s slang, vernacular, or nonverbal cues. In a telepsychology context, shared understandings from fluid dynamic interaction could be blocked by Internet connection disruptions (e.g., screen freezing, buffering) or impeded by the inability to see important nonverbal gestures or expressions, which can necessitate greater expression of empathic statements (Stevens et al., 2020). Empathy might also be impacted by the presence of others in the client’s environment, if the client might disclose differently around them. At the same time, telepsychology often provides an opportunity to observe and interact with the interviewee in their own environment. Being “in” a client’s home (virtually) may provide cues regarding others in their environment, and it may provide more important, intimate information that helps the interviewer fully understand and empathize with the client’s experience. Consider a client sheepishly disclosing a “clutter issue” in a clinic; now consider the same client disclosure in front of background revealing their home as a fire hazard due to severe hoarding behaviors. In essence, visible evidence eliminates distortion or bias that might otherwise emerge from ambiguous or omitted information, facilitating accurate empathy.

Biases may promote empathy or impede it. Research indicates that empathy may be more naturally evoked when we interact with those who we perceive as like us, such as being of the same race (Armstrong, 2017). However, clinicians are trained to become aware of their biases and are cautioned to avoid premature assumptions of empathy (“I’ve had that happen to me” or “I’ve assessed clients with similar cultural backgrounds”), as confirmation bias can prevent soliciting important information (Weiner & Auster, 2007).

Striving for cultural empathy begins with viewing clients as being embedded within cultures and affected by culture-based ways of thinking, feeling, and behaving (Levitt et al., 2022). Thus, to promote accurate cultural empathy, a clinician may consider using a semi-structured approach such as the Cultural Formulation Interview (CFI, described below). Cultural empathy is further developed in an interview through the application of cultural competence, possessed knowledge and skills that enable the interviewer to adapt and work effectively with persons of different cultural backgrounds. For example, if a client from a historically oppressed background reports experiences of discrimination, the interviewer responds with empathic emotion and appropriate follow-up conversation so that the client feels better understood. Further, the interviewer empathizes with and avoids pathologizing understandable distrust of institutions based on negative personal encounters, group-based mistreatment, and historically race-and gender-biased psychiatric diagnosis experiences (Garb, 2021; Miller & Peck, 2020). Evidence indicates that identifying as a woman or sexual minority or perceiving low multicultural competence are risk factors for premature termination from psychological services (Anderson et al., 2019), suggesting effects of myopic cultural hegemony in clinical encounters. By contrast, culturally competent interviewing emphasizes cultural humility (Pashak & Heron, 2022), a modest interpersonal stance that validates the importance of the client’s cultural background and experiences through listening and genuine interest. Validating and empathizing with client disclosures—including selective, appropriate interviewer disclosure—helps clients feel safe and willing to disclose more sensitive information, building understanding and rapport.

Rapport

Within a clinical interview, rapport refers to the development and maintenance of a working relationship that includes positivity, mutual attention and respect, open communication, and collaboration with shared understandings and goals (Gruba-McCallister, 2005; Johnston et al., 1998). Rapport is dynamic (Alison & Alison, 2017), and its depth and emotional valence can change with positive or negative interactions (Hall et al., 2009). Given that feelings of rapport reflect subjective internal states, research has indicated that self-report measures tend to be more useful in assessing rapport than observational methods (Vallano & Schreiber Compo, 2015). Further, because one partner’s sense of rapport in a dyadic relationship can be different than the other partner’s sense of rapport, it is best to assess and compare the perspectives of both partners. The Working Alliance Inventory (WAI; Horvath & Greenberg, 1989) is a commonly used measure that enables this. The client and interviewer can each rate the degree to which there is agreement on overall goals being pursued, the activities being used, and—most pertinent to rapport—the quality of the bond between interviewer and client. Ratings, as well as follow-up discussion of them, can be helpful in understanding how to maintain and/or improve rapport.

As with empathy, greater rapport in clinical interviewing has beneficial effects. Positive rapport facilitates openness, which in turn helps with gathering relevant information to support accurate diagnosis, well-informed case formulation and treatment planning, and client adherence to that plan (Ross, 2013). Likely for these reasons, rapport is one of the most robust predictors of a positive psychotherapy outcome for both in-person and telepsychology services (Aafjes-van Doorn et al., 2024; Horvath, 2001). A single mental health consultation can build hope and understanding in a client (Sung et al., 2023) if an interviewer can establish positive rapport.

Applying specific, trainable clinical skills can help facilitate rapport-building. For example, interviewers are advised to project a relaxed, interested, nonjudgmental demeanor, use active listening, and to assume a generally open body posture (Ivey et al., 2023; Morrison, 2016). Rapport can also be effectively developed in in-person or telehealth settings (Davis et al., 2024; Watts et al., 2020) by appropriately mirroring client paralanguage cues, such as tone, volume, pace, and style (Phillip et al., 2020) and by displaying culturally appropriate nonverbal engagement signals like posture, eye contact, proximal distance, and facial expressivity (Bell et al., 2016). Light, safe humor may be sparingly used to make an interview more comfortable, but it should not be used to avoid important challenging topics or feelings (Ledley & Rauch, 2005; Sattler, 2014). All of these skills should be tailored to the available timeframe and setting.

Developing rapport in tele-psychotherapy settings necessitates unique considerations. For example, an interviewer may attempt humor, but it may not connect the same way it would in-person (McCord et al., 2021; Reese & Chapman, 2017), given the potential for streaming lags or lower audio-visual fidelity to transmit subtleties of facial expression or vocal tone. Body posture or gestures may be less visible, so clinicians may need to amplify their expressiveness through visible channels but without being inauthentic. In virtual interviews, relationship building may feel more remote, feel less vital, suffer from “Zoom fatigue,” and require more energy (McCoyd et al., 2022). Lastly, keep in mind that the client may feel more self-conscious if their screen includes a picture-in-picture (PiP) showing their face. This could influence their responses and affect rapport. The clinician should try to be attuned to the client’s comfort, and they might help the client turn off PiP if it might decrease discomfort and distraction.

Framing and conducting the interview collaboratively also helps build rapport. After brief introductory “small talk,” the interviewer should help prepare the interviewee for what is to come. Expanding from the informed consent process, the interviewer should facilitate discussion of procedures, client expectations, and collaborative goals and context for the interview (Morrison, 2016). This could be especially important if a client assumes that their goals will be ignored or will compete with the interviewer’s. One collaborative rapport-building strategy is to liken the interview encounter to a detective enlisting the interviewee’s help in understanding a mystery, where discoveries will be mutually beneficial (Segal et al., 2019). In line with this, the therapeutic assessment approach (Finn, 2020; Finn et al., 2012) recommends engaging clients in discussing the referral question, asking what concerns they might have about themselves or their situation, and what they would like to learn from the interview. Curiosity can decrease defensiveness and mitigate unhelpful negative emotions in interviews like fear, shame, apprehension (Kashdan & Roberts, 2006). The interviewer can further demonstrate mutuality through occasional, appropriate disclosure of their own thinking process or by supportive communication, such as expressing that a clinical interview can be a vulnerable experience and that the client is showing courage and trust by engaging.

In a circumstance where a structured interview or testing is embedded in a broader assessment, a clinician operating from a therapeutic assessment (TA) mindset would describe basic characteristics of the assessment, explain the relevance of assessment questions, and encourage the client to voice any reflections after completing the assessments. The TA-minded interviewer then ensures that the review of assessment results speaks to the questions the client was interested in at the outset of the interview and delivers any potentially uncomfortable results with empathy and modesty. The interviewer would also elicit the client’s thoughts about assessment results and ask for consistent or contrary life examples to arrive at valid assessment interpretations a client can integrate into their life and self-narratives. This is particularly helpful in working with people of diverse cultural backgrounds (see Strand & Bäärnhielm, 2022 for discussion on integrating and adapting the CFI with a TA-focus). Further, they invite the client to interrupt with questions or comments, to agree, or to disagree. All these actions support positive rapport in that the client experiences respect, empowerment, and efficacy in their participation, rather than feeling like a clinical specimen. Further, a collaborative approach increases a client’s sense of safety and trust, which are broadly helpful to rapport-building and specifically indicated for “trauma-informed” interviewing. Given the high proportions of those presenting to health care settings with a history of trauma and/or posttraumatic stress symptoms (Benjet et al., 2016; Greene et al., 2016), interviews should be trauma-informed by default. By doing so, and by following other rapport-building practices reviewed above, interviews will be more pleasant, motivating, and useful for clients, and they will better inform clinicians’ case formulations.

Technical Skills

Case Formulation

Case formulation (also known as case conceptualization) involves comprehensively understanding a client’s presenting concern, any precipitating and maintaining factors, and a hypothesis of the underlying factors driving the problem, along with their origins. Although a full, thorough overview of case formulation is beyond the scope of this chapter, it is an important technical foundation for clinical and diagnostic interviewing. Indeed, some research suggests that incorporating case formulation into your interview framework results in better outcomes for clients (Persons et al., 2006). A good case formulation stems from a collaborative clinical interview with the client to collect pertinent data related not only to diagnosis but also to various influencing factors that may be driving a diagnosis. Predictions about the client’s diagnoses are then made based on the case formulation, and the hypotheses are then tested by applying a treatment plan that aligns with the client’s and therapist’s goals for treatment (Persons, 2022).

To build on this collaborative process, a useful case formulation is rooted in an effective diagnostic interview. As reviewed later in this section, clinical interviews can be unstructured, semi-structured, or structured. With any interview type, a good starting point is to gain an understanding of the current problem(s). A longstanding psychotherapy theory is that clients present for treatment when feeling demoralized (Frank, 1974), so it is also important to assess why the client presented for treatment at this time and whether any functional impairment is present. As discussed earlier in this chapter, it is important to gather this pertinent information while also maintaining rapport and collaborative interpersonal dynamics. To advance case formulation, additional information should also be gathered about the client’s presentation.

Behavioral Observations

The content a client communicates is just one source of information. Another important source of information comes from observable features, which can provide additional insight into client strengths and problem(s). For instance, the client may present with what they are calling “depression,” but you notice their affect is within normal limits even though they described their mood as “sad.” A client may be presenting for treatment for court-ordered alcohol misuse treatment and appear to have an unsteady gait and slurred speech despite them insisting that they are sober. Behavioral observations can help develop understanding of the client’s problems and place some of their problems into context.

In many instances, clinicians include behavioral observations in their reports and integrate them into their diagnostic rationales and case formulation. The first step in behavioral observation is to identify and operationalize behaviors consistent with the presenting problem. For instance, we may expect a client presenting with problems related to social anxiety disorder to appear anxious speaking with us (e.g., avoiding eye contact, turning red when disclosing sensitive information). It can also be helpful to notice and describe behaviors that are not fully consistent with expected presentations to help in differential diagnosis.

The mental status exam can organize behavioral organization and ensure adequate data are captured (Norris et al., 2016). Table 2.1 lists the key components of a mental status exam and offers some examples of verbiage typically included in an assessment or intake report. Although electronic medical record systems now incorporate the mental status exam into list form with check boxes, it is recommended that the behavioral observation section of the psychological report be written in narrative format to maintain consistency with other sections of the report and enhance meaningful qualitative nuance. Diagnostic Classification and Interviewing Frameworks

For the past several decades, many mental health professionals have approached diagnosis using some iteration of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM). The current iteration of the DSM is the DSM-5-Text Revision (American Psychiatric Association, 2022), which is widely used in the United States and Australia. These diagnoses largely map onto the International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems (11th edition) (World Health Organization, 2018)—a diagnostic framework that much of the rest of the world uses. These diagnostic systems use a categorical-polythetic classification approach to mental disorder classification. This approach assumes that psychopathology can be classified, like medical diseases, into distinct categories—where meeting a diagnostic threshold includes having a sufficient number and range of symptoms. A benefit to such a system for diagnosis is that it capitalizes on reliability. Indeed, when using structured clinical interview, reliability coefficients tend to be high relative to unstructured means (Osorio et al., 2019). Thus, this chapter focuses on a more structured approach to diagnosis to promote consistency in diagnostic clinical interviewing.

It is important to note that alternative models for assessing mental health disorders have been put forth and include the Research Domain Criteria (RDoC; Insel et al., 2010), the Hierarchical Taxonomy Model of Psychopathology (HiTOP) (Kotov et al., 2017), and the Alternative Model for Personality Disorders (AMPD) (American Psychiatric Association, 2022; Krueger et al., 2012).

RDoC was an initiative put forth by the National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH) to move away from categorical-polythetic classification systems. RDoC is meant to (1) quantify dimensional domains as they relate to behaviors and neural systems and (2) foster research to validate these dimensions defined by neurobiology and behavioral measures that cut across current disorder categories. It is important to note that RDoC was not developed to replace current diagnostic systems; rather, it was created to be used as a framework to increase fidelity within research findings.

HiTOP is a more recent initiative that builds upon decades of evidence regarding the structure of psychopathology. Regardless of the types of indicators used, HiTOP has consistently replicated (Forbes et al., 2024; Kotov et al., 2021; Ringwald et al., 2021, 2023). A number of self-report measures are aligned with assessing dimensions within the HiTOP framework, including the Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory-3 (MMPI-3; Ben-Porath & Tellegen, 2020a, b) and the Personality Assessment Inventory (PAI; Morey, 1991, 2020). The creation of additional self-report measures and a structured clinical interview for HiTOP is currently underway.

Lastly, the AMPD is recognized and included in Sect. III (Emerging Models and Measures) of the DSM-5-TR. AMPD is a dimensional model that is used to diagnose personality disorders. There is a self-report method to assess personality impairment severity (Morey, 2017) though it is not yet practical to use in clinical practice due to lack of normative data and clear guidelines as to how to interpret high and low scale scores. However, a structured clinical interview has been developed and validated to assess dimensional personality traits (First et al., 2017) and will be discussed below.

Ways of Interviewing

Diagnostic interviewing can be conceptualized as being either unstructured, semi-structured, or structured (Mueller & Segal, 2014). The type of diagnostic interview one chooses to use is dependent on time, complexity, and whether reliability in diagnosing is a high priority.

Unstructured Diagnostic Interviews

Unstructured diagnostic interviews give the practitioner the freedom to ask questions and follow-up on responses in any manner that they see fit. Unstructured diagnostic interviewing provides expansive opportunities for the interviewer to develop strong rapport and therapeutic alliance with the client (Mueller & Segal, 2014). The flow, sequence, and content of unstructured diagnostic interviews can be guided by one’s theoretical orientation, training, knowledge base, comfort, and interpersonal style. A client’s response style can also guide the interview. Perhaps the client is quite verbal, and you can obtain a rich history and understanding of the presenting problems without too many interruptions. Conversely, you may have a client who is more guarded, and this provides you with the opportunity to build a stronger rapport and trust with the client while you obtain the minimum information needed for a diagnosis.

Structured and Semi-Structured Diagnostic Interviews

In fully structured diagnostic interviews, there is less flexibility than in unstructured interviews, and building rapport with the client often becomes challenging. There are not only questions that the interviewer needs to ask verbatim but also strict rules regarding the execution of the interview. Interruptions tend to be more common in these types of interviews because the interviewer needs the client to stay on task and may need to consistently redirect the client to the task at hand. In structured diagnostic interviews, the questions are designed to assess the specific criteria for mental disorders defined in the DSM or other system (e.g., the AMPD).

In semi-structured interviews, the interviewer would be expected to ask some questions verbatim and/or follow a specific order but has more freedom regarding follow-up questions. Like structured diagnostic interviews, semi-structured interviews tend to be based on a diagnostic system and meant to guide the practitioner through criteria of various mental disorders. Unlike a fully structured interview, the practitioner may rely on their own experience and clinical judgment to ask follow-up questions, build rapport with the client as the interview is conducted, and allow the client to expand in some areas that resonate more strongly.

Advantages and Disadvantages of Diagnostic Interview Types

Why would someone want to conduct a fully structured interview if it might be harder to build rapport and trust with the client? If one uses an unstructured interview, can the diagnoses be trusted? These are some questions one may have after reading the previous section. Indeed, each diagnostic interviewing format has its advantages and disadvantages. Unstructured clinical interviews may be more practical in a high demand clinical setting. For instance, some private payers (i.e., insurance) currently only reimburse a diagnostic clinical interview for up to 60 min, which can create significant limitations in conducting a structured clinical interview. Therefore, in these settings, an unstructured interview is more commonly used because of both time constraints and the type of referral (e.g., therapy). Typically, a client is coming to therapy for a specific reason, and the provider may want to structure their interview to identify an appropriate diagnosis. An unstructured interview may allow the provider to focus on alleviating their distress, exploring whether a particular disorder is present, and then spend some time developing a treatment plan with them, all while showing empathy and building rapport. On the downside, this approach to the clinical interview may prevent exploration of some other differential diagnoses or comorbidities. In fact, some research has suggested that practitioners may miss up to 3–4 diagnoses when using an unstructured format versus a structured interview (Zimmerman & Mattia, 1999). Without structure, bias can emerge and impact diagnosis (Garb, 2021; Norman & Eva, 2010; Woodward et al., 2009). For instance, one may overlook an eating disorder in a man or be more likely to diagnosis a man with antisocial personality disorder when symptom presentation may better point toward borderline personality disorder.

Structured clinical interviews may be appropriate in certain situations. For example, a well-developed, more time-intensive, structured diagnostic interview would be recommended for a research protocol where reliability of diagnosis is a high priority (e.g., a randomized controlled trial; Shabani et al., 2021). Psychometrically, reliability sets the upper bounds of validity (Cronbach & Meehl, 1955). Additionally, a fully structured diagnostic interview can reduce clinician bias and error rate because all pertinent diagnoses are assessed in standardized fashion with little room for clinical judgment. The results of the interview guide the clinician through differential diagnosis considerations and will result in reliable, valid diagnoses. Thus, many research protocols use structured diagnostic interviews to garner confidence in diagnosis. Many of these interviews require training but can be administered by trained non-clinicians (e.g., research coordinator/assistant). As mentioned earlier, these interviews tend to be quite mechanical, leaving little room to build rapport or share empathy. As explored later in this chapter, some of these interviews can last upward to 90–120 min and they typically do not include coverage of personality disorders. Additionally, these interviews require training to administer and are often time intensive to score, though electronic means have reduced scoring time. Giving a fully structured interview may be too burdensome and time-consuming in many clinical practices but is often advisable in research designs.

Semi-structured interviews provide a balance between interview types. One needs to have clinical expertise to know when to ask appropriate follow-up questions, and the guided structure of the interview helps ensure that no major areas in psychodiagnosis are missed. At the onset, however, practitioners still need considerable training, and they must have the appropriate expertise to know how to navigate the unstructured elements of the semi-structured interview. The unstructured components of a semi-structured interview lead to some of the same disadvantages as a fully unstructured interview, but the structured aspect helps improve diagnostic coverage and reliability.

Examples of Fully and Semi-Structured Interviews for DSM-5

Cultural Formulation Interview

The Cultural Formulation Interview (CFI; Lewis-Fernández et al., 2016) was developed in 2013 to provide guidance for psychologists in addressing the relevance of cultural issues in mental health care (American Psychiatric Association, 2013). Although this interview is not used for diagnostic purposes, the CFI is used to ensure that cultural factors are considered in the process of diagnosis and treatment planning. This 20-min interview was designed to equate the importance of the individual perspective with the signs and symptoms of a disorder and provides a semi-structured format to accomplish this goal (Aggarwal & Lewis-Fernández, 2020). The CFI is structured to be straightforward and simple to administer. A total of 16 questions comprises four sections of the interview: cultural definition of the problem; cultural perceptions of cause, context, and support; cultural identity of the individual; and cultural factors affecting self-coping and past help-seeking. When necessary and appropriate, an alternative version of the CFI, the Informant Cultural Formulation Interview, can be conducted with close associates of the client to collect collateral information. This version of the CFI may be particularly helpful when working with clients who have cognitive limitations. The CFI has been administered internationally in field tests and has demonstrated efficacy and improved trust and alliance in the therapeutic relationship (Jarvis et al., 2020).

The Diagnostic Interview Schedule for DSM-5

The Diagnostic Interview Schedule (DIS; Summerfeldt & Antony, 2002) was first published in 1988 and was the first fully structured interview designed to assess for the presence or absence of major psychiatric disorders. The DIS was developed to minimize the need for clinical judgment when making a diagnosis, reducing the education requirements to administer the assessment and broadening its applications. The current DIS for DSM-5 (DIS-5) consists of 14 diagnostic modules and covers several disorders from categories such as depressive disorders, anxiety disorders, trauma-and stressor-related disorders, and substance use disorders. However, this interview does not assess separation anxiety disorder, somatic symptoms and related disorders, dissociative disorders, and most sexual disorders. This modular format of the interview allows the interviewer to choose which diagnostic modules to administer, potentially based on symptom presentation.

The Structured Clinical Interview for DSM-5

Developed to improve validity of psychiatric diagnoses, the Structured Clinical Interview for the DSM (SCID) was published by the American Psychiatric Association with DSM-III (American Psychiatric Association, 1987). The SCID is a semi-structured interview designed to facilitate the diagnosis of a wide range of current or lifetime mental health disorders and can be used for clinical and research purposes. This interview was designed for administration by a mental health professional, though there are no degree requirements to administer this assessment (First, 2015). Clinical experience is the most relevant prerequisite for administering the SCID. The SCID has been revised with each update to the DSM, and the current version is the SCID-5, corresponding with the DSM-5. There are 12 modules in the SCID-5, and each module can be used to diagnose multiple disorders (see Table 2.3). There are online versions of the SCID-5, which can reduce administrator burden and broaden the application of the SCID-5. The NETSCID-5 was the first fully computerized translation of the SCID, and the eInterview was developed to be software to administer the SCID-5 primarily for research purposes. There are currently five versions of the SCID-5 (see Table 2.4), and we will briefly describe each of them.

SCID-5 Research Version: The SCID-5 Research Version (SCID-5-RV; First et al., 2015a, b) was developed to be used in research settings. It is the most comprehensive version of the SCID-5 and includes a broad range of mental health diagnoses, as well as relevant subtypes and specifiers. There are two configurations of the SCID-5-RV available: the “core” version and the “enhanced” version. The “enhanced” version contains all the disorders in the “core” version and includes several optional disorders—separation anxiety disorder, avoidant/restrictive food intake disorder, intermittent explosive disorder, gambling disorder, hoarding disorder, body dysmorphic disorder, trichotillomania disorder, and excoriation disorder. The “core” version can be administered in 45–120 min, and the “enhanced” version can be administered in 45–180 min. The SCID-5-RV is customizable and can be tailored to the needs of researchers.

SCID-5 Clinician Version: The SCID-5 Clinician Version (SCID-5-CV; First et al., 2016) is the most commonly used version of the SCID-5 and is designed to be useful in clinical settings. This interview is an abridged and modified version of the SCID-5-RV meant to decrease clinician burden. The SCID-5-CV assesses the most common mental health diagnoses seen in clinical environments, such as depressive disorders and anxiety disorders. The SCID-5-CV is recommended to diagnose disorders other than personality disorders in clinical, forensic, or assessment settings, as well as in settings where the range of presenting disorders is sufficiently covered by the SCID-5-CV. The administration time for the SCID-5-CV ranges from 30 to 120 min.

SCID-5 Clinical Trials: The SCID-5 Clinical Trials Version (SCID-5-CT; First et al., 2015a) is an adapted and modified version of the SCID-5-RV to include the most common inclusion and exclusion criteria in clinical trials. The administration time is 30–75 min. There are two options for the SCID-5-CT. First, the SCID-5-CT can be fully customizable to feature various inclusion and exclusion criteria. Second, there are several templates of the SCID-5-CT that assess particular inclusionary mental health diagnoses such as schizoaffective disorder, schizophreniform disorder, schizophrenia, bipolar I acute mania, bipolar I or II depression, or adult ADHD. An “exclusionary” version of the SCID-5-CV is also available to exclude individuals from clinical trials with certain mental health diagnoses (e.g., substance use disorders, anorexia nervosa).

SCID-5 Personality Disorders: The SCID-5 Personality Disorder Version (SCID-5-PD; First et al., 2015a) assesses personality disorders according to the categorical classification system in DSM-5. In this assessment, there are ten distinct personality disorders: paranoid, schizoid, schizotypal, antisocial, borderline, histrionic, narcissistic, avoidant, dependent, and obsessive-compulsive. The SCID-5-PD also allows for the diagnosis of unspecified personality disorder. Each diagnosis includes a dimensional rating to note whether the personality disorder is “absent,” “sub-threshold,” or “threshold.” The administration time for the SCID-5-PD is 30–120 min. Additionally, the SCID-5-PD comes with a self-report questionnaire, the SCID-5-PDQ, to shorten administration time; this self-report measure can guide clinicians to possible diagnoses.

SCID-5 Alternative Model of Personality Disorders: The SCID-5 Alternative Model of Personality Disorders (SCID-5-AMPD; First et al., 2018) provides dimensional and categorical systems for diagnosing personality disorders. This interview is consistent with the conceptualization of personality disorders listed in the “Emerging Measures and Models” section of the DSM-5. There are five broad domains to describe personality functioning: negative affectivity, detachment, antagonism, disinhibition, and psychoticism.

QuickSCID-5: In 2021, the QuickSCID-5 was published as a brief alternative version to the SCID-5 (First & Williams, 2021). In contrast to the 2-h administration of the full SCID-5 interview, the QuickSCID-5 was designed to be a fully structured interview administered in 30 min or fewer. Most of the questions require dichotomous “yes/no” responses, eliminating the need to probe for additional, more descriptive examples. As such, this interview removes some of the burden from the clinician to be an expert in diagnostic interviewing. The QuickSCID-5 follows a modular design that allows the interviewer to select any module that is applicable. The modules include mood episodes and disorders, psychotic symptoms screen, alcohol and other substance use disorders, anxiety disorders, obsessive-compulsive disorder, adult ADHD, posttraumatic stress disorder, eating disorders, screening for other disorders, and rule-out mental disorders due to a medical condition and substance/medication-induced mental disorders. The QuickSCID-5 cannot be used to ascertain a psychotic disorder diagnosis, due to recommendations of avoiding such diagnoses with purely dichotomous yes/no responses.

The Diagnostic Interview for Anxiety, Mood, and OCD and Related Neuropsychiatric Disorders

The Diagnostic Interview for Anxiety, Mood, and OCD and Related Neuropsychiatric Disorders (DIAMOND; Tolin et al., 2018) is a semi-structured interview intended for use in adults with suspected anxiety, mood, or obsessive-compulsive and related disorders. The administration time for the DIAMOND ranges from 30 to 90 min. This interview includes questions that assess diagnostic criteria for all disorders in those sections of the DSM-5 and certain diagnoses that commonly co-occur with these diagnostic categories (e.g., autism spectrum disorder, avoidant personality disorder). Similar to the interviews described previously, the DIAMOND follows a modular format, and the selection of modules should be based on presenting problem. In fact, the DIAMOND includes an optional self-report screening to determine which modules would be most appropriate to administer.

The Anxiety and Related Disorders Interview Schedule for DSM-5

The Anxiety and Related Disorders Interview Schedule for DSM-5 (ADIS-5; Brown & Barlow, 2014) is a semi-structured interview designed to assess current mood, anxiety, obsessive-compulsive, trauma, and related disorders. This interview also contains screening questions for various disorders that co-occur with mood and anxiety disorders, such as hoarding disorder, impulse control disorders, feeding and eating disorders, dissociative disorders, and psychotic disorders. Additionally, throughout the interview, the interviewer inquires for dimensional ratings of the key features of all possible disorders, regardless of if the diagnosis is being considered. Dimensional ratings of the final diagnosis(es) are also included to measure the degree of distress or impairment the disorder may be causing. One alternative version of the ADIS-5—the ADIS-5 Lifetime (ADIS-5-L)—contains all of the same diagnoses as the ADIS-5 and includes assessment for lifetime diagnoses. This version also collects a diagnostic timeline to determine the time of onset, remission, and sequence of various diagnoses. The primary disadvantage of the ADIS-5 is the administration time; this assessment takes 2–4 h to administer.

The Mini-International Neuropsychiatric Interview for DSM-5

The Mini-International Neuropsychiatric Interview for DSM-5 (MINI-5) was developed to assess the disorders considered most important in clinical and research settings. This interview was designed to be brief and broad, requiring 15 min to administer and including symptoms from a variety of DSM-5 diagnoses (Sheehan et al., 1997). Each question is dichotomous and allows for yes/no answers. There are several versions of the MINI-5 available, including the MINI-KID, MINI-Screen, and the MINI-Plus. The MINI-KID was developed to assess the 30 most common mental health disorders in children and adolescents aged 6–17 (D. V. Sheehan et al., 2010). The MINI-KID has 24 modules, with separate suicidality modules organized by age group. The parent and child are interviewed together, although all questions are directed toward the child. Parents are asked to only respond if the child provides inaccurate information. To interview parents, the MINI-KID Parent Version would be more appropriate, though it is recommended to use the MINI-KID where possible. The administration time for the MINI-KID can vary between 15 and 50 min. The MINI-Screen and MINI-Plus were developed for use in adults. The MINI-Screen includes only the screening questions from the modules of the MINI-5. The instructions recommend conducting the full module based on positive screens from these questions to gather a more holistic understanding of accurate diagnoses. Lastly, the MINI-Plus is a slightly longer interview that can capture up to 48 disorders, including comorbidities (Pinninti et al., 2003).

The Kiddie Schedule for Affective Disorders and Schizophrenia for DSM-5

The Kiddie Schedule for Affective Disorders and Schizophrenia (K-SADS) is a semi-structured interview developed in 1978, modeled after the Schedule for Affective Disorders and Schizophrenia and geared toward children and adolescents aged 6–18. This interview has been updated with published revisions of the DSM, with the latest version being the K-SADS for DSM-5 (K-SADS-5; De La Peña et al., 2018). This interview was designed to help collect information from children, parents, and other collateral sources. Diagnoses are assessed as current (i.e., symptoms present within two months of interview) or lifetime (Kaufman et al., 1997). The K-SADS-5 consists of a screening section and six supplements. The screening section briefly assesses for affective and psychotic disorders but also includes other disorders such as feeding and eating disorders. Following the screening section, interviewers are able to choose appropriate supplemental assessments to administer. The six supplements include (1) depressive and bipolar disorders, (2) psychotic disorders, (3) anxiety, stress, and obsessive-compulsive disorders, (4) disruptive behavior and impulse control disorders, (5) substance use disorders and feeding and eating disorders, and (6) neurodevelopmental disorders. Administration time for this interview can range between 35 and 75 min, depending on the severity of symptom presentation.

Conclusion

Mastering the clinical interview requires interpersonal and technical skills. An effective interviewer finds a synergy between cultivating a collaborative, empathetic therapeutic relationship and gathering information. To do this, empathy and rapport are foundational to a safe, trusting environment for clients to divulge personal information. Cultural humility and cultural empathy are critical to building rapport and trust. In terms of methods to collect personal information, unstructured, semi-structured, and structured interviews are used to assess symptoms, inform diagnosis, and case conceptualization. Choosing one of these strategies can be a complex decision and can depend on the purpose of the interview, the setting in which the interview will take place, and the amount of time available to administer the interview. Each type of interview has potential benefits and drawbacks to consider. As reviewed, structured interviews maximize validity and reliability, while unstructured interviews encourage open, free communication, rich data collection, and rapport-building.

The evolution of the clinical interview will continue to occur in tandem with innovation and advancement in psychology. For example, additional research is necessary to learn more about the degree of acceptability and validity of different types of interviews when administered across a diverse multicultural population. Several of the semi-structured and structured interviews described earlier are consistent with the diagnostic taxonomy of the DSM-5; however, the DSM is not the only classification system used in the field of psychology. Standardized interviews, such as the ones described in this chapter, will continue to be revised in concurrence with our understanding and conceptualization of mental health disorders. Although artificial intelligence continues to evolve in domains of information aggregation and use, clinical interviewing remains a fundamental skillset based in a human-to-human connection. As psychology continues to progress, the clinical interview continues to be a central and foundational practice.
