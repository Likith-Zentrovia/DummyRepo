This chapter focuses on three different categories of disorders related to sexual function and pleasure, gender, and distressing or harmful sexual behavior as defined by the DSM-5-TR (American Psychiatric Association, 2022): sexual dysfunctions, gender dysphoria, and paraphilic disorders. In general, sexual dysfunctions are conditions that affect one’s ability to function sexually or to experience sexual pleasure; gender dysphoria is a condition in which distress is experienced due to an incongruency between a person’s experienced gender/sex1 (i.e., the internal, psychological experience of a person’s gender/sex) (van Anders, 2015) and their assigned gender/sex (i.e., how their gender/sex is perceived by others); and paraphilic disorders are characterized by atypical, intense, and persistent sexual interests that cause distress or harm (or risk of harm). Typically, clinicians working in the area of sex therapy or sexual medicine will see clients with sexual dysfunctions and/or gender dysphoria, whereas clinicians who specialize in forensic psychology will see clients with paraphilic disorders given that these disorders often entail legal consequences.

Description of the Disorders

Sexual Dysfunctions

According to the DSM-5-TR (American Psychiatric Association, 2022), sexual dysfunctions are characterized by disturbances in a person’s ability to respond sexually or to experience sexual pleasure. Sexual dysfunctions consist of clinically significant issues with desire, interest, and arousal (e.g., erection), orgasm (including ejaculation), and genitopelvic pain and penetration, and they may be diagnosed as being due to the use of medications and/or substances. When more than one sexual dysfunction is present, all should be recorded (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). If the presence of a sexual dysfunction can be explained by comorbid nonsexual mental disorders, such as an anxiety or mood disorder, only the nonsexual disorder diagnosis (e.g., generalized anxiety) should be made (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). In addition, the diagnosis of a sexual dysfunction requires ruling out sexual issues that are better explained by the effects of a medical condition or by severe relationship distress, partner violence, or other stressors (American Psychiatric Association, 2022).

There are seven sexual dysfunctions (Table 14.1; refer to the DSM-5-TR for three other designations [substance/medication-induced, other specified, and unspecified sexual dysfunction]) that are diagnosable when the problem is associated with significant distress and have been present for a minimum of approximately 6 months (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). Subtypes are used to designate the onset, context, and distress severity. In terms of onset, “lifelong” refers to a sexual dysfunction that appears to have always been present, and “acquired” indicates that the sexual dysfunction developed after a period of non-problematic experiences. With respect to context, sexual dysfunctions may be limited to certain types of stimulation, situations, or partners (“situational”), whereas “generalized” dysfunctions are not. Distress severity (i.e., mild, moderate, severe) should be documented for all sexual dysfunctions except for premature (early) ejaculation, in which the severity subtype corresponds to the client’s estimate of ejaculatory latency during penetrative sexual activity (i.e., mild, 30–60 s; moderate, 15–30 s; severe, prior to or at the start of sexual activity, or within 15 s) (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). In clinical settings, such accurate time-based distinctions for premature (early) ejaculation are rarely made; instead, general estimates (e.g., “before my erection,” “as soon as I am aroused,” “within a few seconds of penetration,” “within a minute or two after penetration”) are used to inform severity (Pukall & Bergeron, 2021).

Genitopelvic pain/penetration disorder

Although not explicitly stated in the DSM-5-TR (but stated in some previous versions), sexual dysfunctions are based on the first three stages of the four-phase sexual response cycle (Masters & Johnson, 1966): (1) desire/interest, which consists of fantasies about and the wish to engage in sexual activity; (2) excitement/arousal, which manifests as a subjective sexual pleasure and associated physiological changes (e.g., genital lubrication and engorgement); and (3) orgasm, which consists of the release of sexual tension and the rhythmic contractions of genitopelvic muscles and organs. A problem at any one stage of the sexual response cycle is likely to lead to difficulties with other stages; indeed, clinically, a high comorbidity among the sexual dysfunctions is observed. For example, a client presenting with orgasm difficulties may also experience problems with desire/interest. In addition, genitopelvic pain can potentially interfere with any or all aspects of sexual response. Although sound empirical data on comorbidities are lacking, a recent consensus paper concluded that comorbidity among sexual dysfunctions was more common among cis women than cis men (McCabe et al., 2016).

Although the DSM-5-TR has continued a long tradition that separates sexual dysfunctions into those that affect cis men and cis women—with some of them named in a gender/sex-specific manner (e.g., male hypoactive sexual desire disorder, female sexual interest/arousal disorder)—it specifies that the diagnostic criteria can be applied to people of any gender/sex given that they describe symptoms that are not gender/sex-dependent (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). Furthermore, it specifies that any diagnosis referring to anatomy (e.g., genitopelvic pain/penetration disorder refers to, for example, vaginal penetration and vulvovaginal pain) should be based on the client’s current anatomy and not on their sex assigned at birth (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). Clinical judgment will play an important role in ensuring appropriate, relevant diagnoses are made for gender minority clients (individuals who are, for example, trans or non-binary), especially since research on sexual dysfunctions in this population is limited.

Sexual desire and arousal disorders include male hypoactive sexual desire disorder (HSDD), erectile disorder (ED), and female sexual interest/arousal disorder (SIAD). The diagnosis of male HSDD is made when a client describes persistent or recurrent deficient (or absent) sexual/erotic thoughts or fantasies and desire for sexual activity (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). Although the DSM-5 considers desire dysfunctions as distinct from arousal dysfunctions for men (i.e., there are separate diagnoses for HSDD and ED; Table 14.1), this distinction is not made for women. Based on research indicating that women recognize a high degree of overlap in perceptions of sexual desire and arousal (Graham et al., 2004, this pattern is also true of men; Janssen et al., 2008), the DSM-5-TR includes the diagnosis of SIAD, which replaced the former diagnoses of female HSDD and female sexual arousal disorder (FSAD). SIAD is characterized by at least three of the following: absent/reduced (1) interest in sexual activity; (2) sexual/erotic thoughts or fantasies; (3) sexual excitement/pleasure during sexual activity in almost all or all (75–100%) sexual encounters; (4) sexual interest/arousal in response to any internal or external sexual/erotic cues; and (5) genital/nongenital sensations during sexual activity (75–100% of encounters); and no/reduced initiation of sexual activity and typically unreceptive to a partner’s attempts to initiate (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). The prevalence of desire disorders in women is estimated to be between 15 and 55%—with rates of 40–50% in those aged 65 years and above—and between 15 and 25% in men (McCabe et al., 2016). A diagnosis of ED is made when at least one of the following three symptoms are experienced on all or almost all (75–100%) sexual encounters: marked difficulty in obtaining or maintaining an erection, or a marked decrease in erectile rigidity (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). A wide range of prevalence rates is reported for ED (i.e., 15–76%) as rates vary with age; overall, the prevalence rate of ED in the USA is 22% (McCabe et al., 2016).

Orgasmic disorders consist of female orgasmic disorder (FOD), and, in men, delayed ejaculation (DE) and premature (early) ejaculation (PE). FOD is characterized by the presence of at least one of the following symptoms (experienced in 75–100% of sexual encounters): marked delay in, marked infrequency of, or absence of orgasm; and markedly reduced intensity of orgasmic sensations (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). FOD is estimated to affect between 16 and 25% of women (McCabe et al., 2016). The diagnostic criteria for DE consist of at least one of the following symptoms (experienced in 75–100% of sexual encounters), without desiring the delay: marked delay in ejaculation, and marked infrequency or absence of ejaculation (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). The prevalence of DE is 1–10% (McCabe et al., 2016). PE, which affects approximately 8–30% of men of all ages (McCabe et al., 2016), is diagnosed when a patient describes a persistent or recurrent pattern of ejaculation experienced in 75–100% of partnered sexual encounters within approximately one minute following vaginal penetration and before the individual wishes it. Note that these criteria can also be applied to nonvaginal penetration activities; however, specific duration criteria have not yet been established for these activities (American Psychiatric Association, 2022).

Genitopelvic pain/penetration disorder (GPPPD) is a sexual dysfunction that is currently restricted to individuals with female-typical genitopelvic anatomy; it affects between 14 and 27% of women (McCabe et al., 2016). The diagnosis of GPPPD involves persistent or recurrent difficulties with at least one of the following: (1) vaginal penetration during intercourse; (2) marked vulvovaginal/pelvic pain during vaginal intercourse or penetration attempts; (3) marked fear/anxiety about vulvovaginal/pelvic pain in anticipation of, during, or as a result of vaginal penetration; and (4) marked tensing or tightening of the pelvic floor muscles during attempted vaginal penetration (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). Despite a large representation of research on genitopelvic pain in men and an estimated prevalence of 16.8% (McCabe et al., 2016), the DSM-5-TR states that new research on genitopelvic pain in men suggests that a similar dysfunction may be diagnosable in the future. In the meantime, the DSM-5-TR suggests using the diagnoses of an “other specified” or an “unspecified” sexual dysfunction for men with genitopelvic pain.

Gender Dysphoria

Gender dysphoria (GD), the prevalence of which has been estimated to be between 0.1% and 1% (Byne et al., 2018), is characterized by an individual’s affective/cognitive discontent (i.e., distress, dysphoria) that results from an incongruence between their experienced gender (i.e., the internal, psychological experience of their gender) and their assigned gender (i.e., how their gender is perceived by others based on their outward appearance) (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). The person’s experienced gender may be binary (e.g., a person who was assigned female gender/sex at birth and identifies as a man/male) or non-binary (e.g., a person who was assigned female gender/sex at birth and who identifies as agender). Not all people who experience gender incongruence experience dysphoria or distress. The diagnosis is made when the dysphoria is experienced for a minimum of approximately six months and is associated with clinically significant distress or functional impairment. The following disorders also need to be ruled out: transvestic disorder (i.e., the distressing or impairing behavior of cross-dressing for the purpose of sexual excitement), body dysmorphic disorder, schizophrenia or other psychotic disorders, or other clinical presentations (e.g., the desire to rid oneself of one’s penis for aesthetic reasons, which is rare) (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). In addition, clinical guidelines have been developed in response to the high co-occurrence between GD and autism spectrum disorder in adolescents (Strang et al., 2018). As the expression of GD varies with age, the DSM-5-TR includes separate diagnostic criteria for children and adolescents/adults (Table 14.2). In children, the typical onset of cross-gender behaviors is between the ages of 2 and 4 years, with more frequent expressions of anatomic dysphoria as puberty approaches (American Psychiatric Association, 2022).

It is important to be aware of the high levels of vulnerability of people with GD: before gender-affirming care, they are at increased risk for a variety of mental health disorders including depression and anxiety, and are at increased risk of suicidality (Tordoff et al., 2022). These symptoms remit for most individuals once they undergo gender-affirming care (Tordoff et al., 2022) and interventions that lessen discriminatory and minority stress (Bauer et al., 2015). Gender affirmation consists of a variety of options, some nonmedical (e.g., social and legal transition) and some medical (e.g., hormonal treatment, surgery) (Coleman et al., 2022). Not all individuals with GD will undergo gender-affirming surgery or hormone therapy, and not all will seek mental health care (Margulies et al., 2021).

Paraphilic Disorders

The DSM-5-TR (American Psychiatric Association, 2022) defines a paraphilia as an “intense and persistent sexual interest other than sexual interest in genital stimulation or preparatory fondling with phenotypically normal, physically mature, consenting human partners” (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). It distinguishes between a paraphilia (generally defined as an atypical, intense, and persistent sexual interest) and a paraphilic disorder (a paraphilia that causes distress or harm/risk of harm to others). There are eight specified paraphilic disorders (Table 14.3) and in each one, the specific focus of erotic interest (minimum duration of 6 months) is characterized in Criterion A, and any distress, impairment, or harm that this interest causes is captured in Criterion B. Should an individual meet solely the requirements for Criterion A, then a paraphilia is identified. However, if the elements of Criterion B are also met, then a paraphilic disorder is diagnosed. This distinction reflects a line of thought in which an individual might harbor, for example, a sexual interest in children, in which case the presence of a paraphilia may be identified. However, it is only a paraphilic disorder if these feelings significantly impair the person’s ability to function or if these feelings are acted upon, causing harm (or the risk of harm). Note that it is possible for individuals to have multiple paraphilias/paraphilic disorders. In addition to specified paraphilias, the DSM-5-TR acknowledges a broad range of other paraphilias (e.g., necrophilic disorder), which are recorded as “other specified paraphilia disorder” when the nature of the atypical interest is clear and as “unspecified paraphilic disorder” when it is not.

In terms of the course specifiers, both the terms “in full remission” and “in a controlled environment” can generally be applied to paraphilic disorders. An exception is that pedophilic disorders cannot be declared to be in remission, which has fostered some debate (e.g., Briken et al., 2014). These specifiers were incorporated to reflect the potential for a person to greatly reduce the likelihood of acting on paraphilic interests, thereby countervailing distress, impairment, and the potential for harm. Under such conditions, the paraphilia remains but the paraphilic disorder is deemed to be in remission. The specifier regarding the presence of a controlled environment was included because many individuals who have acted on paraphilic interests have restrictions on their liberty, thereby rendering it difficult to assess their ongoing tendencies.

With regard to the prevalence of paraphilic disorders, the DSM-5-TR cautions that there is considerable uncertainty. It is estimated that the percentage of men who have acted on such paraphilic impulses is (at most) 12% for voyeuristic disorder, 2–4% for exhibitionistic disorder, 10–14% for frotteuristic disorder, and around 3–5% for pedophilic disorder (American Psychiatric Association, 2022). All rates are higher for men than women and sometimes substantially so. The reasons for this disparity are unclear, although sexual drive has been proposed as a mediator (Dawson et al., 2016) and there is some suggestion that rates are somewhat higher for women than previously reported (Joyal & Carpentier, 2017).

Procedures for Gathering Information

Sexual Dysfunctions

The clinical interview is the main technique with which to assess and diagnose sexual dysfunctions. There is no widely used, validated, standardized interview for sexual dysfunctions as is the case for many other DSM-5-TR disorders. However, several authors have proposed clinical interview guidelines and recommendations for coverage of topics and processes (e.g., Levine et al., 2016; Wincze & Carey, 2015).

The clinical interview typically starts with the individual describing the nature of the problem and the reason for seeking treatment. Following an open-ended description of the problem, the clinician may ask more specific questions about when the problem started (onset), the conditions under which it occurs (context), and the extent of the problem and amount of distress experienced because of the issue (severity). Questions can then be asked about the various biological, psychological, and social problems that might be implicated (Levine et al., 2016; Wincze & Carey, 2015). In terms of general biological factors, the clinician should assess and take into account age, general health status (e.g., body mass index), lifestyle factors (e.g., diet, cigarette smoking), hormone levels, chronic pain syndromes (e.g., chronic prostatitis syndrome, vulvodynia), hormone levels, and medical illnesses that affect vascular, sensory, central, and nervous system functions. In addition, questions regarding past surgeries and injuries, especially those in the genital or pelvic region, and past and current medications should be posed (Levine et al., 2016; Wincze & Carey, 2015). It is commonly understood that many medications, such as antidepressants, antipsychotics, and antihypertensives, can detrimentally affect sexual functioning (Healy et al., 2022).

With respect to individual psychological factors, depression and anxiety are often comorbid with sexual dysfunction. If present and treatment does not target the associated mood disorders, treatment will likely not be successful. Substance abuse disorders may also have a major negative impact on sexual functioning. Certain maladaptive cognitive sets, unrealistic expectations, misinformation or lack of information, and negative emotional reactions can also impinge upon sexual function. Past sexual trauma and other negative experiences may set the foundation for sexual problems as well (Levine et al., 2016; Wincze & Carey, 2015). Socially, family-of-origin attitudes regarding sexuality may be instilled early on and predispose the development of a sexual dysfunction. Assessing the quality of the individual’s current relationship is of utmost importance, as problems between the members of the couple may be a cause and/or a consequence of sexual problems. If so, these issues need to be appropriately addressed. Areas of inquiry related to intimate partnership should include anger, distrust, discrepancies in sexual drive, and preferences, communication, and physical attraction (Levine et al., 2016; Wincze & Carey, 2015). The comorbidity of partner sexual dysfunction is common and should be assessed and addressed if the partner(s) is/are willing to be present in the sessions. In addition, ethnocultural and religious attitudes and beliefs are important as they can be implicated in the development and maintenance of sexual dysfunctions (Hall & Graham, 2013; Levine et al., 2016; Wincze & Carey, 2015).

Questionnaires can be used to formally assess the presence and/or comorbidity of sexual dysfunctions. In addition, information from the client’s treating physician with respect to results from laboratory tests (e.g., for hormone function, vascular integrity, nerve function) and physical examinations (e.g., gynecological) will provide useful information for diagnosis and treatment.

Gender Dysphoria

The most recent guideline for the Standards of Care for Gender Diverse People—published by the World Professional Association for Transgender Health (WPATH) in 2022 (Coleman et al., 2022) and freely available at www.​wpath.​org/​—makes several recommendations for healthcare professionals involved in the care of gender diverse individuals and outlines the process of mental health assessment of individuals with GD. The recommendations for healthcare professionals involved in the care of gender diverse adults are that they be: licensed by their statutory board with relevant training; competent in diagnosing mental health disorders preferably using the most recent edition of the World Health Organization’s International Classification of Diseases (or another taxonomy); able to identify co-existing mental health and other psychosocial issues and distinguish these from GD, incongruence, and diversity; capable of assessing capacity to consent to treatment; experienced or qualified to assess clinical aspects of GD, incongruence, and diversity; continuing their education in health care related to GD, incongruence, and diversity; and open to multidisciplinary consultation and referral with other health care professionals involved in gender-affirming care (Coleman et al., 2022). Additional recommendations are made for mental health care professionals involved in gender-affirming medical and surgical treatment of children and adolescents with GD as well as with other gender-diverse individuals seeking care (e.g., eunuchs) (Coleman et al., 2022).

The role of mental health professionals involved in the care of gender diverse individuals consists of many aspects related to the overall well-being of their clients as well as the implementation of specific recommendations when discussing avenues of gender-affirming medical care. Mental health professionals play a critical role in multidisciplinary healthcare teams by conducting a comprehensive assessment to diagnose GD and addressing mental health symptoms that interfere with a client’s capacity to consent to gender-affirming care. Examples of interfering symptoms are cognitive impairment and the presence of psychosis; it is also important to understand that some mental health conditions may not affect the capacity to provide consent and should not be used as a reason to delay gender-affirming care, especially since such care is associated with decreases in mental health symptoms (Coleman et al., 2022). In addition, mental health professionals address mental health conditions that can interfere with a client’s capacity to engage in essential perioperative care. For example, substance use disorder may impede engagement with appointments, and providing treatment for this condition and support for the client will be necessary for positive surgical outcomes (Coleman et al., 2022). Additional recommendations specific to mental health professionals can be found in the Standards of Care (Coleman et al., 2022).

Paraphilic Disorders

This section will focus on the assessment procedures of adult men with paraphilic disorders. Very few women are identified with paraphilias, especially in forensic populations (for a review, see Cortoni, 2018); in addition, paraphilic disorders are generally not diagnosed in adolescence given that this period of sexual development is believed to be exploratory and fluid, and a premature diagnosis of a paraphilic disorder could pathologize sexual behavior that is within a normal range developmentally (Seto, 2018).

Certain paraphilias and their associated disorders are more likely to be encountered in a forensic setting (e.g., pedophilic disorder), and others are more common in traditional mental health settings (e.g., fetishistic disorder). In either case, the approach to the assessment will be similar, although a forensic setting is likely to be more concerned with broader issues such as risk to the public. In addition, those undergoing court assessments may be less inclined to be forthright than those seeking assessment of their own volition. Regardless, the assessment should be facilitated by a warm, respectful, empathic, genuine, and supportive demeanor on the part of the interviewer (Wilcox & Gray, 2017). The Association for the Treatment of Sexual Abusers (ATSA; http://​www.​atsa.​com/​) offers practice guidelines for psychosexual evaluations, and these guidelines emphasize the need to use multiple sources of information, especially when the assessment is involuntary. Interviews should promote engagement, incorporate the client’s perspective, and consider responsivity factors, which refer to individual characteristics and learning differences that may impact treatment effectiveness. Assessors are encouraged to gather information that includes, but is not limited to, the following: psychosexual development; the nature and frequency of sexual practices; paraphilic interests that may not be sexually abusive; the use of sexually oriented services or outlets (including pornography); abusive or offense-related sexual arousal, interests, and preferences; the history of sexually abusive behaviors; information about current and/or previous victim(s); contextual elements of sexually abusive behaviors; and the individual’s level of insight, self-disclosure, and denial (Association for the Treatment of Sexual Abusers, 2014, pp. 18–21).

Questionnaires

The first and second versions of the Multiphasic Sex Inventory (MSI; Nichols & Molinder, 1984; MSI-II; Nichols & Molinder, 2000) measure a number of characteristics of conventional and paraphilic sexual behaviors. Moreover, both contain a treatment readiness scale that provides an index of an individual’s receptivity to treatment. While the MSI has good psychometric properties (Seto et al., 2014), there are fewer studies regarding the MSI-II and its psychometric properties are less clear (Akerman & Beech, 2012).

The Multidimensional Inventory of Development, Sex, and Aggression (Multidimensional Inventory of Development, Sex, and Aggression (MIDSA), 2011) is a computerized self-assessment inventory developed to assess a range of sexual attitudes, fantasies, and behaviors to assist in clarifying intervention targets to reduce the risk of re-offending. The developers have made available extensive development data (Akerman & Beech, 2012), and the scale has demonstrated acceptable psychometric properties (Seto et al., 2014) and contains four validity indicators.

The Clarke Sexual History Questionnaire-Revised (SHQ-R; Langevin & Paitich, 2002) covers different aspects of conventional and paraphilic sexuality and includes two validity indicators. While it is vulnerable to response bias, Laws et al. (2000) note that such measures can assist in a broader evaluation of sexual proclivities. Moreover, employing a test of impression management such as the Paulhus Deception Scales (PDS; Paulhus, 1999) can be a useful adjunct in assessing impression management response bias and, in the case of the PDS, an individual’s level of personal insight.

The Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI) and subsequent versions (the most recent being the MMPI-3; Ben-Porath & Tellegen, 2020) have been used to assess various aspects of sexuality, including paraphilic sexual behaviors (e.g., Hopkins et al., 2016; VanSlyke, 2018). This inventory has the advantage of well-researched validity scales and a lengthy history of research, making it useful for assessments involving the court system (Ben-Porath, 2012).

Viewing Time

Viewing time (VT) measures (e.g., Abel Assessment of Sexual Interest; Abel et al., 1998) were developed to provide an objective but less intrusive assessment of sexual interests than phallometry (see below). In this approach, a test taker is shown a series of slides depicting pictures of clothed people of varying ages. Viewing time on each slide, measured beyond the test-taker’s awareness, as well as self-reported sexual interest are measured. Interest is measured by response delays induced by the preferred sexual content in stimuli to which the individuals are exposed, with the data-driven assumption that the test-taker will look longer at the images they find to be sexually attractive. Although some believe the weak psychometric properties of these procedures render them better used as a clinical tool than a risk assessment measure (e.g., Akerman & Beech, 2012), results of a meta-analysis demonstrate moderate discrimination between groups of sexual offenders and nonsexual offenders as well as significant convergent validity with other measures assessing sexual preference (Schmidt et al., 2017). Amelung et al. (2024) found that VT could also be useful outside of a forensic context to distinguish individuals presenting with pedo-hebephilia (i.e., sexual interests in pre-pubescent and pubescent children) without a history of offending.

Phallometry

Phallometry involves the direct measurement of penile responses when individuals are exposed to auditory or visual sexual stimuli. These can vary on specific dimensions of interest (e.g., age, gender, and level of coercion). Phallometric responses are recorded as increases in either penile circumference or volume with indices reflecting differential responses to sex-typical and paraphilic themes. Phallometric measures have been well validated, although problems remain with a lack of standardization in terms of procedures and stimuli across evaluation centers. A recent meta-analysis (McPhail et al., 2019) supports the validity of phallometric testing for pedo-hebephilic propensities especially if audio and slides are used; the use of video stimuli was not supported. While concerns about the faking of test responses are well grounded and countermeasures are limited (Wilson & Miner, 2016), McPhail and colleagues stress that the validity of test results can be enhanced through the use of evidence-based practices and standardized procedures.

It is recommended that users be trained in phallometry and familiar with its strengths and limitations because the results often carry a lot of weight (e.g., regarding access to biological children) (Association for the Treatment of Sexual Abusers, 2014). Furthermore, the results of such testing should never be used in isolation but rather be incorporated as part of a broader consideration of assessment information.

Assessing Antisocial Tendencies

Adherence to antisocial values is associated with increased rates of nonsexual recidivism for both sexual and nonsexual offenders (e.g., Witte et al., 2006), and the assessment of such sentiments is generally included as part of a comprehensive evaluation of sexual abusers. In their most extreme form, antisocial tendencies are highly associated with criminal behavior, and, in their extreme, they present as psychopathy, which led Hare (2003) to develop the Psychopathy Checklist-Revised (PCL-R) for forensic assessments. Psychopathy is characterized by a lack of empathy or conscience, manipulative behaviors, deceitfulness, along with impulsive and irresponsible behavior. The PCL-R has become widely used and accepted by forensic psychologists and the courts (Murrie et al., 2012). However, these authors demonstrated that the validity of PCL-R is eroded when users are not rigorously trained. Moreover, a subset of items measuring persistent and versatile criminality and aggression (Facet 4) fared better than all other PCL-R elements.

Criminogenic values have also been linked to an increased risk to engage in antisocial behavior. The Criminal Sentiments Scale (CSC; Andrews & Wormith, 1984) measures attitudes toward the justice system and the degree to which an individual is tolerant of law violations and identifies with other criminals. A modified version (Simourd, 1997) has been shown to be a good predictor of future involvement in nonsexual criminal activity among sexual offenders (Witte et al., 2006).

Risk Assessment

Early risk assessment models (before the mid-1990s) used unstructured professional judgment, which resulted in levels of prediction that were not much better than chance. Since the mid-1990s, tremendous strides in the development of risk assessment scales for sexual offenders have been made (Harris & Hanson, 2010). Bonta and Andrews (2017) summarized an important way of classifying risk factors into static versus dynamic/criminogenic factors, with static factors being historical/demographic factors that do not change over time (e.g., criminal history) and dynamic/criminogenic factors being those that can change and impact recidivism (e.g., sexual preoccupation levels). Not surprisingly, it is easier to gain inter-rater reliability on static tools, as they are based on file review with factors that are either present or absent or have an objective response. Dynamic tools require intensive training on the part of the administrator in combination with the use of structured professional judgment in scoring the responses.

In using any of the risk scales that include dynamic factors, assessors must be mindful of the potential for deceit in the individuals being assessed in their efforts to obtain a favorable evaluation. As noted earlier, Association for the Treatment of Sexual Abusers (2014) practice guidelines stress the need to include diverse, independent sources of information; doing so will greatly increase the validity of the scores obtained on risk assessment scales. If such information is lacking, a note to this effect should be made as a limitation of the assessment.

Static Measures

Globally, the Static-99R (Hanson & Thornton, 2000) is the most commonly used and researched actuarial scale for assessing sexual risk potential (Helmus et al., 2022). The obtained score on this scale is compared with that of a large database of offenders from several countries, and the sexual recidivism rates for those with a similar score can be assessed, predicting recidivism up to 10 years after release into the community. Based on extensive research, the likelihood of recidivism decreases with time spent being offense-free in the community, and the Static-99R allows for an alteration of the original score based on time being offense-free in the community during those 10 years (Hanson et al., 2018). The Static-99R has been validated with many cultural groups and across several countries, but some caution needs to be exercised when using it (Phenix et al., 2016) given that risk assessment tools can perform less well with certain cultural groups (e.g., Indigenous offenders in Canada; Haag et al., 2016).

The Sex Offender Risk Appraisal Guide (SORAG; Quinsey et al., 2006) is also a widely used actuarial scale. The SORAG items include considerations of an individual’s offense history, school adjustment, alcohol use, and the presence of personality disorders, and it incorporates the PCL-R score among other factors. The obtained score is compared to actuarially derived recidivism rates for offenders with similar scores over seven and 10 years. The Violence Risk Appraisal Guide-Revised (VRAG-R; Rice et al., 2013) may be employed to assess the risk of general and sexual violence and may be used in place of the SORAG.

Since its introduction in the mid-1990s, the Minnesota Sex Offender Screening Tool (MnSOST; Epperson et al., 1995) and its most recent version (the MnSOST-4; Duwe, 2019) have been widely used actuarial tools in screening sex offenders to prioritize them for sex offender programs and predicting level of risk, thereby helping to inform the level of community supervision required (Duwe, 2019).

Dynamic/Criminogenic Measures

Hanson et al. (2007) developed the Stable-2007, a standardized, empirically validated dynamic risk assessment measure that includes 13 dynamic risk factors associated with sexual recidivism (e.g., significant social influences, capacity for relationship stability, emotional identification with children). It can be used in conjunction with the Static-99R to determine risk, program priority, and treatment goals. A meta-analysis indicated that the Stable-2007 predicts recidivism on its own and adds predictive accuracy to the Static-99R (Brankley et al., 2021).

Tools Using Both Static and Dynamic Risk Factors

The Violence Risk Scale: Sex Offender Version (VRS:SO; Olver et al., 2007, 2018) contains seven static and 17 dynamic risk factors designed to assess sexual risk, assist in treatment planning, and identify changes in risk as a result of treatment or other reasons. VRS:SO scores have been found to predict sexual recidivism (Olver et al., 2007).

Case Illustrations

Case 1

Kelly (early 20s) was referred to sex therapy by her general practitioner for “painful sex.” Her spouse of 3 years (David, also in his 20s) attended the sex therapy sessions. Kelly and David had abstained from sexual intercourse until their wedding night in keeping with their religious beliefs. In David’s words, “the wedding night was a disaster.” Kelly appeared very nervous and when they tried to have intercourse; she winced and complained of pain when he attempted vaginal penetration. The couple continued their attempts over the next few months and, although penetration eventually became possible, it remained intensely painful for Kelly. She also reported discomfort with tampon insertion, although of lower intensity. They had settled into a pattern of sexual interactions characterized by David’s entreaties to have sex, Kelly’s increasingly anxious attempts to avoid it, and the occasional sexual interaction (about once per month) that was painful for Kelly and unsatisfying for both. They rarely discussed the problem. Kelly reported experiencing sexual desire and arousal during nonpainful sexual activities, but her desire/arousal plummeted when she anticipated or experienced the pain. In terms of their past, both reported supportive families and generally happy childhoods, with no history of physical or sexual abuse. They had no mental health concerns. David reported no sexual concerns, and they both reported a strong and satisfying nonsexual relationship.

Areas targeted with psychometric measures were sexual dysfunction, pain-related experiences, and relationship adjustment. Consistent with the information obtained during the clinical interview, FSFI scores supported frequent pain experiences during insertional sexual activity for Kelly and the IIEF did not reveal evidence of sexual dysfunction in David. A pain scale revealed high pain intensity scores, and her scores on measures of pain catastrophizing and anxiety were very high. The DAS confirmed their report of having a satisfying relationship outside of sexual activity. A referral was made to a gynecologist and a pelvic floor physical therapist with expertise in genitopelvic pain; results were consistent with a diagnosis of provoked vestibulodynia (PVD), a common cause of genitopelvic pain, with considerable hypertonicity (tightness) of the pelvic floor musculature. The findings from this assessment were used to design a multidisciplinary treatment plan that simultaneously targeted all problem areas: genitopelvic pain; feelings of guilt related to, and communication about, sex; hypertonicity; and pain catastrophizing and anxiety.

Case 2

Sarah, a 28-year-old trans woman, was referred to sex therapy by her family doctor to obtain a letter for hormone therapy as this step was the next one in her physical transition to a feminine body. Sarah was designated male at birth, but since early childhood (3–4 years of age) recalled feeling distressed about “being a boy,” the stereotypical male toys that she was expected to play with, and the masculine clothing she was expected to wear. She identified as a girl since the age of 5. In her early school years, she avoided sports and all forms of rough-and-tumble play, played only with girls (and typically feminine toys), preferred to wear her hair long, and wore feminine clothing. When asked what she wanted to be “when she grew up,” Sarah would often respond “a woman.” Although she was often bullied by other children (typically boys), her parents, most other family members, and friends (girls) never questioned her choices. As Sarah approached puberty, she noted a sharp increase in distress (severe level) given the more obvious masculine changes that were occurring in her body, especially the growth of facial hair, the development of a masculine body contour, and the deepening of her voice. Sarah identified as lesbian, and dated several partners throughout high school and beyond, eventually marrying her current partner, Samantha, at the age of 25. Sarah was always open about her gender with her partners, and Samantha was described as supportive of any changes that Sarah opted for in terms of her journey as a woman. Sarah dressed femininely, wore makeup and nail polish, and was working with a voice coach to raise her pitch. She also wore a padded bra to feel feminine. She was “out” in the trans community as well as with all close individuals in her social network, and she felt that she was ready to take the next step in terms of her physical transition (hormone therapy). Her distress level in terms of her physical body and being “misread” by others as male/man was moderate to high. She expressed realistic expectations in terms of the changes that hormone therapy entailed, she understood the risks and benefits of the treatment, and she recognized the reproductive implications of the treatment. She was not interested in surgery at this time.

Upon examining her responses to the GIGDQ, Sarah’s gender identity was that of a woman, and her dysphoria was moderate to high. Her scores on anxiety and depression screening tools were in the moderate range. A letter detailing the diagnosis of GD and including the information required by WPATH was sent to a trans-friendly physician at a local clinic to support Sarah’s request for hormone therapy. In addition, Sarah was given a referral to a trans-friendly mental healthcare professional to develop coping strategies for her depression and anxiety.

Case 3

John (age 56) was convicted of breaching a court order prohibiting contact with children. He babysat a 6-year-old boy who disclosed he had been touched inappropriately on his bottom by John. John had 10 prior sentencing dates over 30 years for a range of offenses; five of these were for sexual offenses. All involved boys aged 7–15. John was raised in an intact family by prosocial parents. However, he said his brother bullied him, which contributed to him acting out in school where his peers ostracized him. Behavioral problems emerged in elementary school and many suspensions resulted. He was expelled in Grade 10, which ended his formal education. Throughout his adult years, John continued to struggle to make friends but enjoyed the support of his parents. He spent most of his free time alone and at home, which he preferred. John reported having been sexually abused by an adult male neighbor over a period of a few months when he was 7 years old. According to John, he did not see this experience as harmful. This abuse ended when the neighbor moved away. John recalled that he always had a “healthy” sex drive and that he often used sexual fantasy or masturbation to cope with his loneliness and sadness. John had never been in a cohabiting relationship with an age-appropriate partner but expressed that he would like to be; he described not knowing how to approach other adults and feeling overwhelmed by the thought of dating. VR and phallometric testing indicated equivalent responses to both pubescent and pre-pubescent boys, a secondary but significant response to consenting men, and a negligible response to girls and women. He described being sexually attracted to men and admitted to an interest in boys aged 14–16. The results of the interview and testing were consistent with the diagnosis of Pedophilic Disorder.

John participated in a treatment program in 2018 where few gains were made. He was described as being unreceptive to feedback and steadfast in asserting his victims “accepted and enjoyed” the abuse. He repeated this belief in the current assessment and stressed that he never pressured them and referred at times to having had a “relationship” with them. In regard to his current breach, John described it was “no big deal.” He denied touching the boy and portrayed his contact with the youth as a benign attempt to help a neighbor who required child care at short notice. He had no insight into his role in engineering these circumstances or the people who gave him access to his victims, and he portrayed his victims as the initiators of the sexual activity.

John’s PCL-R score was below the criterion for designation as a psychopath. John’s scores on the Static-99R and Stable-2007 were consistent with a very strong risk of reoffending. On the Stable-2007, John’s results indicated treatment targets aimed at improving his deficits in forming and maintaining adult relationships, emotional identification with children, general social rejection, sexualized coping, deviant sexual preference, and problems in cooperating with supervision. A high-intensity sex offender program was recommended.

Impact of Gender, Race, Culture, and Other Aspects of Diversity

An extensive discussion of the impact of race, culture, diversity, and age on sexual dysfunctions, gender dysphoria, and paraphilic disorders is beyond the scope of this chapter (refer to Hall & Graham, 2013 for a book focusing on sexuality). In all therapies, an interconnectedness perspective can hold much explanatory and clinical potential. Among other recommendations, Hardy and Laszloffy (2002) encourage therapists to view all therapy as cross-cultural and to engage in a constant process of self-exploration. More practically, it is essential for therapists to work respectfully within clients’ religious and other beliefs and to recognize diversity in experiences, orientations, genders, and sociocultural circumstances. To this end, the DSM-5 Handbook on the Cultural Formulation Interview (Lewis-Fernández et al., 2016) may be a useful resource.

Dos and Don’ts

Dos

  1. 1.

    Ask about sexual problems in a direct manner no matter what the presenting complaint.

     
  2. 2.

    Be open and nonjudgmental.

     
  3. 3.

    Recognize diversity in all aspects of sexual and gender identity.

     
  4. 4.

    Understand and respect the clients’ religious and cultural beliefs.

     
  5. 5.

    Provide normalizing statements and gently correct misinformation.

     
  6. 6.

    Ask about solitary sexual activities in addition to partnered activities.

     
  7. 7.

    Obtain information about fantasies as they may provide additional insight into the presenting complaints.

     
  8. 8.

    Ask questions about activities (e.g., anal sex) that are important for sexual health.

     
  9. 9.

    Ask about partner communication and the partner’s responses to the presenting complaint.

     
  10. 10.

    If working with a sex offender, gain information from as many sources as possible in order to obtain as comprehensive a history as possible.

     
  11. 11.

    Refer to a medical doctor for physical tests, laboratory investigations, and treatment.

     
  12. 12.

    If the individual is partnered, encourage the participation of partner(s) in assessment and treatment.

     

Don’ts

  1. 1.

    Don’t let lack of experience or discomfort with sexual issues prevent you from at least asking some screening questions about sexual dysfunctions; open the door for the client to discuss this topic and see if a referral may be needed if you do not feel competent. Most people will not spontaneously bring up a sexual problem. Direct questions must be asked.

     
  2. 2.

    Don’t assume that you know everything about the client and their fantasies, sexual orientation, relationship status, sexual experiences, gender identity, etc.

     
  3. 3.

    Don’t assume that sex and gender are binary concepts.

     
  4. 4.

    Don’t assume that older people, single people, disabled people, etc., are not sexual or sexually active.

     
  5. 5.

    In the case of sex offenders or individuals convicted of sexual crimes, don’t believe everything they tell you as they may want to mislead the clinician into thinking that they are functioning at higher or lower levels depending on the situation.

     

Summary

There are a number of disorders related to sexuality, gender, and paraphilias, and each must be carefully assessed and characterized. At a minimum, a single question about any sexual concern is necessary to potentially raise an issue that the patient may want to address but feels too embarrassed to spontaneously report.
