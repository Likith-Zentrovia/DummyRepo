Description of the Disorders

What Are Eating Disorders?

The term “eating disorders” (EDs) broadly refers to psychiatric disorders marked by maladaptive eating patterns and attitudes about eating, body shape, and body weight. The four ED diagnoses described in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5-TR; American Psychiatric Association, 2022) include anorexia nervosa (AN), bulimia nervosa (BN), binge-eating disorder (BED), and “other specified feeding or eating disorder” (OSFED).

Approximately 82% of all EDs will onset by age 25 (Solmi et al., 2022). Age-of-onset varies by ED, with age of onset for AN typically occurring in early-to-middle adolescence and age-of-onset for BN and BED occurring in the late teenage years to young adulthood. Prevalence rates for the DSM-5-TR EDs in adolescents are 5.7% in females and 1.2% in males (Smink et al., 2014). Epidemiologic research conducted in Australia reported a 3-month point prevalence rate of 16.3% for any DSM-5-TR ED, with most ED cases accounted for by BED and OSFED (Hay et al., 2015). Research in the United States found a combined ED prevalence of 13.1% by age 20 (Stice et al., 2013). Thus, in contrast to views of EDs as rare psychiatric disorders, they are relatively common in the general population. In the following sections, the DSM-5-TR diagnostic criteria for EDs are described.

Anorexia Nervosa

AN is characterized by restriction of energy intake that results in a significantly low body weight, given a person’s age, sex, and other developmental and physical health factors (Criterion A). Persons with AN may display an intense fear of weight gain or engage in persistent behaviors to avoid weight gain (Criterion B). People with AN may report a distorted perception of their weight and body shape, deny the seriousness of their low body weight, or base their self-evaluation on their weight or shape (Criterion C). It is important to note that a person with AN may not report concerns with body image or perceptual disturbance but may still meet criteria if there are persistent behaviors that are designed to prevent weight gain (e.g., excessive exercise, restricting food intake, etc.) and the person does not think that their low weight is a problem.

Body weight is typically assessed in adults by calculating a person’s body mass index (BMI); for children and adolescents, additional information conveyed by BMI-for-age percentiles should be considered to account for growth trajectories. The DSM-5-TR does not provide a specific BMI cutoff for defining “significantly low body weight.” However, BMI cut-offs are included in the new DSM-5-TR severity categories for AN that define severity based on BMI thresholds that range from mild to extreme.

The DSM-5-TR specifies two subtypes for AN: (1) restricting, in which no binge eating or purging behaviors occur, and (2) binge-eating/purging, in which the person engages in binge eating and/or purging behaviors, such as self-induced vomiting, laxative, and/or diuretic misuse.

Bulimia Nervosa

BN is characterized by repeated episodes of binge eating and inappropriate compensatory behavior at least once per week (on average) for a period of 3 months. The person must also report that their self-evaluation is unduly influenced by their shape and weight. An individual cannot concurrently meet the criteria for AN and BN. Thus, significantly low body weight is an exclusion criterion for BN and an inclusion criterion for AN.

An objective binge-eating episode refers to a discrete period (e.g., within 2 hours) in which an individual consumes an objectively large amount of food, in addition to experiencing a sense of loss-of-control (LOC). Although there are no caloric guidelines for what is considered objectively large, the DSM-5-TR indicates that the amount eaten needs to be “definitely larger” than what most other people would eat under similar circumstances. Interviewers may also want to consider the person’s body size and height, gender, and physical activity patterns when making judgments of whether the eating episodes are objectively large. LOC may be expressed by individuals in terms that include, but are not limited to, feeling as though binge-episodes were inevitable, like they could not stop eating once they started (e.g., like a ball rolling down a hill), or that they could not control what or how much was consumed. However, this is not an exhaustive list, and clients may have other lay terminology to describe their own subjective experience of LOC. For example, Forbush et al. (2013) found that people who experience binge eating may characterize LOC as mild, transient dissociation (e.g., not realizing how much they were eating until after they had finished or feeling that they were on “autopilot”). See the dialogue for an example of how to assess for binge-eating episodes.

Inappropriate compensatory behaviors (ICBs) are categorized into purging and non-purging methods. Purging ICBs refer to the forced expelling of food from the body (through means such as self-induced vomiting, laxatives, diuretics) or misuse of certain substances (e.g., insulin omission in persons with Type I diabetes or misuse of thyroid medicine to prevent weight gain or compensate for an eating episode). Non-purging ICBs refer to non-purging behaviors that are used to compensate for binge-episodes, such as by excessive exercise or caloric restriction.

Binge Eating Disorder

BED requires that an individual engage in recurrent episodes of binge eating (see above) in the absence of low body weight and ICBs. In addition, an individual must meet criteria for three out of five associated features for BED, such as (1) eating much more rapidly than normal, (2) eating until uncomfortably full, (3) eating large amounts of food when not physically hungry, (4) eating alone due to embarrassment associated with how much one is eating, and (5) feeling disgusted, depressed, or guilty after a binge. Finally, binge eating must cause marked distress.

Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorders

The DSM-5-TR also includes a category called “OSFED” to encompass ED presentations that cause significant distress or impairment but do not meet diagnostic criteria for AN, BN, or BED. OSFED is the most common ED diagnosis. OSFED does not necessarily imply that the person’s ED is less severe, but rather encompasses the large proportion of serious ED presentations that do not cleanly fit into the other diagnostic categories. A meta-analysis of mortality rates in the various ED diagnoses identified similar mortality rates in OSFED compared to full-threshold EDs (Solmi et al., 2024).

The DSM-5-TR provides five specific examples of presentations that fall within the OSFED category. For example, atypical AN (atypAN) is an OSFED characterized by significant, substantial weight loss (not better explained by a medical condition); yet, despite the significant weight loss, the individual’s weight is within or above a minimally healthy range. Given that the person with atypAN may have developed their illness at a higher baseline weight, this diagnosis is provided to intervene earlier in the illness course, rather than waiting until the person reaches a significantly low body weight. Purging disorder is an OSFED in which an individual engages in recurrent purging behavior to influence their weight or shape in the absence of objective binge-eating episodes. Night eating syndrome is an OSFED characterized by recurrent consumption of food after waking up from sleep or the excessive consumption of food following an evening meal. Notably, the individual has full awareness and recollection of food consumption, which differentiates it from parasomnias such as sleep-related eating disorders. Other OSFEDs include BN and BED of lower frequency or duration. Lastly, in addition to the DSM-5-TR examples of OSFEDs, compensatory eating disorder is a newly proposed OSFED in which a person engages in recurrent non-purging compensatory behavior (e.g., excessive exercise and/or restriction) to influence their weight/shape in the absence of objective binge eating episodes (Negi et al., 2023).

Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder

Although Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder (ARFID) is designated in the DSM-5-TR as a feeding disorder, not an ED, we included ARFID in this chapter because cases of ARFID are often seen in ED treatment settings. ARFID, which can occur within individuals of all ages, is characterized by avoidant or restrictive eating behavior resulting in substantial weight loss or nutritional deficiency or marked psychosocial impairment. An important indicator of ARFID is that the restrictive eating behaviors are not driven by weight or body image concerns. Three common features (though not exhaustive) that can contribute to disturbances in eating among individuals with ARFID include: (1) an apparent lack of interest in eating; (2) an avoidance based on the sensory characteristics (e.g., smells, tastes, textures, temperatures, and appearances) of food; and (3) a concern about the aversive consequences of eating, such as choking or contracting an illness or disease from food.

Due to the consequences of nutritional deficiency in both AN and ARFID, there are considerable similarities in how these two disorders present clinically (Kambanis & Thomas, 2023). It is important to note, however, that body-image disturbances and persistent behaviors to prevent weight gain are not a component of the ARFID diagnosis in the DSM-5-TR. For this reason, it can be particularly difficult to differentiate cases of ARFID from cases of AN in which symptoms of fat phobia are absent. To make a definitive diagnosis of ARFID, the clinician must rule out AN and BN, and ARFID symptoms should not be attributable to cultural practices, a lack of food resources, or a medical condition that better explains low weight.

Procedures for Gathering Information

EDs are complex psychiatric disorders that, if left untreated, result in substantial psychiatric and medical morbidity and high mortality rates (Nielsen & Vilmar, 2021). Given that EDs have the second highest mortality rate of any mental disorder after opioid-use disorders (Chesney et al., 2014), proper assessment and early diagnosis are essential. Procedures for clinical interviewing should be given careful consideration, including identifying the problem areas, establishing rapport, and providing appropriate clinical recommendations (see Table 13.1 for “Dos and Don’ts”). In addition to utilizing well-validated self-report and interview-based tools to ensure proper diagnosis, it is important for clinicians to include the following additional components in their unstructured clinical interviews: (1) weight and dieting history; (2) current and past patterns of disordered-eating behaviors and cognitions [including binge eating, ICBs, and body dissatisfaction (including desire for greater muscularity)]; (3) history of weight-based teasing or bullying; (4) social support and relationships; (5) suicidal ideation, self-harm, and suicide attempt histories; and (6) treatment history.

Dos and don’ts

Do: Establish positive rapport. Many ED behaviors may be associated with embarrassment, guilt, shame, and/or secrecy. Moreover, treatment-seeking rates for individuals with EDs are low; thus, it is not uncommon for an ED assessment to be the first time an individual has discussed their ED thoughts and behaviors with someone

Do: Take a nonjudgmental stance when assessing ED symptoms. Try to use language that normalizes disordered thoughts and behaviors. For example, when asking someone to describe what they ate during a binge-eating episode, it may be helpful to ask, “What else?” (which assumes there is more food that was eaten), rather than “Anything else?” (which subtly implies that there should not be more food that was eaten). It can be helpful to reassure the person being assessed that you would not know to ask these questions about their experience if ED issues were not experienced by many people

Do: Work on your own internalized weight bias. Our society places value on thinness for women and leanness (muscularity) for men and any internalized bias (even unconscious bias) that you may have toward people of higher body weights could be conveyed to your client, hindering your ability to do an effective interview

Do: Be mindful of your facial expressions and reactions during an interview. Sometimes binge-eating episodes can be quite a bit larger than what most people would eat under similar circumstances (e.g., a full jar of peanut butter and loaf of bread) or seem un-palatable (e.g., raw hot dogs). Keeping a neutral expression and familiarizing yourself with the full range of eating behaviors can help you to avoid passing judgment. Maintaining a nonjudgmental stance, in turn, can help you get better quality information from your client, which will improve your ability to assess, diagnose, and create a strong treatment plan

Do: Assess associated impairment in addition to specific ED symptoms. Assessing clinical impairment (e.g., how symptoms impact one’s ability to engage in interpersonal relationships, at work or school, etc.) gives a more comprehensive description of severity. Although the DSM-5-TR provides a categorical system through which AN, BN, and BED severity can be quantified, clinical impairment should also be considered. Measures such as the Clinical Impairment Assessment (CIA; Bohn et al., 2008) or the World Health Organization Disability Assessment Schedule (WHODAS 2.0) may be informative

Do: Establish a timeline to describe when symptoms began and for what duration they occurred. For ED diagnoses, the presence or absence of a single symptom for a period of time can influence diagnosis (e.g., the presence or absence of ICBs influences whether a diagnosis of BN or BED is appropriate)

Do: Get specific details of an individual’s restricting and/or binge-eating episodes. For instance, someone may be able to estimate how many cups or teaspoons of food they ate, or they may be able to provide the brand name of a food that can then be researched to provide additional information. Additionally, do not assume that binge-eating episodes must feature “junk food.” Many persons who binge eat consume “healthy foods” during their binge-eating episodes (e.g., eating an entire package of baby carrots). Consider the volume and amount of food relative to what most other people would eat in a similar situation when evaluating binge-eating episodes. Finally, consider a person’s energy balance needs when evaluating binge-eating and restricting episodes. A 6′4″ man who exercises for 2 hours/day will require more energy from food than a woman who does not exercise and is 5′0″ tall. Similarly, a woman of 5′4″ who weighs 400 pounds will require more energy to maintain her weight than a woman of 5′4″ who weighs 135 pounds. In other words, what constitutes a large amount of food will vary depending on the person’s specific energy input and output

Do: Be sure to ask about the context of a person’s binge-eating or restricting episodes. For example, persons who observe Ramadan practice fasting; yet, this does not preclude these periods of fasting to be considered inappropriate compensatory behavior (in addition to religious practices). Comprehensive questioning, such as clarifying if the individual is motivated to engage in this behavior in any part because of weight/shape impact, can help elucidate ED behaviors that may accompany other, day-to-day behaviors. An additional example of the importance of context might be an instance in which an individual reports binge eating on a holiday (such as Thanksgiving) or at a particular restaurant where portions are typically quite large (such as the Cheesecake Factory); as it would normative for a person to eat an objectively large amount under such circumstances, it can be helpful to consider what amount of food would be reasonable given the setting

Do: Be patient! EDs can significantly impact a person’s insight and ability to describe their experience. Keep in mind that certain aspects of EDs, such as overvaluation of weight and shape, can be challenging to assess. Individuals may have limited insight into these symptoms or may not respond to a particular way of evaluating these concepts. For example, some individuals may not endorse loss-of-control (LOC) during their binge-eating episodes yet describe a sense of inevitability that they would consume a large amount of food; the latter fulfills the LOC criterion for binge-eating episodes and represents an alternative way to characterize LOC. Certain phrasing and descriptions of ED concepts may resonate with some individuals more so than others

Don’t: Do not make assumptions about a person’s ED based on his or her appearance (including body weight or shape). EDs occur in persons of all ethnicities, ages, genders, and body sizes. We simply cannot know whether a person has an ED based on their appearance

Don’t: Do not assume a person’s current ED presentation has been their primary lifetime ED presentation. For example, if a person currently meets criteria for bulimia nervosa and indicates that there was a time in which they were binge eating without engaging in ICBs, binge ED history should also be considered. Furthermore, if a person currently engages in non-purging ICBs (e.g., fasting), this does not preclude them from having a history of purging behaviors (e.g., laxative misuse)

Don’t: Do not forget to consider important factors such as gender, cultural background, and other individual differences when formulating your case conceptualization. In special populations such as athletes, for example, the same amount of exercise that would be deemed “excessive” in an average person may not be considered excessive given the demands of their sport training

We find it useful to collaboratively develop a timeline of the ED behaviors and cognitions that begins with the person’s first eating disorder behavior until the present time. The interviewer should attempt to gather information on the temporal relationships between past eating disorder behaviors and weight fluctuations. For example, if an individual has a history of dieting, the interviewer will want to know how much weight was lost after the first attempt to diet, over what period of time the diet occurred, how much was consumed during that time, what other behaviors occurred during and after the diet ceased, and how the behaviors and thoughts affected their day-to-day functioning, social relationships, and their self-image. The interviewer will also want to obtain the client’s lowest and highest body weights for their height (excluding pregnancy) and inquire about the eating behaviors, ICBs, and physical activity patterns the person was engaging in during that time. Obtaining a full weight history is important because people often “migrate” among ED diagnoses over time (e.g., from AN to BN) without recovering. Thus, obtaining information on weight history will enable the clinician to see the “full picture” with respect to the scope and severity of the ED. Throughout the diagnostic interviewing process, the interviewer should work to remain nonjudgmental, supportive, and collaborative, while also obtaining detailed information necessary to make an accurate diagnosis and develop an effective treatment plan. Given that lay definitions of various ED behaviors differ from clinical and diagnostic definitions, we encourage the clinician to err on the side of obtaining objective information and informant reports, when appropriate.

Finally, it is important that clinicians are sensitive to the physical and cognitive needs of clients with EDs. Clinics that see people with EDs should be able to accommodate a full range of body shapes and sizes. For example, there should be sufficient seat padding or extra cushions to prevent people with low body weights from bruising and bariatric-sized chairs for people with higher body weights. Keep in mind how embarrassing it would feel if you were a client who was seeking help for your eating and body-image issues, and you could not fit through the clinic front doors, or you sat down and broke a clinic chair! You will also want to be mindful of the fact that clients who are malnourished may be slow to respond to questions or be forgetful. It is important to note that malnutrition is not perfectly aligned with body weight—many clients who are severely restricting their food intake (including clients living in larger bodies) may experience cognitive effects associated with inadequate caloric intake. Thus, depending on the client’s needs, the clinician may want to break up the intake session into a few shorter sessions.

Recommendations for Formal Assessment

We highly recommend that clinicians include some type of formal assessment (in addition to unstructured clinical interviewing) as a part of their routine assessment practice for clients with an ED. Approximately 30% of disorders can be missed by the interviewer if unstructured interviews are administered without any formal diagnostic interview (Zimmerman & Mattia, 1999). Other research indicates that early response in the first month of treatment as defined by 2.88% weight gain (for persons with AN) or ≥70% reduction in binge eating or purging (for persons with BED or BN) is one of the only prognostic indicators for end-of-treatment response (Chang et al., 2021). Thus, formal assessment may help clinicians better understand the full diagnostic picture to inform case conceptualization, identify prognosis by monitoring early response, and make evidence-based decisions about the optimal timing and necessity of stepped care. Overall, clinicians who engage in routine assessment of cognitions and behaviors can treat clients faster and more effectively (Lewis et al., 2019). Clinicians who are routinely assessing at intake and throughout treatment, therefore, have a better likelihood of seeing meaningful improvements in their clients.

Given that many people with an ED have a comorbid mental-health condition (Hambleton et al., 2022) and/or a lifetime history of non-suicidal self-injury, suicidal thoughts and behaviors, and suicide attempts (Sohn et al., 2023), we recommend assessing non-ED psychopathology (e.g., depression, anxiety, obsessive-compulsive disorder, substance misuse) in addition to ED-specific psychopathology.

The selected measures (see below) do not represent an exhaustive list of assessments from which to choose. Clinicians should use their judgment of individual client’s needs and up-to-date information on the reliability and validity of a measure to make selections about which assessment to use for a particular client.

Self-Report and Screening Measures

The Eating Disorder Examination-Questionnaire (EDE-Q; Fairburn & Beglin, 1994) is a 28-item self-report version of the Eating Disorder Examination (EDE) interview (see below). The EDE-Q measures constructs that are targeted in Enhanced Cognitive Behavior Therapy for EDs (CBT-E; Fairburn et al., 2003), including Restraint, Eating Concern, Weight Concern, and Shape Concern. Thus, the EDE-Q is a valuable tool for clinicians interested in monitoring changes in relevant CBT-E constructs over the course of therapy. Strengths of the EDE-Q include strong reliability and internal consistency. However, recent research has shown that the factor structure of the EDE-Q has not replicated well across samples (Rand-Giovannetti et al., 2020). In addition, the Weight Concern and Shape Concern subscales may not be comparable across sexes, due to measurement invariance (Rand-Giovannetti et al., 2020). The EDE-Q is free for non-commercial research or clinical use (https://​www.​corc.​uk.​net/​media/​1273/​ede-q_​quesionnaire.​pdf).

The Eating Disorder Inventory-3 (EDI-3; Garner, 2004) is a 91-item self-report measure that is licensed by Psychological Assessment Resources. The EDI-3 takes approximately 20 min to administer and 20 min to score (although interested clinicians can purchase an online scoring package). Three scales—Drive for Thinness, Body Dissatisfaction, and Bulimia—directly pertain to EDs, whereas the other nine scales assess correlated psychopathology (e.g., Maturity Fears, Perfectionism, Emotion Dysregulation, etc.). Although the EDI-3 has good reliability and convergent validity (Brookings et al., 2021), the three-factor structure does not replicate in African American women (Rothstein et al., 2017). The EDI-3 has limited discriminant validity from depression (Anderson et al., 1999). The EDI-3 has substantial normative data available, computerized scoring, and it provides useful supplementary materials (e.g., ED risk scores and ability to purchase a self-report diagnostic measure).

The Eating Pathology Symptoms Inventory (EPSI; Forbush et al., 2013) is a 45-item self-report measure that assesses Body Dissatisfaction, Binge Eating, Cognitive Restraint, Muscle Building, Purging, Restricting, Excessive Exercise, and Negative Attitudes toward Obesity. The EPSI is freely available for non-commercial research or clinical use [although any adaptations must be approved in writing by the copyright holder (Dr. Kelsie Forbush) prior to use]. Strengths of the EPSI include strong psychometric properties in men and women, persons with high and low body weights, and a stable factor structure (Forbush et al., 2013). The EPSI has strong support for its clinical utility, reliability, and validity. To download the EPSI, go to: https://​care.​ku.​edu/​sites/​care/​files/​files/​1.​pdf.

The SCOFF (Morgan et al., 2000) is a five-item self-report tool that is designed to screen for the possible presence of an ED. The content assessed in the SCOFF includes: (1) making oneself “sick” (i.e., self-induced vomiting) due to eating large amounts of food; (2) losing control over eating; (3) losing more than one stone (i.e., 15 pounds) over a 3-month period; (4) feeling one is “fat” when others disagree; and (5) endorsing the belief that food dominates one’s life. Individuals who provide affirmative answers to two or more questions on the SCOFF should be referred for a comprehensive ED assessment. Past research has shown that the SCOFF has high specificity (>0.90) and adequate sensitivity (>0.50) for screening EDs (Solmi et al., 2015). A disadvantage of the SCOFF is the relatively high false-positive rate.

The Brief Assessment of Stress and Eating (Forbush et al., 2022) is 10-item measure to identify individuals who may have an ED. The BASE has adequate to strong psychometric properties, including high sensitivity (0.87) and adequate specificity (0.58) (Forbush et al., 2022). A strength is that the BASE is more accurate at detecting an ED in men compared to the SCOFF.

Structured and Semi-structured Interviews

General Interviews

The Structured Clinical Interview for DSM-5-TR Disorders (SCID; First & Williams, 2016) is a semi-structured interview that assesses most of the common mental disorders. Although the SCID for DSM-5-TR disorders includes a module to assess feeding and EDs, it may be worthwhile for the clinician to administer an additional ED-specific interview, given that there are many aspects of ED psychopathology that are not measured in the SCID (e.g., internalized weight stigma, body checking, frequency of self-weighing) or that are not assessed comprehensively (e.g., excessive exercise). However, the SCID provides coverage of mood, anxiety, obsessive-compulsive, psychotic, and substance use disorders (and other disorders), providing important diagnostic breadth. A limitation of the SCID is that it can be challenging to learn to administer correctly, particularly if the clinician is not familiar with a broad range of psychopathology presentations. Thus, clinicians who wish to administer the SCID should receive appropriate training and supervision prior to use.

The Mini-International Neuropsychiatric Interview (MINI; Sheehan et al., 1998) is a structured diagnostic interview that provides coverage of 17 common psychiatric disorders and has been translated into 70 languages. The MINI takes approximately 15 min to administer, making it a widely used instrument in clinical settings and epidemiological research. The MINI includes AN, BN, and BED, but does not directly assess OSFEDs.

Eating Disorder Specific Interviews

The Eating Disorder Examination (EDE; Cooper & Fairburn, 1987) is a semi-structured interview that assesses ED diagnostic criteria and other relevant constructs. There are four EDE sub-scales: Eating Concern, Weight Concern, Shape Concern, and Restraint; the EDE also features behavioral items to assess specific ED behaviors (e.g., binge eating, purging, etc.). The EDE is a free, downloadable measure available from https://​www.​credo-oxford.​com/​pdfs/​EDE_​17.​0D.​pdf. The EDE is an “investigator-based interview,” which means that the interviewer should have training in ED-relevant concepts and diagnostic criteria. There is evidence for test–retest reliability over periods of 1–2 weeks for EDE sub-scales and most behavioral items; there is also evidence for inter-rater reliability and internal consistency (Thomas et al., 2014). The factor structure of the EDE has not been consistently replicated, and there is mixed evidence for the EDE’s construct validity (for a review, see Thomas et al., 2014).

The Eating Pathology Symptoms Inventory—Clinician Rated Version (EPSI-CRV; Forbush et al., 2020) is a semi-structured interview that was created based on the EPSI self-report measure (Forbush et al., 2013). The EPSI-CRV was designed to be used with less training than the EDE. The EPSI-CRV includes the eight dimensions of the self-reported version of the EPSI as well as additional modules that are used to formulate current (past 3 months) ED diagnoses. The EPSI-CRV enables the diagnosis of multiple forms of EDs, including OSFED. The EPSI-CRV showed evidence for interrater reliability, convergent and discriminant validity, predictive, and criterion-related validity for distinguishing ED diagnoses (Forbush et al., 2020). The EPSI-CRV is a free, downloadable instrument that includes a set of free training videos and suggested readings (see https://​kuscholarworks.​ku.​edu/​handle/​1808/​29616).

The Eating Disorder Assessment for DSM-5-TR (EDA-5; Sysko et al., 2015) is a semi-structured interview for the assessment of DSM-5-TR feeding and EDs. The computerized version, available from https://​eda5.​org/​, uses an algorithm to reduce the number of questions asked of interviewees; the paper-and-pencil and computerized versions had an excellent diagnostic agreement with one another (kappa = 0.83). The EDA-5 demonstrated diagnostic agreement with the EDE (kappa = 0.74) and evidence for test–retest reliability over a 7–17-day period (kappa = 0.87; Sysko et al., 2015). The EDA-5 is free, although researchers and clinicians with access to funds can make voluntary contributions to support the upkeep of the measure.

Medical Assessment

If psychological assessment suggests an ED, there are specific physical assessments that should be performed by a trained medical professional. Vital signs, including orthostatic blood pressure and heart rate, temperature, and last menstrual period (for individuals born biological women who are of childbearing age) should be performed at each medical visit. Changes in orthostatic vital signs could indicate acute dehydration or chronic malnutrition. In the adolescent population, blood pressure changes are not often seen, but heart rate changes are common. Malnourishment may present with a low heart rate (bradycardia), low body temperature (hypothermia), or prolonged missed periods (secondary amenorrhea).

During history-taking it is important to ask about symptoms that are related to a state of starvation. All organs can be affected during this state, but early and most common complaints include gastrointestinal (GI) complications such as constipation, slowing of the stomach (gastroparesis), bloating, or reflux; cold or bluish coloration of the skin (cyanotic extremities); dizziness or fainting; infrequent menstruation (oligomenorrhea); fatigue or difficulty concentrating. In adolescents, other common presentations to the pediatrician include a decrease in athletic ability or an unexpected drop in grades.

The physical examination should include aspects that incorporate the above complaints paying particular attention to the GI and cardiovascular systems. The integumentary system is also important to monitor as lack of subcutaneous fat can result in frequent bruising or pressure ulcers, which usually have delayed healing time and are, therefore, at risk for infection. It is important to note that because bone marrow suppression can be seen during a state of malnutrition, patients may not present with typical signs and symptoms of an infection, so there should be a low threshold to treat. In adolescents who are premenarchal, it is essential to determine pubertal progression so menarche can be accurately anticipated, as it can be delayed due to malnutrition. For those who use vomiting as a method of purging, additional signs on exam include callouses on the back of the hand (Russell’s sign), swollen salivary glands (parotiditis), and a swollen or red throat. The patient may also have fluid retention or marked diuresis, so utilizing weight gain as a marker of progress can be misleading.

Monitoring physical health through laboratory and radiology findings is essential. Ongoing medical complications of EDs include electrolyte abnormalities that may reflect water loading (i.e., intentionally drinking high volumes of water to increase body weight), vomiting, laxatives or diuretic use, arrhythmias, and osteopenia or osteoporosis due to chronic bone loss. Treatment should be focused on re-nourishment, using “food as your medicine.” Patients can be treated symptomatically to help alleviate discomfort during the refeeding process. Laxatives are not encouraged due to the high rate of abuse, but polyethylene glycol is a viable option. Patients need to be followed closely during their recovery, as acute complications and need for hospitalization are common. Thus, therapists should work closely with a medical professional who has experience treating patients who have EDs.

Finally, the Academy for Eating Disorders (AED) developed a free, user-friendly medical care standards guide, that is useful for therapists and medical professionals alike (https://​www.​aedweb.​org/​resources/​publications/​medical-care-standards). We highly recommend all providers who work with clients with an ED familiarize themselves with the guide.

Case Illustrations

Dialogue About Binge-Eating Episodes

Therapist: You mentioned that there have been times when you felt as though your eating was out of control. What do you typically eat during these eating episodes?

Sarah: Umm…I don’t know. [Long pause] …I guess a little of everything.

Therapist: A little of everything; okay. What are some foods you typically eat?

Sarah: Pizza, ice cream, candy…ugh, I feel so gross.

Therapist: I’m here to support you and not pass judgment about your eating habits. I wouldn’t even know to ask about your eating habits if other people didn’t struggle with similar eating issues. I can imagine that it must be uncomfortable sharing this information with me, particularly because we don’t yet know each other well, and please know that it does not change my view of you in the slightest.

Sarah: Thank you. I’ve never told anyone about these binges before, so it’s hard to talk about.

Therapist: I really appreciate your willingness to talk with me, especially because it’s something that you’ve held inside for so long. Do you mind if I ask more specific questions to try to get a better picture of your eating habits? That might help us work together to come up with a good plan to figure out how to address the food issues that you said you’d like to change.

Sarah: Sure. That makes sense.

--

The therapist used several strategies to support the client in opening up about her binge-eating episodes. The therapist assured the patient that although it is uncomfortable to discuss binge-eating episodes, the therapist would not judge her for the amount or types of foods she ate. The therapist also did not refer to the binge-eating episodes as “binges” until the client had done so, which is helpful if the client does not perceive their eating to be “binge eating.”

--

Therapist: You said you eat pizza; what type of pizza do you typically eat?

Sarah: My go-to is to order pizza for delivery.

Therapist: What pizza place do you tend to order from?

Sarah: I usually get Pizza Hut pizza.

Therapist: What size and what type of toppings?

Sarah: My usual order is a large, thin crust with pepperoni and sausage, and breadsticks.

Therapist: How much will you typically eat?

Sarah: The whole pizza and four or five breadsticks, usually.

Therapist: Typically, more like four or more like five breadsticks?

Sarah: Probably five. I’ll sometimes also have ice cream.

Therapist: What kind?

Sarah: Cookie dough. I usually get the generic brand.

Therapist: How much of the ice cream will you have?

Sarah: The whole container.

Therapist: What size container?

Sarah: Just the standard pint-size.

Therapist: Okay, so Pizza Hut pizza and breadsticks, and cookie dough ice cream. What else?

Sarah: Candy, sometimes. Usually Snickers, and I’ll eat one of those regular-sized bars.

Therapist: What else?

Sarah: That’s it.

Therapist: And over what period of time do you eat these foods?

Sarah: It’s usually on my lunch break, so an hour.

Therapist: Thank you for going through that with me. I would imagine it could be difficult to discuss the binges if you’ve never told anyone about them before. Just know I really appreciate you telling me because it helps me get a clear sense of your eating patterns so that we can later come up with a plan together to address them.

--

The therapist used several more strategies to ask about the binge-eating episodes. You can see the level of detail the therapist asked for in detailing the foods consumed. A useful guideline is to get enough detail that you could go to the store (or restaurant) and recreate the exact meal or snack. With some patients, using a portion or measurement guide may be helpful (i.e., helping them to describe their eating episodes with descriptors like cups, teaspoons, and ounces or indicate portion sizes using their hands). The reason for obtaining such detailed information on eating episodes is because the size of the episode (small or typical meal or snack vs. an amount that is definitely large) determines whether the person should be diagnosed with purging disorder, compensatory eating disorder, BN, or BED.

Another helpful strategy is to use the phrase, “What else?” in asking about foods consumed in the binge-eating episode. This phrasing tells the patient that you expect there to be more foods consumed, as opposed to the phrasing, “Anything else?” which can suggest to the client that the amount of food he or she has described is already large enough. In this dialogue, the therapist asked, “What else?” until the patient reported being finished describing the eating episode. The therapist provided positive reinforcement (praise) to the patient for discussing the binge-eating episodes and reiterated the rationale for why this information was sought.

Dialogue About Loss of Control Over Eating

Therapist: You’ve described episodes of overeating when you’ll have five peanut butter sandwiches, which include 10 slices of white bread and 15 ounces of peanut butter. Do you feel a sense of loss-of-control over your eating during these times?

Will: Not really, no.

Therapist: Do you feel like you could’ve stopped eating once you started?

Will: Yeah, I planned to have a binge, so I just let myself go on until I was full.

Therapist: So, this was a planned event. Did you feel it was inevitable you’d eat that amount?

Will: I guess so, because I had planned it out. I’ve just been overeating like this for so long that I’m kind of numb to it now. It’s just something I plan to do. When it first started, it felt way more out-of-control. Now I just plan it into my week because I know it’s going to happen.

--

The therapist asked about LOC and the patient denied having that experience during his recent binge-eating episodes. The therapist then asked follow-up questions to inquire about LOC in different ways that might resonate with the patient (e.g., feeling like he couldn’t stop eating once he had started and feeling it was inevitable that he would eat that amount of food). Each of these strategies was used to assess LOC, which the patient eventually endorsed. In our experience, sometimes the sense of being out-of-control over binge-eating episodes may diminish over the course of the ED illness (e.g., the client eventually plans to have a binge-eating episode each Friday night, so the planned episode does not feel as out-of-control). In situations in which it is unclear whether the client experienced LOC, it can help to ask open-ended questions to elicit their thoughts and feelings during the eating episode. The take-home message is that even if LOC is mild, it is still considered present.

Dialogue About Exercise

Therapist: Do you ever exercise or participate in sports?

Betsy: Yes, but I usually exercise to maintain a healthy heart…not necessarily to lose weight.

Therapist: Being healthy is important to you. Tell me more about what a typical exercise session looks like for you.

Betsy: I have a job as a fitness instructor, so I usually teach classes during the week.

Therapist: Okay, what types of classes do you teach and how long do they last?

Betsy: I teach a cardio aerobics class and sometimes yoga. Classes are typically 1 hour with a 5-minute warm-up and a 5-minute cool-down.

Therapist: I’m familiar with some of those group classes. I also know that some instructors differ in how much they actually exercise in the class. Some instructors don’t do all the exercises, or they use really light weights. They might walk around to give modifications and additional instructions to folks. Other instructors use class time to get their own work out in and do all the exercises. How would you describe your teaching style?

Betsy: Oh, I do all the exercises. I push myself pretty hard. My goal is to encourage my classes to test their physical limits and to get stronger. I have to model working hard for them.

Therapist: You want everyone to get a great workout in. How many classes do you typically teach in a day?

Betsy: On average, about two or three.

Therapist: Alright, do you do any other types of physical activity in addition to teaching classes?

Betsy: Oh yeah, I usually try to get my own workout in four times a week.

Therapist: When do those fit in? On days when you teach or on your days off from teaching?

Betsy: Typically, on days that I teach classes because I’m already at the gym and dressed to workout.

Therapist: Okay, so it sounds like part of the reason you exercise is for the health benefits and because it is your job. I totally get that. I’m wondering if there is any added benefit that you might lose weight or change your body shape by working out.

Betsy: Oh yeah, it is definitely an additional benefit. It also makes me feel less guilty about when I might overeat or drink too much alcohol over the weekend.

Therapist: Okay, now that I have a better idea of your exercise patterns and the reasons why you exercise, I’m going to ask some more specific questions. Would that be alright?

Betsy: Yeah, that’s fine.

Therapist: How many days per week, on average, do you exercise for two or more hours?

Betsy: Six days a week, typically.

Therapist: Okay, and have you ever exercised when you were sick or injured?

Betsy: I have to. I can’t miss work every time I’m sick or have an injury.

Therapist: Okay, when you are sick or injured, do you still push yourself very hard or do you modify what you are doing? For example, on a 1–10 scale, with 10 being the hardest, how hard do you push yourself when you’re sick or injured?

Betsy: I always push myself. It’s important to be an upbeat workout instructor. I typically push myself to get to an 8 or 9 every time I teach, even if I’m not feeling well or hurt.

Therapist: Could you sing or hold a conversation when you’re pushing yourself to an 8 or 9?

Betsy: No, but that’s the point. I’m showing my students by example how to push their limits.

Therapist: That makes a lot of sense. You’re leading by example.

Betsy: Exactly!

Therapist: Could you tell me about a time when you exercised when you were sick or injured?

Betsy: I had a twisted ankle a few times.

Therapist: Ouch, those can hurt! Did you ever go to a doctor for your twisted ankles?

Betsy: A few times. They just tell me to rest my ankle. They don’t understand that I have a job to do and can’t afford to take time off.

--

The therapist used several strategies to fully assess the patient’s exercise behaviors. The patient initially mentioned that her exercise was about health and that she exercised frequently for her job, but not to influence body shape or weight. Because exercise can be a healthful behavior for many people, patients with EDs will sometimes frame their exercise in a positive manner. The therapist, however, should continue to ask about the patient’s exercise pattern in a nonjudgmental manner to better understand how the ED and exercise might be related. The therapist in this case validated what the patient was saying and asked whether there was any added benefit that her shape and/or weight could be influenced by her exercise behaviors. The patient also framed her exercise as being necessary for work, so the therapist asked detailed questions about how much effort the patient put into exercise (e.g., more than other instructors) as well as the frequency of exercise (i.e., including additional workouts in addition to teaching exercise classes as part of her job). The therapist also asked about perceived exertion to evaluate intensity (e.g., how hard was Betsy pushing herself during the activity). Assessing perceived exertion is another way to help determine if the exercise was potentially unhealthy. It is also important to inquire about days when exercise persisted despite an injury or illness or when against medical advice. For example, in this vignette, Betsy exercised at almost her maximum intensity, even when she was sick or injured, which revealed she exercised in a rigid way despite her doctor’s orders to rest. Finally, Betsy volunteered that she exercised to compensate for an overeating episode. If she had not volunteered this information, it would have been important for the clinician to ask if exercise was used to compensate for an eating episode.

Impact of Gender, Race, Culture, Age, and Other Aspects of Diversity

Ethnicity and Culture

Recent studies suggested that ED prevalence among African American, Latinx, Asian, Pacific Islander, and/or Native American ethnic minority groups is comparable to, or higher than, ED rates among non-Latinx European Americans (Cheng et al., 2019). Although members of ethnic-racial minority groups are not protected from developing an ED, they are less likely to receive a referral for ED assessment or treatment than European Americans (Moreno et al., 2023). Even when referred for treatment, ethnic and racial minorities are less likely to utilize mental-health resources than their European-American counterparts. The lack of treatment referral and treatment receipt in ethnic-minority groups is theorized to be the result of increased stigmatization of mental health services (Misra et al., 2021). Another important consideration is the potential need to include the family in the assessment and treatment process. For example, excluding family members from clinical services could violate the Latinx cultural value of familismo, which refers to a strong identification and attachment to nuclear and extended families. Finally, therapists should be aware of cultural adaptations and tailor their intake interview to assess culturally relevant issues that may maintain ED behaviors. For example, Exum et al. (2022) theorized that for Black women, discrimination, strong black woman identification (cultural and societal expectations of strength), and culturally specific body-image ideals may lead to stress. In turn, ED behaviors may serve as a maladaptive coping mechanism for mitigating the high stress that Black women are exposed to in society.

Patients who identify as a member of an ethnic-racial minority group who appear for services may be more likely to present with a higher level of psychiatric impairment and disorder severity (Burke et al., 2021). High severity may be due to cultural stigma and discrimination, which may lead some individuals to live with ED symptoms longer and wait to seek treatment (or not receive appropriate referrals) until they are severely impaired.

Despite having greater symptom severity upon initial treatment presentation, certain minority groups may simultaneously report lower body-image concerns than their European American counterparts. Researchers suggested that potential differences in body image disturbance may be due to cultural preference for “curvier” body sizes among members of certain ethnic and racial groups (Lowy et al., 2021). East Asian individuals are likely to prioritize body image based on thinness, and culturally important features, such as eye shape and complexion (Brady et al., 2017). South Asian individuals may report body image disturbance due to pressures to achieve competing appearance ideals, weight stigma from multiple sources (e.g., mothers, relatives, and “aunties”), and pressures to possess light skin (Goel et al., 2021). Finally, for first-generation ethnic-racial minorities, clinicians should evaluate the role of acculturation in the onset and maintenance of ED symptoms.

Assessment Tip: During assessment and treatment, it is important to consider the cultural practices of the population. Although something may seem abnormal or maladaptive to you, it may be common practice in certain cultures. Racism, discrimination, and sexism are also risk factors for maladaptive behaviors, so maintaining cultural humility and working to the best of one’s ability to understand the client’s experience is critical.

Religion

Religious diets, practices, and rituals can impact ED assessment and treatment. It is crucial to disentangle religious modesty from body avoidance in various populations. For example, tzniut (the character trait of modesty in dressing and behavior) is an important tenet in the Orthodox Jewish religion. Similarly, it is important to parse apart motivations for underlying eating behaviors. For example, Ramadan and Navratri are culturally sanctioned periods of religious fasting. One the one hand, it is not appropriate to pathologize culturally sanctioned eating behaviors. On the other hand, it is important to recognize that weight- and shape-related motivations for dietary restraint can co-exist with religious motivations for fasting. Thus, therapists should not assume that religion is the primary or only reason motivating fasting. We recommend that therapists ask if “any small part” of the behavior is also motivated by the desire to lose weight or compensate for an eating episode. If a client indicates that a secondary motivation of religious fasting or body avoidance is to accommodate the ED, then this provides the therapist with an opportunity to align the client’s behaviors with their stated values.

Assessment Tip: When working with clients of varying religious backgrounds, it is important to practice cultural humility by doing personal work to learn more about the client’s religion, consulting with spiritual leaders, and identifying ways to honor religious practices, while also promoting ED recovery. Including religious leaders as partners during assessment and treatment can help ensure that conceptualization and treatment are culturally responsive.

Food Insecurity

EDs are frequently elevated among people with food insecurity (Becker et al., 2019). Food insecurity is defined by the US Department of Agriculture as a condition of limited or uncertain access to adequate food (National Research Council, 2006). Food insecurity is disproportionately experienced by people with lower incomes, people from minoritized racial and ethnic groups, people with disabilities, and people who identify as a sexual minority, which may be attributed to factors such as worsened socioeconomic conditions, structural racism, and discrimination. Although the mechanisms linking food insecurity to ED psychopathology are still being evaluated, one theory posits that individuals with food insecurity experience “feast-or-famine” cycles (Dinour et al., 2007), which may drive patterns of dietary restriction and binge eating. According to this model, during times of lower food availability, some household members may restrict food intake out of necessity or to make food available to other members of the household. When food is more abundant, such as when financial resources become available, people in the feast-or-famine cycle may be more likely to binge eat. Traditional assessments for EDs have focused on shape/weight-related motivations for dietary restraint, with no current measures specifically assessing food insecurity as a motivation for dietary restraint. It is, thus, important to ask clients if dietary restriction is due (at least in part) to limited food availability. Clinicians should seek to understand how dietary restraint that is motivated by desire to change weight or shape may interact with food insecurity.

Assessment Tip: When assessing among people with food insecurity, therapists should nonjudgmentally evaluate the contexts during which ED behaviors occur to develop case conceptualizations that capture all potential mechanisms.

Cisgender Women and Men

Women have a higher prevalence of EDs than men. It could be that ED behaviors are less likely to be identified in men, that men may not recognize that they have an ED and are less likely to seek treatment, or that the current diagnostic system does not accurately capture the experience of EDs in men. Compared to other EDs, men are most likely to be diagnosed with BED and to report overeating. Men are more likely to endorse a desire for a muscular figure, consistent with Western body ideals. To this end, men with EDs may strive to gain muscle mass or have a lean body (with low percent body fat) to enhance the appearance of muscularity.

Persons Who Are Transgender or Gender Diverse

Gender plays a critical role in how EDs are experienced. The experience of gender for many people is fluid and better defined as a continuous variable in which a person can identify anywhere along the continuum of “feminine” and “masculine.” We define the term transgender broadly to be inclusive of any person whose gender identity does not match the gender that would correspond with their sex assigned at birth. Transgender individuals are more susceptible to EDs than cisgender individuals. Within the transgender community, 17.7% of individuals have elevated ED symptomatology, and individuals assigned female at birth are especially at risk for developing an ED during their lifetime (Rasmussen et al., 2023). Elevated ED symptoms may also intersect with gender dysphoria in transgender persons. For example, levels of body dissatisfaction are high among transgender individuals who believe that their body is too feminine (i.e., perception of having too large of breasts, hips, or proportion of body fat as compared to muscle) or too masculine (i.e., perception of having too much muscle mass and an undesired V-shaped torso) (Cusack et al., 2022). To influence their body weight or shape in a way that is more personally desirable, transgender individuals may engage in disordered eating and unhealthy exercise that could result in the development of an ED (Mirabella et al., 2020). Gender-affirming treatments (e.g., hormone therapy, surgical interventions), therefore, may play a critical role in improving ED symptoms, but gender-affirming medical treatments are not sufficient stand-alone ED interventions (Rasmussen et al., 2023).

Assessment Tip: When working with clients, it is critical to ask about their pronouns and to use these pronouns in every subsequent interaction. Finally, careful assessment of body image may be helpful in working with persons who identify as nonbinary or transgender, particularly to determine to what degree gender dysphoria may influence the ED.

Sexual Orientation

Recent studies have found the prevalence of DSM-5-TR EDs among individuals who identify as a sexual minority is 1.9–3.6 times higher than in individuals who identify as heterosexual (Kamody et al., 2020). Sexual minority men and women are reported to experience greater overall ED symptoms, in addition to increased risk factors that can worsen symptoms, such as discrimination, prejudice, internalized homophobia, and sexual violence (Kamody et al., 2020). Overall, gay and bisexual men are more likely to report ED symptoms compared to heterosexual men. Bisexual women are more likely to report binge-eating, purging, and dieting.

To facilitate effective assessment and treatment, it is important for clinicians to assess social support and how these systems influence eating pathology for sexual minority individuals. Strong social support and engagement within the lesbian community are reported as protective factors for lesbian women, whereas greater involvement in the gay community was identified as a risk factor for eating concerns for gay and bisexual men (Morrison et al., 2020). Although social support can fluctuate during the “coming out” process, “coming out” can positively influence ED symptom severity.

Assessment Tip: When assessing for EDs in a sexual-minority population it is important to consider social and environmental stressors and their impact on the client’s life experience and ED diagnosis. Sexual minority individuals may strive for a body ideal that is different from what a heterosexual individual with body image concerns may idealize.

Age

ED onset most typically occurs in adolescent- and young-adult populations (Hudson et al., 2007). However, recent research has shown that maladaptive eating behaviors and cognitions occur at significant rates among children and older adults (Campbell & Peebles, 2014; Lapid et al., 2010). According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2018), about 42% of children and adolescents between grades nine through 12, reported feeling too fat or wanting to be thinner, and about 81% reported being afraid of becoming fat as early as 10-years-old (CDC, 2018). Additionally, about 5% reported taking diet pills that were not prescribed by a doctor, vomiting or taking laxatives (4.4%), and/or not eating for 24 h or more (13%; Hudson et al., 2007; Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2018).

Similarly, extreme dieting behaviors in older adults have been found to increase with age (Mangweth-Matzek et al., 2016). For men over the age of 45, the frequency of purging behaviors has increased, when compared to younger individuals (Mitchison et al., 2014). Although literature on older adults is limited, recent literature found increased body dissatisfaction among older adult women (Kilpela et al., 2015).

Assessment Tip: Clinicians should also assess body image in the context of normal shape and weight changes that occur during development (e.g., during adolescence, menopause, or through the normal process of aging).

Athletes

Among Norwegian elite athletes, the estimated prevalence of EDs is up to 32.8%, and rates of EDs are higher in athletes relative to non-athletes, which is consistent with findings from an early meta-analysis (Torstveit et al., 2008). In the United States, athletes were more likely to screen positive for an ED compared to non-athletes. Some research suggests that not all athletes are equally susceptible to developing an ED. For example, a meta-analytic review of ED psychopathology in female athletes vs. non-athletes found that athletes participating in weight class sports and sports emphasizing thinness/leanness (e.g., rowing, cross country, figure skating, etc.) had higher levels of disordered eating relative to other sport types (e.g., volleyball, basketball; Chapa et al., 2022). Among athletes, some symptoms of eating disorders may be more prevalent (e.g., excessive exercise) or less prevalent (e.g., body dissatisfaction) relative to non-athletes (Chapa et al., 2022). Athletes, especially athletes participating in sports that focus on thinness/leanness may be exposed to additional risk factors that non-athletes or athletes participating in other sports do not encounter; these additional risk factors include: (1) the belief that sport performance is optimal at a lower weight, (2) revealing uniforms, and (3) reinforcement from judges and/or coaches for certain body types. Due to the serious health risks associated with EDs (e.g., irregular heart rate, imbalanced electrolytes, bone deterioration), athletes who continue to participate in their sport with an ED are highly susceptible to physical injury, anemia, chronic fatigue, irregular menstruation (for women), and other serious medical problems due to relative energy deficiency syndrome (or REDs).

Identifying unhealthy exercise behaviors in athletes might be challenging because: (1) physical activity and sport participation are typically considered healthful behaviors and (2) athletes who exercise more than their coach recommends or when sick or injured may appear dedicated to their sport and/or team (and might even be rewarded). However, when patients engage in exercise past the point of their physical capacity, despite sickness or injury, against medical advice, or to compensate for an eating episode, it is unhealthful. Exercising more than recommended by coaches, against medical (or sport trainer) advice, or when sick or injured may be suggestive of an underlying ED rather than indicative of sport commitment.

Assessment Tip: When working with athletes, it is also recommended that you ask for collateral information from coaches, coaches’ assistants, and/or sports medicine staff.

Individuals in the Perinatal Period

Prevalence estimates of ED in pregnant women range from 4.3 to 7.5% (Easter et al., 2013), though there is mixed evidence regarding the course of symptoms throughout the perinatal period (pregnancy and postpartum). Some evidence suggests ED symptoms may improve or remit during pregnancy due to positive changes in eating behavior and body image (Makino et al., 2020). However, ED changes during pregnancy may not be sustained during postpartum period and may worsen after delivery. Other research demonstrated that both pregnancy and postpartum were associated with increased risk of ED symptoms (Makino et al., 2020).

Elevated rates of EDs during the perinatal period could be due to substantial changes in human chorionic gonadotropin, estrogen, progesterone, oxytocin, prolactin, body weight and shape, appetite, social roles, and sleep, all of which are contributors to an ED. Women who do not meet the increased metabolic/caloric needs of pregnancy and postpartum (including while breastfeeding) may experience intense hunger which may eventually lead to overeating and could trigger a binge-restrict pattern. Other ED behaviors could be motivated, in part, by societal pressure to return to pre-pregnancy weight/shape.

EDs that occur during pregnancy may affect both maternal and fetal health outcomes. Maternal EDs are associated with heightened rates of birth complications, cesarean delivery, low birth weight, and intrauterine growth restriction. Mothers with a current or past ED may have an elevated risk of developing postpartum depression and anxiety.

Assessment Tip: Given the significant risks related to perinatal EDs, it is imperative to assess for disordered eating during and after pregnancy. Assessment or screening could be implemented in routine health checks during pregnancy and throughout the infant’s first year. Healthcare providers, including obstetrician-gynecologists, pediatricians, midwives, and doulas, should actively assess eating habits, inappropriate compensatory behaviors, and body dissatisfaction to determine whether referral to ED specialist care is warranted.

Summary

EDs are complex psychiatric disorders that, if untreated, adversely affect social relationships, work productivity, and physical health and can lead to premature death. Despite common stereotypes, EDs have a prevalence that is similar to depression or anxiety. Moreover, EDs occur across the lifespan, in persons with food insecurity, in all genders, and in ethnic-racial minority groups. Having a rudimentary understanding of how to assess and diagnose EDs is, therefore, requisite for all mental health clinicians and physicians. This chapter provided information on the key signs and symptoms of EDs and how to assess these issues. We encourage the interested reader to refer to additional information and helpful resources provided by the National Eating Disorder Association (https://​www.​nationaleatingdi​sorders.​org/​) and Academy for Eating Disorders (https://​www.​aedweb.​org/​home) to learn more about the assessment, diagnosis, and treatment of EDs.
